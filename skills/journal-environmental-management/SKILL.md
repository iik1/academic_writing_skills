# Writing Style Skill: Journal of Environmental Management

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** SECTOR
- **Publisher:** Elsevier
- **Discipline / Scope:** Interdisciplinary environmental science and management. Covers pollution control and waste management (water/air/soil); natural resource and ecosystem management; climate change adaptation and mitigation; environmental policy, economics, and social science; and the development of technologies for environmental systems. Research must demonstrate a clear connection to practical environmental management applications. Submissions from any disciplinary background (biology, chemistry, ecology, engineering, economics, public policy, remote sensing, and others) are eligible if the environmental management relevance is central.
- **Typical article types:** Research Article (6,000–8,000 words, up to 8 figures/tables combined); Review Article (critical, up to 12,000 words, up to 12 figures/tables); Short Communication (2,000–3,000 words); Discussion Letter (up to 1,000 words, response to or comment on a prior JEM publication).
- **Guidelines source URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/journal-of-environmental-management/publish/guide-for-authors
- **Articles analyzed (full-text):** Insufficient direct full-text access for systematic coding — see note below.
- **Articles analyzed (extended-preview only):** Multiple abstracts, highlights, and partial text from ScienceDirect article pages across volumes 349–370 (2024) cross-referenced with author guidelines, reference manager style files, bibliometric analyses, and editorial policy statements. Representative topics reviewed: CO2 dynamics in tidal rivers (Vol. 349), phosphate/fluoride recovery (Vol. 367), green production technology adoption in rural China (Vol. 370).

## Author Guidelines (Hard Rules)

> These are the journal's stated requirements. They take precedence over observed patterns when there is a conflict.

### Manuscript Structure
Articles must be divided into **clearly defined and numbered sections**. Section numbering follows the decimal system: 1, 1.1, 1.1.1, 1.1.2, 1.2, 2, 2.1, etc. Subsections may have brief headings.

Standard research article sections (no fixed titles mandated, but decimal numbering required):
1. Introduction
2. Materials and Methods (or Study Area and Methods, or Data and Methods)
3. Results
4. Discussion (sometimes combined as Results and Discussion)
5. Conclusion
References

An **Abstract** appears before the body; **Highlights** and optionally a **Graphical Abstract** appear as separate submission items.

Authors are required to submit a **Novelty and Relevance Statement** explaining: (a) originality with respect to state-of-the-art; (b) relevance and contribution to environmental management knowledge; (c) applicability to a wider audience and wider contexts.

### Length Requirements
- **Research Article:** 6,000–8,000 words including tables, illustrations, and references. Up to 8 figures and tables combined.
- **Review Article:** Up to 12,000 words including references. Up to 12 figures and tables combined.
- **Short Communication:** Approximately 2,000–3,000 words.
- **Discussion Letter:** Up to 1,000 words.
- Submissions exceeding limits require a detailed cover letter explanation; the editor may reject on this basis alone.

### Abstract
- **Unstructured**, single paragraph. No subheadings within the abstract.
- **Maximum 250 words.**
- Must state: purpose of the research, principal results, and major conclusions.
- Must stand alone (no reliance on the body text).
- Avoid references in the abstract (cite author and year if absolutely unavoidable).
- Avoid non-standard abbreviations; define any abbreviations at first mention.

### Citations and References
- **Author–year system** (Elsevier Harvard style). In-text: `(Smith, 2020)` for single author; `(Smith and Jones, 2020)` for two authors; `(Smith et al., 2020)` for three or more. Multiple citations in parentheses separated by semicolons: `(Baker, 2015; Palmer and Milhollin, 2004)`.
- References cited in text must be present in the reference list; vice versa.
- Reference list ordered **alphabetically by first author surname**, then chronologically.
- Journal article format: `Surname, Initial(s)., Year. Article title. Journal Name, Volume (Issue), pages. https://doi.org/DOI`
- Book format: `Surname, Initial(s)., Year. Title, edition. Publisher, Place.`
- Book chapter format: `Surname, Initial(s)., Year. Chapter title, in: Editor(s) (Ed./Eds.), Book Title. Publisher, Place, pp. X–Y.`
- **Unpublished results and personal communications** are not permitted in the reference list.
- **Conference proceedings abstracts and grey literature** are not acceptable citations.
- For same author, same year: distinguish with a, b suffix: `(Smith et al., 2001a; Smith et al., 2001b)`.
- Responsibility for accuracy of bibliographic citations lies with the authors.

### Headings
- Sections numbered with Arabic numerals; subsections with decimal notation (1, 1.1, 1.1.1, 2, 2.1, etc.).
- Brief descriptive headings are encouraged for subsections.
- No more than three heading levels are typical in practice.
- Appendices labeled A, B, etc., with their own sub-numbering (Table A1, Fig. B1).

### Tables and Figures
- Up to 8 figures/tables combined for Research Articles; up to 12 for Review Articles.
- Figures and tables numbered consecutively (Fig. 1, Table 1); referenced in text before they appear.
- Tables: captions above; figure captions below.
- Maps: must allow locating the study area using common mapping platforms; must include only the area studied. Required note on all maps: "Map lines delineate study areas and do not necessarily depict accepted national boundaries."
- **Graphical Abstract** (encouraged, not mandatory): minimum 531 × 1328 pixels (h × w), readable at 5 × 13 cm at 96 dpi. Preferred formats: TIFF, EPS, PDF, or MS Office. Submitted as a separate file.
- **Article Highlights** (required): 3–5 bullet points, each maximum 85 characters including spaces. Submitted separately. Should capture novel results and any new methods.
- Source files must be editable (.doc/.docx for Word; .tex for LaTeX). PDFs are not acceptable source files.

### Language and Tone Requirements
- Articles must be written in **well-written, professional English**.
- American or British spelling is acceptable, but must be consistent throughout.
- Avoid abbreviations and formulae in titles unless they are established and widely understood.
- Keywords: maximum 6, immediately after the abstract, American spelling, avoid general or plural terms, avoid conjunctions ("and", "of"), only established abbreviations.

### Prohibitions
- **Desk rejection triggers:**
  - Highly localized case studies or laboratory-scale experiments using standard methods, unless broad international relevance is convincingly demonstrated.
  - Review articles that simply summarize existing literature without providing novel, practical insights.
  - Overly lengthy review articles in the style of a textbook chapter, thesis, or dissertation literature review.
  - Submissions without a clear connection to practical environmental management applications.
- Do not submit to multiple journals simultaneously.
- PDFs are not acceptable as source files.
- Unpublished results and personal communications may not appear in the reference list.
- Conference abstracts and grey literature may not be cited.
- Maps must not depict national boundaries beyond the study bounding box without the required disclaimer note.

## Observed Writing Patterns

### Voice and Person
Mixed practice. Third-person constructions are common: "this study investigates", "the results indicate", "it was found that". First-person plural ("we") appears in methods sections when describing researcher actions ("we collected", "we applied") and in interdisciplinary collaborative work. First-person singular ("I") is absent. The overall tendency leans toward third-person or passive constructions in the abstract and introduction ("This study aims to", "The objective of the present study was to") and past-tense passive in methods and results ("Data were collected", "Samples were analyzed"). [INFERRED — high confidence]

### Sentence and Paragraph Style
Sentences are medium length (18–28 words typical). Methods sections tend toward shorter, procedurally dense sentences. Discussion and introduction favour longer sentences with subordinate clauses. Paragraphs are moderate to dense: 4–8 sentences, each developing a single claim with evidence or elaboration. Methods sections use tight sequential prose ("First... Subsequently... Finally..."). No bullet points within the body text. [INFERRED]

### Hedging and Epistemic Stance
Moderate hedging throughout. Results are described with "indicate", "suggest", "demonstrate", or "reveal" rather than "prove" or "show definitively". Causal language is used only when study design supports it; cross-sectional and observational designs hedge with "is associated with", "is linked to". Generalisation is scoped to the study context. Common hedging markers:
- "may", "might", "could" — uncertain causal attribution
- "suggests", "indicates", "appears to" — interpretation
- "to our knowledge" — claiming novelty
- "future research should investigate" — forward directions
[INFERRED]

### Transitional and Cohesive Phrases
- Section transitions: "Building on these findings, ...", "Having established X, we now turn to..."
- Within-paragraph links: "Furthermore", "In addition", "However", "Notably", "In contrast", "Consequently", "As a result"
- Between results sub-sections: "Fig. 1 illustrates...", "As shown in Table 2...", "The results for [parameter] are presented in..."
[INFERRED]

### Formality and Register
Consistently high formal academic register. No colloquial phrasing, contractions, rhetorical questions, or advocacy language. Precise technical terminology with first-use abbreviation definitions (e.g., "total organic carbon (TOC)") is the norm. Discipline-specific jargon from ecology, chemistry, engineering, economics, and policy science coexists in the same paper, reflecting the journal's interdisciplinary readership. [GUIDELINE + INFERRED]

### Guideline vs. Practice Discrepancies
- **Abstract structure:** Guidelines say unstructured; in practice, many authors write quasi-structured abstracts with implicit Background/Methods/Results/Conclusions flow without subheadings.
- **Figure/table limit:** The stated limit of 8 (Research Article) is enforced, but additional figures may appear in supplementary files.
- **Results and Discussion:** Guidelines permit separate or combined. Separation is more common in ecological/natural science papers; combined "Results and Discussion" is more common in applied engineering, policy, and social science papers.

## Section-by-Section Writing Guide

### Abstract
- Open with the research problem or context (1–2 sentences).
- State the objective explicitly (1 sentence): "This study investigates...", "The aim of this work was to..."
- Describe the approach/method briefly (1–2 sentences).
- Report key findings numerically where possible (2–3 sentences).
- Close with the management or policy implication (1 sentence): "These findings have implications for..."
- Total: 200–250 words. No references. Define all abbreviations at first use.

### Introduction
Follows the CARS model:
1. **Establish territory** (2–4 paragraphs): Assert importance of the research area with dense citation. Opens with a broad environmental significance statement.
2. **Establish niche** (1–2 paragraphs): Identify the specific gap. Typical framings: "However, limited attention has been paid to...", "Despite the growing body of research on X, Y remains poorly understood.", "To date, no study has simultaneously examined X and Y in the context of Z."
3. **Occupy niche** (1–2 paragraphs): State study objectives explicitly. The management relevance must be flagged here. Typical structure: "To address this gap, the present study aims to: (1)... (2)... (3)...". Brief preview of paper structure: "The remainder of this paper is organized as follows..."

Citation density: high (3–8 citations per paragraph in territory establishment; 1–3 per sentence in gap statement).

### Methods
- Past tense passive predominates: "Samples were collected", "Data were obtained from", "Statistical analysis was performed using".
- Study area/case description typically precedes data collection procedures, often with a map.
- Data sources cited directly with author, year, and dataset name.
- Software, instruments, and analytical procedures named with version: "R version 4.2.0", "MODIS Terra satellite (Collection 6)".
- Procedural detail sufficient for replication: sample size, collection dates, analytical thresholds, model parameterisation.
- Statistical methods state the test, threshold, and software: "Differences were tested using one-way ANOVA (α = 0.05) in SPSS v26."

### Results
- Present tense for describing figures/tables: "Fig. 2 shows...", "Table 1 presents..."
- Past tense for findings: "The highest concentrations were recorded in...", "The model explained 78% of variance (R² = 0.78)."
- Statistics reported inline: mean ± SD, p-values, 95% CI. Full statistical tables supplement prose.
- Minimal interpretation in Results; explanations deferred to Discussion.
- Subsection structure mirrors the methods sequence.

### Discussion
- Opens by restating the key finding and connecting it to prior work.
- Each substantive result anchored to confirming or contrasting prior literature: "consistent with [Author, Year]", "in contrast to [Author, Year]".
- Mechanisms addressed: "The observed increase in X may be attributable to Y, given that..."
- **Management and policy implications discussed explicitly** — a JEM requirement.
- Limitations in a dedicated paragraph: "This study has several limitations. First,... Second,..."
- Future research follows limitations: "Future studies should examine..."
- Disagreement with prior work framed constructively with boundary conditions: "This discrepancy may reflect differences in [scale/climate zone/data resolution]."

### Conclusion
- Brief section (300–600 words for a Research Article).
- No new data or citations not already in Discussion.
- Restates key contributions concisely (1–2 paragraphs).
- States management/policy implications explicitly.
- Closes with a forward-looking scientific or management statement.

### References
- Elsevier Harvard author-year style; alphabetical by first author surname.
- Full journal names (not abbreviated).
- DOI included for all references: `https://doi.org/...`
- No personal communications, unpublished data, conference abstracts, or grey literature.

## Common Pitfalls
1. **Failing the "broad interest" test at desk review.** JEM will desk-reject papers based on a single case study or single country if the paper does not explicitly demonstrate why its findings are generalisable or transferable to other contexts. Make the broader relevance case in the introduction and conclusion.
2. **Omitting or genericising the Novelty and Relevance Statement.** JEM requires this statement at submission. A vague statement ("This study fills a gap") is insufficient — reviewers expect specificity about what is new relative to named prior studies.
3. **Review articles that summarise without synthesising.** JEM explicitly desk-rejects reviews that function as annotated bibliographies. A review must provide critical synthesis, an original framework, or a meta-analytic argument.
4. **Missing or malformed Highlights.** Highlights (3–5 bullet points, max 85 characters each) are required at submission. Bullets that exceed the character limit or describe the topic rather than the findings need revision before processing.
5. **Incorrect reference types.** JEM prohibits conference abstracts, grey literature, and personal communications in the reference list. Verify that all cited policy documents and monitoring data qualify as citable sources.

## Representative Phrase Bank

### Introduction phrases
- **Gap statement:**
  - "Despite considerable progress in understanding X, the mechanisms governing Y in [system/context] remain poorly characterized."
  - "However, limited empirical evidence exists on the relationship between X and Y at [spatial/temporal scale]."
  - "To date, few studies have simultaneously examined X and Y in the context of Z, particularly under [condition]."
  - "Existing models do not adequately account for [factor], which may play a critical role in determining [outcome]."
- **Objective statement:**
  - "The aim of this study was to investigate the effects of X on Y using [method] in [setting]."
  - "To address this knowledge gap, we developed [model/approach] to quantify [variable] across [domain]."
  - "This study aims to: (1) characterise...; (2) evaluate...; (3) assess the implications of... for environmental management."
  - "The present study contributes to this debate by providing the first [analysis/dataset/framework] for..."

### Methods phrases
- **Procedure description:**
  - "Water samples were collected at [n] sites between [dates] using [protocol]."
  - "Statistical analysis was performed using [software] version [X]; significance was set at α = 0.05."
  - "The model was calibrated against observed data from [period] and validated using an independent dataset from [period]."
- **Data source statement:**
  - "Land use data were obtained from [dataset] produced by [organisation] at a spatial resolution of [X] m."
  - "Monthly precipitation records were extracted from the [dataset name] database (Author, Year)."
  - "Field measurements were conducted during [season/year] following [standard/protocol]."

### Results phrases
- **Statistic inline:**
  - "The highest concentration of [X] was recorded at site S4 (mean ± SD: 12.3 ± 1.7 mg L⁻¹), significantly exceeding the threshold value (p < 0.001)."
  - "The random forest model explained 83% of the variance in [outcome] (R² = 0.83, RMSE = 0.24)."
  - "A significant positive association was found between [X] and [Y] (r = 0.67, p < 0.01, n = 128)."
- **Figure reference:**
  - "As shown in Fig. 3, concentrations exhibited a pronounced seasonal pattern, peaking in [month] and declining through [month]."
  - "The spatial distribution of [variable] is illustrated in Fig. 4, which reveals..."

### Discussion phrases
- **Connecting to literature:**
  - "These findings are consistent with [Author, Year], who reported [similar finding] in [analogous system]."
  - "In contrast to [Author, Year], our results indicate [difference], which may reflect the distinct [soil type/climate regime/land use] of the study area."
  - "The observed relationship between X and Y extends the framework proposed by [Author, Year] by demonstrating [new dimension]."
- **Limitation framing:**
  - "A notable limitation of this study is the reliance on [data source/time period/single site], which may restrict the generalisability of these findings to [other contexts]."
  - "The cross-sectional design precludes causal inference; future longitudinal studies are needed to establish directionality."

### Conclusion phrases
- **Summary opener:**
  - "In conclusion, this study demonstrates that [key finding], with important implications for [management domain]."
  - "The findings of this study provide robust evidence that [X], underscoring the need for [management action]."
- **Future direction:**
  - "Future research should examine [specific question] across a broader range of [climatic zones/land use types/temporal scales] to enhance the generalisability of these findings."
  - "The integration of [method/dataset] into future models would allow more precise estimates of [outcome] under [scenario]."
