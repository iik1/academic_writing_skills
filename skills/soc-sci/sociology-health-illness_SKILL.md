# Journal Writing Skill: Sociology of Health and Illness

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** SOC SCI
- **Publisher:** Wiley-Blackwell (on behalf of the Foundation for the Sociology of Health and Illness)
- **Scope:** International journal publishing sociological articles on all aspects of health, illness, medicine, and healthcare; submissions must demonstrate clear empirical or theoretical relevance to sociology.
- **Guidelines URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/page/journal/14679566/homepage/forauthors.html
- **Field baseline:** soc-sci-field-writing-style
- **Articles spot-checked:** 2 (10.1111/1467-9566.13709; 10.1111/1467-9566.70023)

## Hard Constraints (Author Guidelines)

> These override FIELD_SKILL where they conflict.

### Structure
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm. No mandated section names; qualitative and quantitative articles organise sections according to disciplinary conventions.

### Length
Original research articles: ≤9,000 words **including references**. Review articles: ≤8,000 words excluding references. [ENFORCED] The inclusion of references in the word count for original articles is tighter than many sociology outlets and should be planned for.

### Abstract
Unstructured (plain paragraph); ≤200 words. No subheadings required or observed. [ENFORCED — word limit; PRACTICE ONLY — unstructured format, confirmed across both spot-checked articles]

### Citations
Harvard author-date system, both in-text and in the reference list. In-text forms: "Jameson (2005)" or "(Light 1998)"; for three or more authors, "James et al." In-text comma usage varies slightly between articles (some use "Smith, 2020"; others "Smith 2020") — both appear accepted by the journal's editorial process. [ENFORCED]

### Headings
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm in qualitative and mixed-methods work (descriptive labels, no numbering). Quantitative articles from health-science traditions use numbered sections and subsections (e.g., 1, 1.1, 2, 2.1 …). Both conventions appear in published articles; neither is prohibited. [See Structural Deviations below]

### Tables and Figures
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm.

### Explicit Prohibitions
Submissions without clear empirical or theoretical relevance to sociology will not be considered. This is stated explicitly in the guidelines and applies regardless of health or medical focus.

## Observed Deltas from FIELD_SKILL

> Only dimensions where this journal deviates from the field baseline.

### Structural Deviations

**Numbered section hierarchy in quantitative submissions.** FIELD_SKILL describes qualitative and quantitative sociology articles using descriptive (unnumbered) section headings. In this journal, quantitative and epidemiological articles routinely use a numbered section structure: Introduction (with numbered subsections 1.1, 1.2 …), Methods (2, 2.1 …), Results (3), Discussion (4, 4.1 …), Conclusions (5), Data Availability Statement. This mirrors public-health and epidemiology formatting conventions. Mixed-methods and qualitative articles use descriptive all-caps headings without numbering (e.g., INTRODUCTION, DATA AND METHODS, FINDINGS).

The journal publishes both conventions without apparent preference. Authors should match the formatting convention of their methodological tradition rather than defaulting to sociology norms alone. [PRACTICE ONLY — observed in quantitative articles; not stated in guidelines]

### Abstract Content

**Quantitative abstracts include specific statistics.** FIELD_SKILL does not specify whether abstracts should contain quantitative detail. In this journal, quantitative articles routinely present odds ratios with 95% confidence intervals in the abstract itself (e.g., "OR = 2.91, 95%CI: 2.33–3.65"). This is closer to epidemiology practice than typical sociology convention and is well within the 200-word limit. Authors writing quantitative papers should follow this pattern rather than a more interpretive sociological abstract style. [PRACTICE ONLY — confirmed in quantitative articles]

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Exceeding the word count because references are included.** At 9,000 words including references, a well-cited article with 50–70 references can consume 800–1,100 words in the reference list alone. Plan the manuscript body around ~7,900–8,200 words.

2. **Non-sociological framing.** Articles that draw on health sciences, epidemiology, or medicine without anchoring the contribution in sociological theory or concepts are desk-rejected. The sociological relevance must be explicit in the introduction, not assumed.

3. **Using a structured abstract.** The journal does not use structured abstract subheadings (Background, Methods, Results, Conclusions). A plain discursive paragraph is expected, though quantitative papers should include key statistics within that paragraph.

4. **Applying purely sociological section conventions to quantitative submissions.** Authors from epidemiology or public health backgrounds may need to slightly adapt their section numbering style; authors from sociology writing quantitative papers should be aware that numbered subsections within Methods (e.g., 2.1 Study population, 2.2 Measures) are normal and expected at this journal.
