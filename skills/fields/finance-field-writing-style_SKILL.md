# Field Writing Style: FINANCE

## Scope and Intended Use

This baseline encodes shared writing conventions observed across ABS 4* journals in the FINANCE field. It covers structural norms, voice and register, argumentation patterns, methods presentation, results reporting, citation practices, and hedging conventions as they appear consistently across leading empirical and theoretical finance journals.

This baseline does NOT cover: journal-specific formatting rules, word or page limits, submission requirements, publisher-specific conventions, or statistical reporting thresholds that differ by journal. Journal-specific skills take precedence whenever they conflict with this baseline.

The norms described here are derived from ABS 4* journals. ABS 4 journals in the field largely follow the same conventions; no systematic deviations were identified that would warrant a separate baseline.

---

## Typical Article Structure

At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect a canonical article structure that remains stable across empirical, experimental, and structural papers. Deviations require justification.

**Near-universal sections (present in virtually all articles):**

1. **Abstract** — Concise summary (typically 150–250 words). States the research question, primary method or data, key findings, and main contribution. Structured as a single paragraph. JEL classification codes and keywords appear as separate fields on the published page, not in the abstract body.

2. **Introduction** — The most important section. Always present; always the first section; rarely given an explicit heading numbered "1" in isolation (it is simply "Introduction" or "1. Introduction"). Contains the research question, motivation, method preview, results preview, contribution statement, literature positioning, and a closing roadmap. See Argumentation section for full detail.

3. **Data / Data and Institutional Background** — Describes data sources, sample construction, variable definitions, and descriptive statistics. Often includes institutional context. May be merged with a Methods section or stand alone.

4. **Empirical Analysis / Results** — One or more numbered sections presenting main findings. Subsections organize results by hypothesis, specification, or sub-sample. Nearly always includes a robustness treatment (see below).

5. **Conclusion** — Short (typically 1–4 paragraphs). Restates key findings, states implications, and often suggests future research directions.

6. **References** — Always present; follows text.

7. **Tables and Figures** — Integrated in-text or placed after references. Tables carry extensive footnotes defining variables, samples, and standard-error clustering choices.

8. **Appendix** — Present in the large majority of papers. Contains additional robustness tests, alternative specifications, data construction details, and proofs. In many JFE and RFS papers, robustness tests reside primarily in the appendix rather than the main text.

**Common but not universal:**

- **Theory / Model section** — Present in papers with formal models or structural estimation; absent in reduced-form empirical papers.
- **Mechanism / Channel analysis section** — Increasingly common as a distinct section following main results; documents which economic channel drives the headline finding.
- **Background / Institutional Context section** — Present when the setting requires detailed orientation (e.g., novel markets, regulatory regimes, experimental protocols).
- **Robustness section (standalone)** — Sometimes present as a dedicated section in the main text (more common in papers published in the Journal of Finance); in others, robustness is embedded as subsections within the results section or deferred to the appendix.
- **Related Literature subsection** — Either embedded within the introduction as a labeled subsection ("Related literature") or integrated as narrative paragraphs; a standalone Literature Review section is essentially never used.

**Section numbering:** Arabic numerals throughout (1, 2, 3, 2.1, 2.2, etc.) is the standard. Roman numerals appear only rarely and in unusual paper formats.

**Closing roadmap:** The introduction almost invariably ends with a brief roadmap sentence such as "The rest of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 describes the data, Section 3 presents the empirical analysis, and Section 4 concludes."

## Voice, Person, and Register

At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect:

- **Person:** First-person plural ("we") for multi-authored papers. First-person singular ("I") for single-authored papers. Impersonal constructions ("this paper shows…", "the analysis reveals…") appear occasionally but are not dominant and should not replace first-person as the primary voice.

- **Active voice:** Active constructions dominate. Standard verbs: "we find," "we show," "we document," "we estimate," "we construct," "we examine," "we argue," "we propose." Passive voice is acceptable for describing procedures ("standard errors are clustered at…") but should not be the primary register.

- **Formality:** High throughout. No colloquialisms, no casual transitions, no rhetorical questions used as organizational devices (though research questions may be stated in question form). Tone is confident and precise.

- **Confidence calibration:** Leading journals expect authors to write with authority about their findings. Excessive tentativeness (over-hedging) reads as weak. Overconfidence on speculative claims reads as careless. The calibration norm is: strong declarative claims for results that are well-identified; measured language ("our results suggest," "consistent with") for mechanisms and interpretations.

## Sentence and Paragraph Style

- **Sentence length:** Medium to long. Finance writing favors complex sentences with embedded relative clauses, parenthetical clarifications, and multi-part statements. Short, punchy sentences appear occasionally for emphasis after a complex explanation, but are not the norm.

- **Paragraph density:** Dense. Paragraphs in the introduction and methods sections are typically 5–10 sentences. Results paragraphs are slightly shorter but still substantial.

- **Technical density:** High. Domain-specific vocabulary (identification, fixed effects, instrumentation, selection on observables, parallel trends, etc.) is used freely without apology. Papers do not define standard econometric terms.

- **Equation integration:** Equations are integrated into prose with proper grammatical introduction and follow-up explanation. Notation is defined on first appearance.

## Argumentation and Rhetorical Moves

**Introduction structure:** Leading finance journals expect a tightly structured introduction following a recognizable pattern:

1. **Opening move — Establish significance.** The first 1–3 paragraphs place the paper in a broad, high-stakes economic context. Quantitative scale is often invoked (market size, economic magnitude). Heavy citation from the outset signals command of the literature.

2. **Identify the gap or puzzle.** Prior work is acknowledged and its limitations are stated with precision. The framing is almost never adversarial; prior papers are credited and the gap is framed as an extension or completion rather than a refutation. Phrasing such as "yet it remains an open question whether…" or "prior work has focused on X, but less is known about Y" is common.

3. **State the research question explicitly.** Often one or two sentences explicitly name what the paper asks.

4. **Preview the empirical approach.** Often substantial — the identification strategy, data, or experimental design is described in the introduction itself, not deferred to a methods section. Reviewers in this field expect to understand the methodology from the introduction.

5. **Preview key findings with specific numbers.** It is a strong norm to report quantitative magnitudes in the introduction. Vague findings ("we find a significant positive effect") are insufficient for ABS 4*/4 introductions.

6. **State contributions, typically in a numbered list.** A paragraph or series of paragraphs explicitly lists the paper's innovations: "First, we introduce…. Second, we provide evidence…. Third, our results show…. Finally, we contribute to…." This explicit enumeration is expected.

7. **Review related literature.** Either integrated as narrative or set off as a labeled subsection ("Related literature"). Papers position themselves relative to multiple prior strands. The positioning is specific (naming key papers and explaining how the current paper differs or extends them), not generic.

8. **Close with roadmap.** A sentence or two describing the paper's section structure.

**Contributions framing:** The norm is to state contributions assertively. Phrases like "We are the first to show…," "We provide novel evidence that…," and "Our paper contributes to X by Y" are expected. Understating contributions is a costly error at this level.

**Closing moves (paper-level):** The conclusion mirrors the introduction. It opens by restating the main finding in 2–3 sentences, then states what the findings mean for the literature and, where relevant, for practitioners or policymakers. It often ends by acknowledging limitations and suggesting future research directions — framed as genuine scientific opportunity, not defensive hedging.

## Methods Conventions

**Tense:** Mixed. Present tense is standard for describing the approach ("we use the following specification…," "our instrument is…"). Past tense appears when reporting completed empirical work. Present tense is the default for active claims about the results.

**Level of procedural detail:** High. The field standard is sufficient detail for replication. Variable construction, sample selection criteria, time periods, data sources, and standard-error clustering choices are documented. The appendix often provides additional granular construction details.

**Identification strategy:** Given prominent and explicit treatment in all empirical papers. The identification assumption is stated clearly and its plausibility is argued for. For instrumental variables papers: the instrument is described, its relevance and validity are argued, and first-stage results are reported. For difference-in-differences papers: the parallel trends assumption is discussed, with event-study plots increasingly expected. For structural models: identification comes from functional form assumptions and moment conditions, which are discussed explicitly.

**Robustness:** A systematic treatment is expected at the ABS 4*/4 level. This includes alternative samples, alternative variable definitions, alternative standard-error clustering strategies, and placebo or falsification tests. Whether robustness occupies a dedicated section or is embedded in results and/or the appendix varies by journal and paper length, but the substance is non-negotiable.

**Data disclosure:** Replication packages (data and code) are now routinely required or expected. Papers include a "Data availability" statement.

**Ethics and pre-registration:** Experimental papers increasingly include IRB approval statements and pre-registration references. This is growing quickly in the field.

## Results and Reporting Norms

**Tables vs. prose:** Tables are the primary vehicle for results. Prose provides a guided tour of the tables rather than an exhaustive recitation of every coefficient. The norm is to narrate the most important coefficient(s) in the text, note statistical significance, discuss economic magnitude, and direct the reader to the table for full detail.

**Table design:** Multiple regression specifications are presented in adjacent columns of the same table, with fixed effects and controls systematically added or varied. Table notes document the sample, dependent variable, unit of observation, clustering of standard errors, and significance thresholds. Significance notation (* p<0.10, ** p<0.05, *** p<0.01 or similar) is standard in table footnotes.

**Statistical precision:** Finance writing is precise. Exact coefficients and standard errors (or t-statistics) are reported, either in the table or in prose. The statistical significance level must be stated explicitly.

**Economic significance:** Equally important as statistical significance. Authors are expected to translate coefficients into economically meaningful terms (e.g., as a percentage of the sample mean, or in dollar or basis-point terms). Failure to discuss economic magnitude is a common reviewer criticism.

**The reporting–interpretation boundary:** In the results section, reporting and interpretation are interleaved but distinguishable. Reporting statements are followed closely by interpretive statements. Extensive interpretation is concentrated in mechanism sections; the main results section focuses on establishing the patterns.

**Null results:** Typically reported clearly rather than buried. If an expected coefficient is insignificant, this is stated and interpreted (lack of power, genuine null, or alternative explanation).

## Discussion and Conclusion Norms

**Conclusions are short.** A 1–4 paragraph conclusion is the norm at ABS 4*/4 level.

**Standard conclusion moves (in order):**
1. Brief restatement of the paper's question and core finding
2. Statement of implications — for the academic literature and, where applicable, for practitioners or policymakers
3. Acknowledgment of limitations (brief, honest, non-defensive)
4. Future research directions (genuine and specific)

**Discussion vs. Conclusion:** Most papers merge discussion and conclusion into a single "Conclusion" section. A separate "Discussion" section is uncommon in mainstream empirical finance papers; it appears more frequently in experimental papers where mechanism exploration is extensive.

**Mechanisms:** When mechanism analysis is central to the paper's argument, a dedicated "Mechanisms" section in the main text (before the conclusion) is common. This section tests whether the proposed economic channel (rather than an alternative) is operative, using additional regressions or cross-sectional heterogeneity tests.

**Limitations:** Acknowledged briefly and constructively. Extensive limitation discussion is uncommon in the published version; reviewers expect core limitations to be addressed during revision.

## Citation and Literature Engagement

**Format:** Author-year parenthetical format throughout. Example: "(Fama and French, 1993)" or "Fama and French (1993) show that…." No footnote-style or numbered citation systems are used in this field.

**Direct quotation:** Essentially never used. All prior work is paraphrased and attributed by citation.

**Citation density by section:**
- **Introduction:** High. Multiple citations per paragraph in opening pages is typical. Literature positioning is dense and specific.
- **Methods:** Medium. Key methodological precedents and data sources are cited. Standard econometric methods are cited only when non-standard.
- **Results:** Low to medium. Results are compared to prior estimates where relevant.
- **Conclusion:** Low. Prior work is referenced briefly in the context of implications.

**How disagreement is framed:** Direct adversarial disagreement with prior work is rare and carefully managed. Authors frame departures as "extending," "complementing," or "providing new evidence that modifies earlier conclusions under a broader set of conditions." When overturning a prior result, the framing credits the prior contribution and attributes the different finding to a distinct setting, better data, or a refined identification strategy.

## Hedging and Epistemic Caution

**Overall hedging level:** Low to moderate. Finance writing at the ABS 4*/4 level favors confident declarative statements for well-identified empirical findings and measured language for interpretations and mechanisms.

**When hedging is not used:**
- Statements of empirical findings with well-established identification
- Descriptions of identification strategy and data
- Claims about statistical significance
- Statements of paper contributions

**When hedging is used:**
- Interpretation of mechanisms ("our results suggest," "consistent with the view that," "we interpret this as evidence of")
- Claims that go beyond the data ("while we cannot directly test X, the pattern is consistent with…")
- Statements about external validity ("our results may not extend to other settings")
- Discussion of future research ("we believe this avenue warrants further investigation")

**Common hedging vocabulary:** "suggest," "indicate," "is consistent with," "appears to," "we interpret," "our results are consistent with the hypothesis that," "to the extent that." These are calibrated tools: which phrase is chosen signals the author's confidence in the claim.

**Overconfident language:** Claims of mechanism that are not directly identified are scrutinized carefully. The norm is to distinguish clearly between "we document a pattern consistent with mechanism Z" and "we directly test and confirm mechanism Z."

## What This Baseline Does NOT Specify

The following are deliberately excluded because they belong to journal-specific skills:

- Word limits and page limits
- Reference list formatting details
- Specific section naming conventions preferred by individual journals
- Publisher formatting rules (font, margin, line spacing)
- Submission system requirements
- Journal-specific statistical reporting thresholds (some journals require exact p-values; others accept significance-level notation)
- Journal-specific policies on internet appendices vs. supplemental materials
- Turnaround expectations and desk-rejection criteria
- Figure format requirements (color vs. greyscale)
- Anything that varies systematically across journals within the field
