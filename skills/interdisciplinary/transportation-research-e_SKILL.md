# Journal Writing Skill: Transportation Research, Part E: Logistics and Transportation Review

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** Tourism, Transport and Hospitality (SECTOR)
- **Publisher:** Elsevier
- **Scope:** Logistics and supply chain management from a transportation perspective, with a dominant focus on mathematical modelling, operations research, and optimisation. Empirical and behavioural logistics studies also published but represent a minority of output.
- **Guidelines URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/transportation-research-part-e-logistics-and-transportation-review/publish/guide-for-authors
- **Field baseline:** tourism-transport-hospitality-field-writing-style
- **Articles spot-checked:** 2 (10.1016/j.tre.2024.103535 — railway container sharing yard optimisation; S1366554524001145 — technology-driven logistics editorial/special issue)

## Hard Constraints (Author Guidelines)

> These override FIELD_SKILL where they conflict.

### Structure
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm for OR/mathematical modelling papers (Introduction → Problem Formulation → Methodology → Computational Experiments → Sensitivity Analysis → Conclusion). No additional journal-mandated section sequence beyond standard Elsevier requirements.

Required manuscript components (in addition to main body):
- Title page
- Abstract
- Keywords (1–7)
- Highlights (required, not optional)
- Graphical abstract (required, not optional)
- Declaration of competing interests
- Funding disclosure (if applicable)
- Declaration of generative AI use (if applicable)

### Length
No explicit word or page limit stated in the guidelines. ENFORCED limits apply only to the abstract (see below).

### Abstract
Unstructured (no labelled subsections). Maximum 250 words. Must briefly state purpose, principal results, and major conclusions. Avoid references in the abstract; if essential, cite author(s) and year. Avoid non-standard abbreviations or define them at first mention. — **ENFORCED**

### Citations
Author-year format (Elsevier Harvard variant). In-text: (Author, Year), (Author and Author, Year), (Author et al., Year). Reference list: surname(s) and initials, year in parentheses, then title, journal, volume, pages. Consistent with field APA/author-year norm. — **FIELD DEFAULT**

### Headings
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm.

### Tables and Figures
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm. Graphical abstract must be supplied as a separate image file (required submission component, not decorative). — **ENFORCED**

### Explicit Prohibitions
- Inclusive language required throughout: avoid bias based on age, gender, race, ethnicity, culture, sexual orientation, or disability; use gender-neutral plural constructions as default. — **ENFORCED**
- AI tools may not be listed as authors or co-authors.
- AI use in manuscript preparation must be disclosed in the submission; AI cannot substitute for human critical thinking or authorship responsibilities. — **ENFORCED**
- Simultaneous submission elsewhere prohibited.
- Previously published work (other than preprints, conference abstracts, lectures, or theses) not accepted.

## Observed Deltas from FIELD_SKILL

> Only dimensions where this journal deviates from the field baseline.

### Structural Deviations

**OR/Optimisation Structure is the Default, Not a Variation**

FIELD_SKILL presents the OR paper structure (Introduction → Problem Formulation → Solution Algorithm → Numerical Experiments → Conclusion) as one of several methodological variations within the field. At TRE, this structure is the *dominant default*. The standard 8-section social-science sequence (Introduction → Literature Review → Method → Results → Discussion → Conclusion) is the minority case, not the baseline. Authors writing for TRE should default to the OR/optimisation structure unless their paper is explicitly empirical/survey-based.

Evidence: Both research articles checked followed the OR structure with no standalone literature review section. — **PRACTICE ONLY**

### Statistical Reporting

**Computational Metrics Replace Hypothesis Testing**

FIELD_SKILL notes that OR papers use computational performance metrics rather than hypothesis-testing statistics. At TRE, this applies to the *dominant article type*, not a variation. Expected reporting elements are: solution quality benchmarks, computation time comparisons against solver baselines, scenario/sensitivity analysis across parameter ranges, and scalability testing. p-values, confidence intervals, and effect sizes are absent from OR papers and are not expected. These are not relaxed norms — they are inapplicable by design.

ABS 3 note: For the minority of empirical/survey papers in this journal, hypothesis-testing standards may be somewhat less demanding than at ABS 4 journals in this field, but this cannot be confirmed from the two articles checked. — **PRACTICE ONLY (OR papers); ABS 3 relaxation possible for empirical papers, unconfirmed**

### Other Journal-Specific Patterns

**Highlights and Graphical Abstract Both Required**

FIELD_SKILL classifies highlights as "optional but common" and "standard in Elsevier journals." At TRE, highlights are a mandatory submission component. The graphical abstract — not mentioned in FIELD_SKILL — is also mandatory. Both must be submitted regardless of article type. — **ENFORCED**

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Using the social-science 8-section structure.** TRE's dominant format omits a standalone literature review; relevant prior work is integrated into the Introduction. Submitting a paper with a full Literature Review section signals unfamiliarity with the journal's methodological culture.

2. **Missing the graphical abstract.** Unlike many Elsevier journals where it is optional, TRE requires a graphical abstract as a submission component. Omitting it will likely cause a desk-level return.

3. **Abstract over 250 words.** The 250-word cap is explicitly stated in the guidelines and applies to all article types.

4. **Hypothesis-testing framing in OR papers.** Framing OR contributions in terms of statistical significance and effect sizes is incongruent with the journal's dominant reporting culture. Benchmark comparisons and computational experiments are the expected evidence base.
