# Field Writing Style: OPS&TECH

## Scope and Intended Use

This baseline captures the shared writing conventions of ABS 4* and ABS 4 journals in operations management, supply chain management, and production management. It covers macro structure, voice, argumentation, methods reporting, citation practice, and hedging norms that are consistent across the field's leading outlets. It does not cover journal-specific formatting, word or page limits, submission requirements, or publisher rules. Where a journal-specific skill conflicts with this baseline, the journal-specific skill takes precedence.

---

## Typical Article Structure

At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect a canonical section sequence that varies by paper type but follows a recognizable template.

**Quantitative empirical papers** (the dominant type) typically contain:
1. Introduction
2. Theory and Hypotheses (or Literature Review and Hypotheses Development)
3. Methodology / Data and Methods (with subsections: data and sample, measures/variables, model specification)
4. Results (with subsections: main results, endogeneity checks, robustness checks, post-hoc or supplementary analyses)
5. Discussion (sometimes merged with Conclusion)
6. Conclusion (sometimes merged with Discussion, or combined as "Summary, Discussion and Future Research")
7. References
8. Appendices / Online Supplementary Material (for robustness tables, additional analyses, variable details)

**Qualitative and mixed-methods papers** follow a similar arc but replace the hypothesis development and statistical results sections with Theoretical Background, Research Design / Methodology, Findings (often divided into thematic or case-based subsections), and an integrated Discussion and Conclusion.

**Conceptual and theory-development papers** replace empirical methods with a theory development section, analytical arguments, and illustrative examples or propositions; conclusions focus on implications and research agenda.

**Analytical modelling papers** (more prevalent in some outlets than others) typically contain: Introduction, Model Setup / Formulation, Analysis / Results (mathematical proofs or numerical analyses), Managerial Insights, and Conclusion.

Near-universal sections across all paper types: Introduction, some form of theoretical grounding (even in highly analytical papers), and a concluding section. A subsection or section addressing managerial implications is near-universal across the field; reviewers expect practical relevance to be made explicit. A dedicated treatment of limitations and future research is also near-universal, typically appearing toward the close of the discussion or conclusion.

Practice varies: Discussion and Conclusion may be separate sections, a single merged section, or a combined "Summary, Discussion and Future Research" section. No single format dominates across the field.

## Voice, Person, and Register

Leading journals in this field expect first-person plural ("we") as the standard authorial voice for describing research design, analytical choices, and results — including single-authored work. Impersonal constructions ("this study examines," "the analysis reveals") appear alongside "we" constructions but are not the dominant mode.

Voice is mixed rather than uniformly active or passive. Active voice is common when the authors describe their own choices and arguments ("we model," "we find," "we argue"). Passive constructions appear frequently when describing data collection, variable construction, and procedures ("data were collected," "firms were identified as," "the sample was restricted to"). In highly analytical papers, impersonal present-tense constructions are common ("the model predicts," "the equilibrium exists").

The register is uniformly formal and precise. Colloquial language, rhetorical questions directed at the reader, and informal hedges are avoided. Clarity is expected alongside rigor; unnecessarily complex sentence constructions are not rewarded.

---

## Sentence and Paragraph Style

Sentences tend toward medium to long length, particularly in theory development and discussion sections where complex causal mechanisms require precise articulation. Short sentences appear selectively for emphasis. Paragraphs are medium to dense; theory and literature review paragraphs are citation-heavy and argument-driven, while results paragraphs are tighter and anchored to specific statistical or analytical outputs.

Technical vocabulary from relevant theoretical traditions (behavioral theory of the firm, transaction cost economics, resource dependence theory, dynamic capabilities, prospect theory, institutional theory, and operations research formalisms) is used precisely and without over-explanation for an expert audience. The field rewards methodological and conceptual rigor over general accessibility.

Practice varies: analytical and modelling papers use mathematical notation extensively and expect readers to follow formal proofs; management-science-oriented empirical papers blend formal notation with narrative; qualitative papers are prose-dense with no formal notation. The appropriate register and technical density depend substantially on the paper type.

## Argumentation and Rhetorical Moves

**Introduction structure:** Leading journals in this field expect an introduction that follows a CARS-like (Create a Research Space) logic:

1. Establish the phenomenon's importance — anchor the research in a practically and theoretically significant problem, often illustrated with a concrete example or motivating statistic.
2. Establish the niche — identify a tension, gap, or insufficiency in the existing literature. This is typically done by reviewing multiple streams of prior work and demonstrating that they are incomplete, provide conflicting evidence, or fail to address a specific boundary condition or context.
3. State the research question(s) — at the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect explicit and crisply stated research questions, often posed directly and numbered.
4. Preview the approach — describe the method, data source, theoretical framework, or analytical strategy employed.
5. State the contributions — contributions are listed explicitly, often as numbered statements or in "First... Second... Third..." form. Contributions are typically framed across multiple dimensions: theoretical, methodological, and managerial.
6. Preview the findings — a summary of main results in the introduction is expected and conventional.

The introduction carries high citation density. The gap is framed as an opportunity rather than a critique; prior work is acknowledged for its contributions before its limitations are identified.

**Contribution positioning:** Contributions are stated explicitly and prominently near the end of the introduction. Reviewers expect contributions to be specific: to which theory or literature they contribute, how they extend or challenge prior understanding, and why the findings matter for managers or policymakers. Generic statements of relevance are insufficient at the ABS 4*/4 level.

**Literature positioning:** Prior work is organised into recognisable theoretical streams. Conflicting prior evidence is framed as a puzzle or productive tension that the paper resolves. Authors position their work as extending, integrating, contextualising, or reconciling prior streams — not as refuting them.

**Closing rhetorical moves:** Contributions are revisited in the discussion or conclusion and tied directly to the empirical or analytical results. Practical and managerial implications are developed explicitly. Limitations are acknowledged openly but framed constructively, often followed immediately by future research directions they motivate.

## Methods Conventions

**Tense:** Data collection, sample selection, and analytical execution are reported in past tense. Conceptual and definitional statements use present tense ("we define supply base stability as..."). Model equations and formal specifications often use present or timeless present.

**Procedural detail:** At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect comprehensive procedural transparency. In quantitative empirical work, this includes explicit sample construction criteria, variable operationalisation with mathematical formulas where relevant, model specification with equation notation, and justification for key analytical choices with citation to methodological precedents. Qualitative work details sampling logic (theoretical or purposive), data collection process, analytical approach (e.g., grounded theory, thematic analysis, qualitative comparative analysis), and reliability and validity measures (inter-rater reliability, triangulation).

**Identification and robustness:** Causal claims require explicit identification strategy in quantitative work. Instrumental variables, difference-in-differences designs, natural experiments, propensity score matching, and other quasi-experimental approaches are deployed when causal inference is claimed. A robustness section — as a dedicated subsection of Results — is near-universal in quantitative empirical papers. Standard robustness tests include alternative operationalisations of key variables, subgroup analyses, and tests addressing alternative explanations. Endogeneity is addressed routinely and explicitly, not only when flagged by reviewers.

**Qualitative and mixed methods:** The field accepts case study, interview-based, and mixed-method designs, particularly in outlets focused on supply chain and operations management. Methodological rigour is demonstrated through transparency about site/informant selection, data saturation logic, analytical steps, and evidence of systematic coding. The procedural detail expected mirrors that of quantitative work: choices must be explicitly justified.

## Results and Reporting Norms

In quantitative empirical papers, tables are the primary vehicle for results; regression tables, structural equation model fit statistics, or configuration tables occupy a central place and are discussed rather than merely reproduced in prose. Figures are used to display interaction effects, theoretical frameworks, and summary matrices. Prose narrates the pattern across tables and flags the most theoretically important results without simply restating every coefficient.

Brief inline interpretation appears in results sections ("consistent with H1, the coefficient is positive and significant at the one-percent level") but fuller theoretical interpretation is reserved for the discussion. The boundary between results and interpretation is maintained, though not absolutely; the convention is to anchor claims to specific tables or figures before elaborating their meaning.

Statistical significance is typically indicated with asterisks at conventional thresholds, with standard errors or standard deviations in parentheses. Effect sizes and substantive significance are discussed in addition to statistical significance; reviewers expect authors to interpret the magnitude of effects, not only their direction and significance.

Null results and unexpected findings are reported and discussed transparently. Unexplained patterns are noted and, if possible, investigated through post-hoc analyses.

Qualitative and analytical papers integrate results and interpretation more naturally; in these papers there is no hard boundary between findings and their meaning.

## Discussion and Conclusion Norms

At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect the discussion and conclusion to accomplish the following in sequence:

1. **Restate the core findings** briefly, without merely repeating the results section.
2. **Theoretical interpretation** — explain why the findings came out as they did and connect results to the mechanisms proposed in the theory or hypotheses section.
3. **Reconciliation with prior literature** — connect findings explicitly to the streams reviewed in the introduction; address any conflicting prior evidence.
4. **Theoretical contributions** — revisit and elaborate the contribution claims from the introduction, showing specifically how the results substantiate each claim.
5. **Managerial implications** — a dedicated subsection is expected in most outlets; practical implications are distinct from the theoretical argument and explicitly addressed.
6. **Limitations and future research** — limitations are acknowledged openly and framed constructively; each limitation typically prompts a future research direction.
7. **Brief closing** — a short concluding statement that restates the paper's core contribution.

Practice varies: whether Discussion and Conclusion appear as separate sections, a merged section, or a combined "Summary, Discussion and Future Research" section is not standardised across the field. No consensus norm exists on this dimension; authors should follow outlet-specific conventions.

## Citation and Literature Engagement

**Density by section:** Citation density is high in the Introduction and Theory / Literature Review sections — multiple citations per substantive claim, combining foundational papers with recent contributions. Density is moderate in Methods sections, where citations primarily justify data sources, established measures, and methodological choices. Density is low in Results sections, where citations appear mainly to anchor comparisons with prior empirical benchmarks. Density rises again in the Discussion when findings are reconciled with prior literature.

**Paraphrase vs. quotation:** Direct quotation is rare and reserved for definitional statements by seminal authors or precise terminological distinctions. Paraphrase is the overwhelmingly dominant mode. Authors are expected to engage substantively with prior ideas, not reproduce their phrasing.

**How disagreement is framed:** Disagreement with prior work is framed as productive tension rather than error ("evidence remains mixed," "prior frameworks do not fully account for," "this prediction has not been tested in the context of"). The paper's contribution is positioned as resolving, extending, or contextualising the tension — not as correcting a mistake.

**Citation format:** In-text citations follow author-date (APA-adjacent) format throughout.

## Hedging and Epistemic Caution

Overall hedging intensity in this field is moderate to high, with marked variation by paper section and type.

**Where hedging is expected:**
- Theoretical mechanisms and hypotheses: modal constructions ("we propose that firms may...," "we expect that...," "when X, Y is likely to...") are conventional in hypothesis development
- Interpretation of empirical results: hedged reporting is standard ("the results suggest," "our findings are consistent with," "the evidence points to")
- Scope conditions and generalisability: explicit qualification of results to the study's context, time period, or industry is expected
- Unexplained mechanisms: where a proposed mechanism is not directly observed, hedged causal language is required ("one potential explanation is...," "this may reflect...")

**Where hedging is not expected:**
- Contribution claims: stated directly and confidently ("this paper contributes to X by...," "to our knowledge, this is the first study to...," "we demonstrate that...")
- Formal hypotheses: stated as direct directional predictions, not hedged possibilities
- Core reported findings: when results are statistically or analytically established, they are reported directly ("consistent with H2, the interaction effect is positive and significant")

Overuse of hedging weakens a paper at the ABS 4*/4 level; excessive qualification signals conceptual imprecision or insufficient theoretical grounding for the claims made.

## What This Baseline Does NOT Specify

The following elements belong in journal-specific skills and are explicitly excluded from this baseline:

- Word or page limits for manuscripts or abstracts
- Required or prohibited section names (e.g., whether "Theory and Hypotheses" or "Literature Review" is preferred)
- Publisher formatting requirements (font, margin, line spacing, reference list style)
- Submission portal requirements and file format conventions
- Whether p-value asterisks are permitted or exact p-values are required
- Whether a structured abstract is required and its permitted length
- Whether a separate Managerial Abstract or Executive Summary is required
- Special issue requirements or editorial scope emphasis
- Acceptance rates, review timelines, or desk rejection criteria
- Requirements for ethics statements, data availability statements, or conflict of interest disclosures
- Specific style guide edition (e.g., APA 6th vs. 7th)
- Whether figures must be submitted separately or embedded in the manuscript
- Journal-specific statistical reporting rules (e.g., reporting standards for SEM, fsQCA, or experimental manipulation checks)
