# Field Writing Style: ECON

## Scope and Intended Use

This baseline encodes the shared writing conventions of ABS 4* and ABS 4 journals in economics, derived from direct analysis of full-text working papers targeting top-tier economics outlets and confirmed against article abstracts, tables of contents, and titles from the published record of ABS 4* journals. It covers macro structure, voice, argumentation, methods, results, citation norms, and hedging as practised at the highest level of the discipline.

This baseline does NOT cover: journal-specific formatting requirements, word or page limits, submission portal rules, publisher typesetting conventions, mandatory data availability statements, or JEL classification requirements. Those belong in journal-specific skills. When a journal-specific skill conflicts with anything stated here, the journal-specific skill takes precedence.

---

## Typical Article Structure

At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers and editors expect a clear, disciplined section architecture. The canonical ordering differs by paper type.

**Empirical papers (near-universal structure):**
1. Introduction
2. Background / Institutional Setting (where the context requires it; optional)
3. Data
4. Empirical Strategy / Identification
5. Results
6. Robustness and Extensions (may be merged into Results as subsections)
7. Conclusion

**Theory and structural papers (near-universal structure):**
1. Introduction
2. Related Literature / Literature (standalone section more common in theory papers)
3. Model Setup / Environment
4. Analysis / Equilibrium / Characterisation
5. Results / Welfare Analysis / Applications
6. Extensions (optional)
7. Conclusion

**Near-universal across all types:** Introduction, at least one substantive body section, and Conclusion. A standalone appendix or online appendix for proofs, robustness checks, and data description is standard; its existence is expected rather than optional.

**Optional/variable:** Standalone Related Literature section (often omitted in shorter empirical papers where literature is surveyed within the Introduction); Background/Institutional Setting section; dedicated Mechanisms or Discussion section between Results and Conclusion.

Section headings use Roman or Arabic numerals; either is acceptable. There is no standard naming convention for the conclusion.

## Voice, Person, and Register

At the ABS 4*/4 level, first-person plural is the near-universal default. Leading journals in this field expect "we" as the subject of most analytical and descriptive sentences regardless of the number of authors; even sole-authored papers are frequently written in first-person plural. Constructions such as "we show," "we find," "we develop," "we estimate," and "we argue" dominate.

Active voice is strongly preferred. Passive constructions appear in narrow contexts—chiefly when describing data generation processes not under the authors' control.

Formality is high but not archaic. The register is precise, technical, and economical. Contractions are absent; colloquial expressions are absent. Leading journals do not reward needless complexity: a sentence that can be made shorter without sacrificing precision should be made shorter.

For theory papers, formality intensifies further. Theorem and proof environments shift the register toward mathematical logic, but prose between formal results remains in complete, well-formed sentences using "we."

There is no field norm requiring impersonal hedging to substitute for authorial commitment. A clear claim is preferable to a diffuse one.

## Sentence and Paragraph Style

Sentence length is medium to long. Technical content—embedded conditionals, parenthetical qualifications of model assumptions, lists of variables—drives sentences toward the longer end. Introductions and conclusions permit somewhat shorter, more direct sentences. Methods and theory sections frequently contain complex sentences whose length is justified by logical precision.

Paragraph density is high. Paragraphs in the body rarely contain fewer than four sentences and often run six to ten. Each paragraph develops a single idea completely. Very short paragraphs (one or two sentences) are reserved for transitions or for emphasising a result.

Technical vocabulary density is high throughout. Discipline-specific terminology is used without definition; readers are assumed to be trained economists. Acronyms for well-known estimators, datasets, and concepts are used freely once introduced.

## Argumentation and Rhetorical Moves

**Introduction structure** follows a recognisable pattern that differs from the generic CARS model in one key respect: results are announced in the introduction, not withheld. The typical sequence is:

1. **Motivating hook** — Establishes why the question matters via a stylised fact, policy puzzle, real-world event, or theoretical paradox. Kept short; reviewers expect the paper to reach its question quickly.
2. **Research question** — Stated explicitly, usually within the first two paragraphs.
3. **Approach and data** — Concise description of the methodology, identification strategy, dataset, or theoretical framework. Sufficient to characterise the paper but not to teach the method.
4. **Preview of main results** — Announced directly and quantitatively where possible. "We find that X increases Y by Z percent" is the norm.
5. **Contributions to the literature** — Stated explicitly, either in the introduction or in a standalone Related Literature section. "We contribute to the literature on X by Y" is standard. Contributions are typically enumerated; multiple contributions are normal.
6. **Roadmap** — A final paragraph describing the paper's structure section by section. Near-universal: "The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 describes... Section 3 develops..."

**Positioning relative to prior work:** Treated as a required element. Authors identify the papers most similar to theirs and explain precisely what the present paper does that those papers do not. Positioning is concrete and specific.

**Gap framing:** Gaps are identified as theoretical insufficiencies, missing empirical evidence, data limitations in prior work, or unresolved debates—not merely "lack of research."

**Closing moves:** The conclusion revisits main results, draws policy or theoretical implications, acknowledges limitations, and suggests future directions. No new results appear in the conclusion. It is shorter than the introduction.

## Methods Conventions

**Tense:** Mixed, following a principled logic. Data and estimation procedures use past tense ("we constructed the panel by merging...", "we estimated equation (1) using..."). Model properties, theoretical results, and identification logic use present tense ("the model implies that...", "the instrument satisfies the exclusion restriction if..."). This mixed-tense convention is consistent and expected.

**Procedural detail:** Sufficient for a trained economist to replicate in principle, but not a step-by-step recipe. Standard procedures are named and cited rather than fully described; non-standard or novel elements receive careful treatment. For structural estimation, parameter identification and the estimation algorithm are described. For reduced-form work, the regression specification, sample restrictions, and standard error treatment are stated precisely.

**Identification strategy:** For empirical papers, a required element. The identification strategy is stated clearly—typically as a subsection heading or prominent subsection—and the assumptions required for a causal interpretation are stated and defended.

**Robustness:** Leading journals in this field expect robustness checks as a standard component. Common formats include alternative sample definitions, alternative functional forms, placebo tests, and instrumental variable checks.

**Data description:** Source, coverage, sample restrictions, and key variable definitions are described. Summary statistics tables are near-universal.

## Results and Reporting Norms

**Boundary between reporting and interpretation:** Results and interpretation are typically integrated. A paragraph presenting a coefficient is normally followed immediately by interpretation of magnitude, sign, and economic significance. Separate Discussion sections exist but are less common than in some social science fields.

**Role of tables vs. prose:** Tables are the primary vehicle for quantitative results. Prose highlights key coefficients, signs, and magnitudes that bear on the paper's main claims; it does not reproduce every number in every table.

**Statistical narration precision:** Claims are reported with quantitative specificity. "We find that the policy increased employment by approximately 4 percentage points" is the norm. Vague directional statements are insufficient without accompanying magnitudes. Economic significance is expected alongside statistical significance.

**Null results:** Handled directly and reported as results. Well-powered null results are publishable when the question is important and identification is strong.

**Figures and tables:** Both are common. Figures illustrate key outcome variation, event-study designs, distributional results, and model fit. Table notes explain variable definitions, standard error clustering, and sample restrictions.

## Discussion and Conclusion Norms

Leading journals in this field expect the conclusion to be a genuine synthesis, not a verbatim summary of the abstract. The conclusion typically:

- Restates main findings in plain language, more interpretively than the abstract
- Draws out policy or theoretical implications where non-obvious
- Acknowledges limitations honestly but concisely
- Suggests directions for future research (brief)

**Merged vs. separate Discussion and Conclusion:** Practice varies: no consensus norm exists. Mechanisms discussion is more commonly folded into the Results section than separated into its own section.

**Length:** Conclusions are short relative to the paper—typically one to three pages. The introduction is generally longer than the conclusion.

## Citation and Literature Engagement

**Citation density by section:**
- Introduction: high — the literature is engaged intensively as gap and contributions are established
- Methods: medium — standard methods and datasets cited once, briefly; novel methods receive fuller citation
- Results: low to medium — citations appear when comparing magnitudes with prior estimates or discussing mechanisms
- Conclusion: low

**Citation format:** Author-year (parenthetical or in-text) is near-universal. Numbered footnote references are not the norm. Footnotes carry supplementary material and qualifications, not primary citations.

**Paraphrase vs. direct quotation:** Direct quotation is extremely rare. The field norm is to paraphrase prior claims and attribute them by citation.

**Handling disagreement:** Disagreement is handled with precision and without hostility. The standard framing acknowledges prior work and locates the source of the difference: "While [prior authors] find X using [approach], we find Y; the difference likely reflects [specific reason]." Authors are expected to explain why results differ, not merely note that they do.

---

## Hedging and Epistemic Caution

Overall hedging intensity is low relative to most social science fields. Clear claims are strongly preferred. The field norm is to defend a claim through identification, robustness, and transparency about assumptions—not to hedge the claim itself into vagueness.

**Contexts where hedging is expected:**
- External validity: results derived from a specific population or period are noted as such. "Our results pertain to..." or "it remains to be seen whether this extends to..." is standard.
- Causal interpretation beyond the identified parameter: local average treatment effects, partial equilibrium estimates, and complier-specific effects are labelled as such.
- Data limitations: attrition, measurement error, and potential confounders that cannot be fully addressed are acknowledged with "we cannot rule out that..." followed by the specific threat.
- Mechanism vs. reduced-form: results consistent with but not proving a mechanism use "consistent with," "suggestive of," or "in line with the prediction of [model]."

**Contexts where hedging is NOT expected:**
- Deriving theoretical results: "The model implies that..." is a derived result, not a conjecture.
- Reporting a regression coefficient: the confidence interval carries the uncertainty; additional verbal hedging is redundant.
- Stating contributions in the introduction: "We show," not "We attempt to show."

## What This Baseline Does NOT Specify

The following are explicitly outside the scope of this baseline:

- Word limits, page limits, or manuscript length recommendations for any specific journal
- Section naming conventions mandated by any specific journal's style guide
- Publisher formatting requirements (fonts, margins, line spacing, figure resolution)
- Submission portal requirements or cover letter conventions
- Journal-specific data availability, replication package, or pre-registration requirements
- Statistical reporting requirements mandated by specific journals
- Supplemental appendix length or format conventions mandated by specific journals
- Double-blind vs. single-blind review conventions
- Any norm applying to only one or two journals rather than representing field-wide consensus
