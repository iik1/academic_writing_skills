# Journal Writing Skill: Corporate Governance: An International Review

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** FINANCE (ABS classification; the journal's actual scope is interdisciplinary corporate governance spanning management, organizational behavior, finance, and law)
- **Publisher:** Wiley-Blackwell
- **Scope:** International and cross-disciplinary research on corporate governance mechanisms, board structure, ownership, executive compensation, and governance reforms. Draws heavily on management, organizational behavior, and behavioral sciences alongside finance.
- **Guidelines URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/page/journal/14678683/homepage/forauthors.html (requires institutional access; could not be directly retrieved)
- **Field baseline:** finance-field-writing-style
- **Articles spot-checked:** 2 full text + 3 abstracts (Boutchkova et al. 2021, 10.1111/corg.12339; Yin et al. 2021, 10.1111/corg.12353; Bose et al. 2021, 10.1111/corg.12349; abstract-level confirms for Hupka et al. 2026, 10.1111/corg.70030; Temiz et al. 2026, 10.1111/corg.70032)

## Hard Constraints (Author Guidelines)

> The Wiley author guidelines page was inaccessible during preparation of this skill (403 errors throughout). Constraints below are derived from consistent article practice and are labeled accordingly.

### Structure
CGIR articles follow a management-journal structure that departs from FIELD_SKILL in two ways: (1) the literature review is not embedded in the Introduction but placed in a dedicated Section 2; (2) that section combines literature review with formal hypothesis development. Typical section sequence observed:

1. Introduction
2. Literature and Hypotheses (with subsections per hypothesis)
3. Sample and Methodology (or Data and Methods)
4. Empirical Findings (with subsections per hypothesis test)
5. Discussion / Conclusion (sometimes merged into section 4 close)
6. References
7. Author Biographies

**PRACTICE ONLY** — confirmed across spot-checked articles but not verified against guidelines text.

### Length
Unknown; guidelines inaccessible. Articles observed: approximately 7,000–10,000 words (excluding references, tables, figures). No confirmed word limit.

### Abstract
**ENFORCED (inferred) — the most important delta from FIELD_SKILL.**

CGIR uses a structured abstract with four mandatory labeled paragraphs (not a single unstructured paragraph as in FIELD_SKILL):

| Label | Content |
|---|---|
| **Research Question/Issue** | The specific question or issue the paper addresses |
| **Research Findings/Insights** | Data source, sample, and key empirical results |
| **Theoretical/Academic Implications** | What the findings contribute to theory and academic knowledge |
| **Practitioner/Policy Implications** | Relevance for practitioners, regulators, or policymakers |

This format was confirmed identically across all five articles spot-checked (2021 and 2026 vintages), making it near-certain to be an enforced guideline. There are no JEL classification codes. Keywords appear as a separate labeled line below the abstract.

An optional **Video Abstract** hyperlink may also appear below the abstract.

### Citations
Author-year format consistent with FIELD_SKILL. The journal uses APA-style "&" (ampersand) in parenthetical citations — "(Adams, de Haan, Terjesen, & van Ees, 2015)" — and "and" in running text: "Adams and Ferreira (2009) found…". This is the standard Wiley/APA convention and does not deviate materially from FIELD_SKILL, but note the "&" specifically.

### Headings
Follows FIELD_SKILL Arabic-numeral convention. Wiley's HTML rendering applies a pipe character ("1 | INTRODUCTION") but this is a display artifact, not a manuscript formatting requirement.

### Tables and Figures
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm. Table notes document sample, dependent variable, SE clustering, and significance levels (*, **, ***). No observed deviation.

### Explicit Prohibitions
None confirmed (guidelines inaccessible).

## Observed Deltas from FIELD_SKILL

### Abstract Format
**Field norm:** Single unstructured paragraph (150–250 words); no labeled sections within the abstract.

**This journal:** Four mandatory labeled paragraph sections (Research Question/Issue / Research Findings/Insights / Theoretical/Academic Implications / Practitioner/Policy Implications). Each label appears as a bold prefix before the paragraph text. The practitioner implications field makes explicit policy and managerial relevance a required abstract component, not an optional addition.

**Classification: PRACTICE ONLY** (highly consistent across all articles; almost certainly enforced by guidelines, but guidelines text could not be verified). Treat as enforced in practice.

### Structural Deviation: Literature Review Placement and Hypothesis Development
**Field norm (FIELD_SKILL):** Literature positioning is either embedded in the Introduction as narrative paragraphs or set off as a labeled "Related literature" subsection within the Introduction. Formal numbered hypothesis statements are uncommon in the ABS 4*/4 finance journals on which FIELD_SKILL is based.

**This journal:** The Introduction establishes motivation and context but does not contain the full literature review. A dedicated Section 2 ("Literature and Hypotheses" or equivalent) presents the theoretical background and culminates in formally numbered hypothesis statements. Each hypothesis is presented as a standalone labeled statement: "Hypothesis 1. [complete declarative statement of the predicted relationship]." Subsections of Section 2 map one-to-one with hypothesis tests. This is standard management-journal architecture.

**Classification: PRACTICE ONLY** (consistent across articles; characteristic of the journal's management-journal identity).

### Citation Scope
**Field norm:** Citations concentrated in finance, accounting, and economics journals.

**This journal:** Citations routinely include management journals (Academy of Management Journal, Administrative Science Quarterly, Journal of Business Ethics, Organization Science) and behavioral sciences (psychology, organizational behavior) alongside finance journals. This is a disciplinary consequence of the governance subject matter. Authors positioning papers in CGIR should engage management and organizational literature even when the methods are standard finance empirics.

**Classification: PRACTICE ONLY**

### Author Biographies
**Field norm:** FIELD_SKILL does not mention author biographies; they are absent in mainstream finance journals.

**This journal:** Short author biographies (3–5 sentences per author) appear at the end of published articles. These are likely a Wiley/journal requirement.

**Classification: PRACTICE ONLY** (observed; could not verify against guidelines).

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Submitting an unstructured abstract.** The four-field structured abstract is non-negotiable. An unstructured abstract (as expected by mainstream finance journals) will fail desk review or require resubmission before the paper reaches editors.

2. **Burying the practitioner/policy implications.** The abstract's fourth field requires genuine practitioner or policy content. A perfunctory sentence ("managers should consider governance") is insufficient. Because this field is mandatory in the abstract, it should also be substantively addressed in the Introduction and Conclusion.

3. **Treating this as a pure finance journal.** Despite ABS classification under FINANCE, reviewers expect engagement with management and organizational theory. Submitting a paper that cites only finance journals and ignores the governance/management literature will be penalized on "contribution to the literature."

4. **Embedding hypotheses in the Introduction.** The FIELD_SKILL norm of delivering the paper's argument in a single dense Introduction does not fit CGIR convention. Hypotheses belong in a dedicated Literature and Hypotheses section; moving them into the Introduction violates the structural template reviewers expect.
