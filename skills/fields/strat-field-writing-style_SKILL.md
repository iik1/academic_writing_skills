# Field Writing Style: STRAT

## Scope and Intended Use

This baseline captures the shared writing conventions of ABS 4* and ABS 4 journals in strategic management. It covers macro structure, voice, argumentation, methods reporting, citation practice, and hedging norms that are consistent across the field's leading outlets. It does not cover journal-specific formatting, word or page limits, submission requirements, or publisher rules. Where a journal-specific skill conflicts with this baseline, the journal-specific skill takes precedence.

---

## Typical Article Structure

At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect a canonical section sequence that varies modestly by paper type but follows a recognizable template.

**Empirical papers** typically contain:
1. Abstract (research summary and, in many outlets, a separate managerial summary)
2. Introduction
3. Theory and Hypotheses (or Theoretical Background / Literature Review)
4. Data, Variables, and Method (or Empirical Setting and Methodology)
5. Results
6. Discussion and Conclusions (usually a single merged section)
7. References
8. Appendices / Online Supplement (for robustness material)

**Theoretical / computational / conceptual papers** commonly replace the empirical methods and results sections with a Model or Theoretical Development section, followed by Analysis and Robustness Checks before the discussion.

**Review papers** (meta-analyses, systematic reviews) follow a similar arc but replace hypothesis development with coding procedures and aggregation methods.

Near-universal sections across all paper types: Introduction, some form of theoretical grounding, a concluding section that integrates findings. Optional or paper-type-dependent: separate Robustness section (very common in empirical work), Managerial Implications subsection within discussion, dedicated Limitations and Future Research subsection.

A dedicated subsection on limitations and future research directions is near-universal within the discussion or conclusion; it is not expected to be a standalone section but typically appears as the penultimate or final subsection before references.

## Voice, Person, and Register

Leading journals in this field expect first-person plural ("we") as the default authorial voice throughout the manuscript, including single-authored pieces in practice. Impersonal constructions ("this paper argues", "the study examines") appear occasionally but are not the dominant mode.

Active voice is strongly preferred over passive, particularly for describing analytic choices, theoretical claims, and interpretations. Passive constructions appear in data and sample description ("data were collected from", "firms were identified as") but active voice dominates everywhere else.

The register is uniformly formal and precise. Colloquialisms, rhetorical questions directed at the reader, and informal hedges are avoided. At the same time, the prose is not archaic or unnecessarily complex; clarity is valued alongside rigor.

---

## Sentence and Paragraph Style

Sentences tend toward medium to long length, particularly in the theory and discussion sections where complex relationships must be articulated precisely. Short sentences are used selectively for emphasis at the opening or close of a paragraph. Paragraphs in the theory section are dense and citation-heavy; paragraphs in the results section are tighter and more data-anchored.

Technical vocabulary from relevant theoretical traditions (resource-based view, dynamic capabilities, transaction cost economics, institutional theory, organizational learning, etc.) is used precisely and without over-explanation for an expert audience. The field rewards conceptual depth and specificity over accessible brevity.

## Argumentation and Rhetorical Moves

**Introduction structure:** Leading journals in this field expect an introduction that follows a CARS-like (Create a Research Space) logic:

1. Establish the phenomenon's importance and scope — why this topic matters practically and theoretically.
2. Establish the niche — identify a tension, inconsistency, or gap in the existing literature. This is typically done by reviewing multiple streams of prior work (often framed as "one set of studies found... a second set found... a third set...") and demonstrating that prior explanations are incomplete, contested, or limited in scope.
3. State the research question — at the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect an explicit, crisply stated research question, often posed directly.
4. Preview the approach — briefly describe the method, data, or theoretical framework employed.
5. State the contributions — contributions are listed explicitly, often numbered or presented in a series of "First... Second... Third..." statements. Contributions are typically framed along multiple dimensions: theoretical, methodological, and managerial/practical.
6. Preview the findings — a summary of the main results appears in the introduction; this is expected, not a spoiler.

The introduction typically runs several paragraphs and carries high citation density. The gap is framed as an opportunity rather than an attack on prior work; prior studies are acknowledged for their contributions before their limits are identified.

**Contribution positioning:** Contributions are stated explicitly and prominently — usually near the end of the introduction. Phrases such as "our study advances research on X by...", "we contribute to... in three ways", and "to the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to..." are conventional. Reviewers expect the contribution statement to be specific, not generic.

**Literature positioning:** Prior work is organized into coherent streams or traditions. Disagreement or contradiction across streams is framed as a puzzle or opportunity rather than as error. The authors position their paper as a synthesis, extension, reconciliation, or bridge, not as a refutation.

## Methods Conventions

**Tense:** Data collection, sample selection, and descriptive steps are reported in past tense. Model specification and variable operationalization are often described in present tense when introducing definitions ("We define X as..."), then shift to past tense when describing execution ("We gathered data from...").

**Procedural detail:** At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect fine-grained procedural transparency. Sample selection criteria are reported step by step with explicit justification for each inclusion/exclusion decision. Variable operationalization cites established prior measures where possible and motivates any deviation.

**Identification and robustness:** Causal claims require explicit attention to identification strategy. Instrumental variable approaches, difference-in-differences designs, propensity score matching, and other quasi-experimental methods are increasingly expected when causal inference is claimed. A robustness section — either as a standalone section or as a dedicated subsection — is near-universal in quantitative empirical work. Sensitivity analyses, alternative operationalizations, and placebo tests are standard.

**Statistical reporting:** Practice varies across journals in this field: some require exact p-values or confidence intervals without asterisks; others permit conventional significance markers. Authors should consult journal-specific guidelines. In all cases, effect sizes and practical significance are expected alongside statistical significance; reviewers will flag results that report only p-values without contextual interpretation of magnitude.

**Qualitative and mixed methods:** The field accepts qualitative case studies, process studies, and mixed-method designs, particularly in outlets with broader scope. For qualitative work, transparency about data sources, coding procedures, and inter-rater reliability is expected, with similar procedural detail to quantitative work.

## Results and Reporting Norms

Results sections in quantitative papers are primarily table-driven; key coefficients, standard errors, and fit statistics appear in tables, with prose summarizing the pattern and directing attention to specific findings. Tables are not merely decorative; the prose is not a simple recitation of table contents but adds interpretive context and flags the most theoretically important results.

The boundary between results and interpretation is maintained but not absolute. Brief "inline" interpretation appears in results sections ("consistent with H1, the coefficient on X is positive and significant"), but fuller theoretical interpretation is deferred to the discussion. The discussion section is where findings are explicitly connected to prior theory, prior empirical studies, and contribution claims.

Null results are reported and discussed; they are not treated as failures. At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers increasingly expect authors to interpret non-significant results rather than pass over them in silence.

## Discussion and Conclusion Norms

At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect the discussion and conclusions to accomplish several tasks in sequence:

1. **Restate the core finding** briefly, without merely repeating the results section.
2. **Theoretical interpretation** — explain why the findings came out as they did, connecting to the theoretical mechanisms proposed in the hypotheses or theory section.
3. **Reconciliation with prior literature** — explicitly connect findings to the streams of literature reviewed in the introduction. Conflicting prior results are re-examined in light of the new evidence.
4. **Theoretical contributions** — revisit and elaborate the contribution claims from the introduction, showing specifically how the results substantiate each claim.
5. **Managerial implications** — a dedicated subsection on practical implications is expected in most outlets. This is distinct from the main argument and signals relevance for practitioners.
6. **Limitations and future research** — limitations are discussed openly but framed constructively. Each limitation is typically accompanied by a proposed future research direction. The framing convention is: "The limitations of our study provide interesting avenues for future research."
7. **Conclusion** — a brief closing paragraph that restates the central contribution without simply summarizing what came before.

Discussion and Conclusions are typically merged into a single section at the ABS 4*/4 level. Separate Discussion and Conclusion sections appear occasionally but are less common than the merged format.

## Citation and Literature Engagement

**Density:** Citation density is high in the introduction and theory/hypothesis development sections — typically multiple citations per substantive claim, with key foundational papers cited alongside more recent contributions. Citation density drops in the methods section (primarily for data sources and established measures), drops further in the results section (citations appear mainly to prior empirical benchmarks), and rises again in the discussion when findings are connected back to prior work.

**Paraphrase vs. quotation:** Direct quotation is rare and reserved for definitional or conceptually foundational statements by seminal authors. Paraphrase is overwhelmingly dominant. Authors are expected to engage with the substance of prior work, not reproduce its phrasing.

**How disagreement is handled:** Disagreement with prior work is framed as productive tension ("evidence remains mixed," "scholars have reached conflicting conclusions," "prior conceptual frameworks are ill-equipped to explain this discrepancy") rather than as criticism of individual scholars. The paper's contribution is then positioned as resolving, extending, or reframing the tension.

**Author-date format:** In-text citations follow author-date format (APA-adjacent), consistently throughout.

## Hedging and Epistemic Caution

Overall hedging intensity is moderate. The field expects epistemic caution in theory development and interpretation, but confident language when reporting results and stating contributions.

**Where hedging is expected:**
- Theoretical mechanisms: "we argue that firms may...", "we conjecture that...", "it is likely that..."
- Interpretation of results: "our findings suggest...", "the evidence appears consistent with..."
- Scope conditions: "these results may not generalize to contexts where..."
- Discussion of mechanisms not directly tested: "one potential explanation is..."

**Where hedging is not expected:**
- Contribution claims: these are stated directly and confidently ("this study is the first to...", "our results demonstrate that...")
- Formal hypotheses: hypotheses are stated as direct predictions, not hedged possibilities
- Core empirical findings: when the data support a hypothesis, the finding is reported directly ("consistent with H1, the degree of integration exerts a positive effect...")

Overuse of hedging weakens a paper at the ABS 4*/4 level; reviewers interpret excessive qualification as either conceptual imprecision or insufficient theoretical grounding for the claims made.

## What This Baseline Does NOT Specify

The following elements belong in journal-specific skills and are explicitly excluded from this baseline:

- Word or page limits for manuscripts or abstracts
- Required or prohibited section names (e.g., whether "Theoretical Background" or "Literature Review" is preferred)
- Publisher formatting requirements (font, margin, line spacing)
- Submission portal requirements and file format conventions
- Whether p-value asterisks are permitted or exact p-values required
- Whether a dual abstract (research + managerial) is required
- Special issue or editorial requirements
- Acceptance rates, review timelines, or editorial scope emphasis
- Requirements for ethics statements, data availability statements, or conflict of interest disclosures
- Specific style guide edition (APA 6th vs. 7th, etc.)
- Whether figures must be submitted separately or embedded
