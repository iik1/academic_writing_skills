# Field Writing Style: SOC SCI

## Scope and Intended Use

This baseline documents the shared writing conventions of ABS 4 journals in the Social Sciences field, derived from direct analysis of published articles. It covers structural norms, rhetorical conventions, voice, citation practice, and hedging patterns observable across leading journals in political science, sociology, socio-economic analysis, public health social science, risk analysis, and critical human geography.

This baseline does NOT cover: journal-specific word or page limits, section naming conventions required by individual journals, publisher formatting rules, submission requirements, or statistical reporting standards specific to any single outlet. Where a journal-specific skill conflicts with this baseline, the journal-specific skill takes precedence.

The SOC SCI field is methodologically diverse—encompassing quantitative causal inference, qualitative ethnography, mixed methods, comparative case studies, survey experiments, and critical theory. Several norms therefore vary by methodological tradition rather than converging to a single standard. These variations are flagged explicitly below.

## Typical Article Structure

At the ABS 4 level, reviewers expect a clearly articulated sequential structure that moves from motivation through evidence to interpretation. The canonical section set is:

**Near-universal sections (present in virtually all quantitative and mixed-methods work):**
- Abstract
- Introduction
- Theory / Literature Review (sometimes integrated into the Introduction, sometimes separate)
- Data and Methods (or equivalent)
- Results / Findings
- Discussion and/or Conclusion

**Common in quantitative work:**
- Robustness Checks / Sensitivity Analyses (often as a dedicated subsection or appendix)
- Mechanisms (to probe causal pathways after main results)
- Supplementary / Online Appendix (containing additional tables, model specifications, coding details)

**Common in qualitative and ethnographic work:**
- Background (contextual framing of the empirical setting)
- Findings divided into thematic subsections rather than a single Results section
- Conclusion that synthesizes themes and articulates broader implications

**Optional or field-specific:**
- Formal Hypothesis section (more common in quantitative political science than in sociology or geography)
- Data Availability statement (increasingly standard but not yet universal)

The canonical ordering follows the logic of the research process: motivation → prior knowledge → gap → design → evidence → interpretation. Leading journals expect this progression to be legible even when the section labels depart from convention, as they sometimes do in qualitative and theoretical work published in human geography outlets.

## Voice, Person, and Register

Practice varies considerably across methodological traditions, but some patterns are consistent:

**First-person plural ("we")** is the dominant convention in multi-authored quantitative and mixed-methods work. Authors write "we argue," "we find," "we estimate," and "we show" throughout the introduction, results, and discussion. This voice positions the authors as active agents of inquiry rather than passive reporters.

**First-person singular ("I")** is fully acceptable for single-authored work and is used without apology in political science and sociology outlets. Reviewers in this field do not expect false pluralisation.

**Passive voice** is conventionally deployed in methods and results sections to emphasise procedures and findings over the persons conducting them ("data were collected," "models were estimated," "participants were recruited"). However, passive voice in the introduction and discussion is widely regarded as unnecessarily distancing. Leading journals at the ABS 4 level show an increasing preference for active constructions throughout, with passive reserved for procedural description.

**Register** is uniformly high formality across quantitative and policy-oriented journals. Critical human geography and some sociology outlets permit a moderately more accessible or even experimental register, including first-person auto-ethnographic narration or essayistic prose. This variation is a genuine norm difference across sub-disciplines, not a deviation from standards.

**Hedging** is used throughout but calibrated to context (see Hedging section below).

## Sentence and Paragraph Style

Across quantitative journals in political science and socio-economic analysis, sentences tend toward medium to long length, accommodating embedded qualifications, conditional clauses, and parenthetical citations. Paragraphs are typically dense: three to six sentences organised around a single claim or step in the argument.

In qualitative sociology and human geography outlets, sentences may be shorter and more varied, especially where prose conveys narrative momentum. Paragraphs in findings sections may be longer when they carry extended vignettes or interview material.

Technical density is high in quantitative work: methods sections assume familiarity with regression notation, identification strategies, and common robustness diagnostics. In qualitative outlets, theoretical density replaces statistical density—concepts receive careful definitional treatment and are situated within disciplinary genealogies.

Across all sub-fields, reviewers at the ABS 4 level expect precise, economical prose. Redundancy, vague summarising phrases, and hollow transitional sentences are markers of weaker submissions.

## Argumentation and Rhetorical Moves

**Introduction structure:** The CARS (Creating a Research Space) model is the dominant convention across all journals in this field. Introductions consistently:
1. Establish the importance or prevalence of a phenomenon (establishing territory)
2. Identify what existing scholarship has not resolved or explained (identifying the gap)
3. State the paper's specific contribution and how it fills that gap (occupying the niche)

The gap is typically framed as a limitation of existing methods, a missing empirical case, an unresolved theoretical debate, or an underexplored mechanism—not as the wholesale failure of prior work. Reviewers expect a constructive framing that positions the paper as extending the literature rather than discrediting it.

**Contribution statement:** Explicit contribution statements are expected. In quantitative political science and socio-economic analysis, contributions are often enumerated ("This paper makes three contributions..."). In sociology and qualitative work, the contribution is more commonly embedded in a flowing argument in the introduction rather than listed. Both conventions are accepted within their respective sub-field norms.

**Positioning prior literature:** Prior work is engaged constructively. Disagreement is framed as "extending," "refining," "reconsidering," or "complicating" existing claims rather than as straightforward refutation. The rhetorical posture is to absorb and build upon, not demolish.

**Closing moves:** Conclusions restate the core finding, connect it to broader theoretical or policy implications, acknowledge limitations, and indicate directions for future research. The closing section is expected to expand outward—from the specific finding to its relevance for wider debates—rather than merely summarising.

## Methods Conventions

**Tense:** Methods are described in the past tense in quantitative and most mixed-methods work ("we estimated," "data were gathered," "participants were assigned"). In ethnographic work, ethnographic present is acceptable for illustrating field conditions.

**Level of detail:** Reviewers at the ABS 4 level expect sufficient procedural detail for replication assessment. In quantitative work, this includes: identification of the estimator and its justification, variable operationalisation, sample construction, treatment of missing data, and specification of any fixed effects or clustering. In qualitative work, detail covers: sample composition, recruitment, data collection mode, analytical approach, and positionality where relevant.

**Identification and robustness:** In quantitative causal inference work, the identification strategy receives prominent treatment—often in a dedicated subsection—and the paper is expected to anticipate the most plausible threats to validity (confounding, reverse causation, attrition) and address them explicitly. Robustness checks are nearly universal in quantitative work at this level and are typically presented as a separate section or set of tables, not buried in footnotes.

**Ethics and positionality:** Ethics statements and data availability notes are increasingly standard across the field. In qualitative and ethnographic work, researcher positionality and reflexivity are expected, often in the methods section.

**Pre-registration:** Pre-registration of analysis plans is increasingly valued in quantitative causal inference work and is sometimes noted prominently in the paper.

## Results and Reporting Norms

**Quantitative:** Tables and figures carry the primary evidential burden, but they are not self-interpreting. Prose narration is expected for each key result, directing the reader to specific model columns, panels, or statistics and explaining their substantive significance. Results sections tend to be leaner on citations and denser on statistical narration.

**Statistical narration precision:** At the ABS 4 level, narration of statistical findings goes beyond reporting significance: effect sizes, confidence intervals, and substantive magnitudes are expected. The norm is to convey what the finding means for a real-world quantity, not only whether a coefficient clears a significance threshold.

**Null results:** Null results are narrated rather than suppressed. Authors are expected to interpret null findings explicitly—whether they reflect true absence of effect or limitations of statistical power—particularly when the null contradicts prior theoretical predictions.

**Qualitative and ethnographic:** Findings are conveyed through extended prose, narrative vignettes, and interview excerpts. The boundary between reporting and interpretation is less marked: analysis is woven into description. Tables appear rarely or not at all; figures may convey conceptual frameworks rather than data.

**Separation of reporting and interpretation:** In quantitative work, results sections present findings and robustness evidence; interpretation and theoretical implications are reserved for the Discussion. In qualitative work, this separation is often partial or absent, with analysis integrated into the findings narrative.

## Discussion and Conclusion Norms

Practice varies: some journals and sub-disciplines maintain separate Discussion and Conclusion sections; others merge them into a single closing section. No single norm dominates across the field.

Where separate, the Discussion interprets findings in relation to prior theory and literature, revisits the paper's contribution in light of evidence, and addresses limitations directly. The Conclusion then summarises and expands outward to implications.

Where merged, the combined section typically progresses from interpretation to synthesis to limitation to implication without rigid subdivision.

Limitations are expected to be acknowledged honestly rather than minimised. The convention is to state them, explain why they do not fatally undermine the core finding, and suggest how future work might address them. Reviewers at the ABS 4 level are sceptical of papers that claim no significant limitations.

Future research directions are expected but should be substantive: specific gaps that the present paper opens rather than generic calls for more work.

## Citation and Literature Engagement

**Citation density by section:**
- Introduction: high. Each substantive claim about the state of the literature is supported by one or more citations. Dense citation clusters in the first two to three paragraphs are common.
- Theory/Literature Review: very high. This section is the primary space for sustained engagement with prior work; citation density is at its peak, often with multiple citations per sentence when surveying competing positions.
- Methods: moderate. Citations support methodological choices (estimator selection, variable construction) but procedural description is the section's primary function.
- Results: low to moderate. Citations appear when comparisons to prior empirical findings are made, but narration of results is largely citation-free.
- Discussion/Conclusion: moderate. Citations are used to contextualise findings within the broader literature and to support claims about implications.

**Paraphrase vs. quotation:** Paraphrase is the near-universal norm across quantitative political science, socio-economic analysis, risk analysis, and public health social science. Direct quotation from secondary sources is rare and typically reserved for particularly precise definitional claims. In qualitative sociology and human geography, quotation from primary data (interviews, field notes, documents) is expected and central to the evidentiary practice; quotation from secondary literature remains less common than paraphrase.

**Framing disagreement:** Disagreement with prior work is framed diplomatically. The standard moves are: acknowledging the prior contribution, noting the specific limitation or scope condition that the present paper addresses, and positioning the new finding as a refinement or extension. Aggressive dismissal of prior work is uncommon and viewed as unprofessional in this field.

## Hedging and Epistemic Caution

Overall hedging level is moderate across the field, calibrated to the strength of the evidence and the nature of the claim.

**When hedging is expected:**
- When moving from association to causation ("these findings are consistent with the interpretation that...," "suggests," "is associated with")
- When extrapolating from a specific empirical context to a broader population or phenomenon
- When acknowledging the role of unmeasured confounders or data limitations
- When interpreting mechanism-level findings that are indirect or based on observational data

**When hedging is reduced or absent:**
- When stating the paper's central contribution (contributions are typically stated directly: "this paper shows," "we demonstrate")
- When describing the paper's design and methods (past-tense procedural description carries no hedge)
- When reporting statistically robust findings that have survived multiple robustness checks

**Qualitative work:** Hedging in qualitative research is often epistemic rather than statistical—acknowledging interpretive plurality rather than uncertainty about point estimates ("this can be read as...," "one interpretation is..."). Claims about generalisability are typically modest and explicitly bounded.

**Risk analysis sub-field:** Uncertainty quantification is integral to the sub-discipline; hedging in this context is not merely rhetorical but substantive, and authors are expected to be precise about the sources and limits of their uncertainty estimates.

## What This Baseline Does NOT Specify

- Word or page limits for any journal in this field
- Required section headings or naming conventions specific to any journal
- Publisher formatting requirements (references style, font, margin, submission format)
- Statistical reporting requirements (specific test statistics, table formatting norms) beyond the general expectations described above
- Field-specific conventions for systematic reviews, meta-analyses, or registered reports
- Disciplinary norms within sub-fields that may diverge from this baseline (e.g., formal modelling conventions in international relations theory; ethnomethodological conventions in qualitative sociology)
- Anything else that is outlet-specific rather than shared across the ABS 4 tier of this field
