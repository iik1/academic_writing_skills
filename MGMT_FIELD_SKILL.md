# Academic Writing Conventions: Management (MGMT)

## 1. Macro Structure

### Standard article structure
Management articles follow one of four structural templates depending on article type:

**Quantitative deductive**: Abstract → Introduction → Theory and Hypotheses → Methods → Results → Discussion → Conclusion (or combined Discussion and Conclusion)

**Qualitative inductive**: Abstract → Introduction → Theoretical Background / Prior Literature → Research Setting and Methods → Findings → Discussion → Conclusion

**Theoretical / Conceptual**: Abstract → Introduction → [Conceptual sections organized by theoretical argument] → Discussion → Conclusion. No empirical sections. Sections are named by the substantive argument they advance, not by generic labels.

**Integrative review**: Abstract → Introduction → Search Strategy and Inclusion Criteria → Synthesis Sections (organized thematically or chronologically) → Research Agenda / Future Directions → Conclusion. Often includes a methods section describing the review process.

### Section presence and labeling
- A standalone Theory section is standard in quantitative work; it develops hypotheses and closes with an explicit numbered hypothesis statement.
- Qualitative work may use "Theoretical Background" or "Prior Literature" as the pre-empirical section; it orients the study without pre-specifying hypotheses.
- "Findings" is the preferred label over "Results" in qualitative work. "Results" is standard in quantitative work.
- Discussion and Conclusion may be separate sections or merged; a standalone Discussion is more common in top-tier outlets.
- Acknowledgments, Declarations of Competing Interest, and Data Availability statements appear at article end.

### Abstract
- Structured or unstructured; both appear across outlets.
- Typically 150–250 words.
- States the research question or gap, briefly describes the method or approach, summarizes the main finding, and identifies the theoretical contribution.
- No citations in abstract.

## 2. Introduction

### CARS structure (Create-A-Research-Space)
Management introductions follow the CARS model consistently:

1. **Establish territory**: Open by situating the topic within a recognized field or conversation. Cite foundational and recent work to show the territory is active and important.
2. **Establish niche**: Identify a gap, contradiction, underexplored phenomenon, or tension in existing theory or evidence. State explicitly what is unknown or contested.
3. **Occupy niche**: State the research question or objective, describe the approach (empirical context, method, or theoretical strategy), preview the contributions.

### Research question
- Stated explicitly, usually near the end of the introduction.
- Often framed as a direct question ("We ask: …") or as a statement of purpose ("We examine how…", "We seek to explain…").
- A single focal question is standard; sub-questions may appear but the main question is clearly primary.

### Contribution statement
- Contributions are announced explicitly in the introduction, typically as a numbered list or as distinct sentences that begin "First, we contribute…", "Second,…", "Third,…".
- Two to four contributions is the norm; a single contribution is rare; five or more is unusual.
- Contributions are framed in terms of existing literatures ("We extend resource dependence theory by…"; "We contribute to the growing literature on…").

### Length
- Introductions typically run 800–1,500 words (roughly 3–6 paragraphs).
- Long literature reviews do not appear in the introduction; prior work is acknowledged briefly to establish the niche, with full engagement reserved for the Theory section.

## 3. Voice and Person

### Pronoun usage
- First-person plural ("we") is the default for all co-authored work throughout the paper—methods, results, discussion, contribution claims.
- Single-authored theoretical work occasionally uses first-person singular ("I"), particularly in AMR-style pieces that develop the author's own framework.
- Passive voice is used selectively: for describing data sources, institutional processes, or when the agent is genuinely irrelevant ("Surveys were distributed via…"). Active constructions dominate argument and interpretation.

### Tone
- Academic but direct. Assertions are made with appropriate hedging but without excessive qualification.
- Personal voice is acceptable when advancing an argument; authors write "We argue," "We contend," "We propose."

## 4. Argumentation

### Logical architecture
- Each section or subsection advances a distinct step in the argument; sections do not merely review literature but develop a position.
- Theory sections in deductive work build logically toward each hypothesis. The reasoning chain (premise → mechanism → boundary condition → prediction) is made explicit.
- Conceptual/theoretical papers develop propositions rather than hypotheses; propositions are labeled (P1, P2…) and are accompanied by explicit verbal reasoning rather than statistical tests.

### Propositions vs. Hypotheses
- "Hypotheses" (H1, H2…) are used when the paper presents empirical tests. They take the form of directional predictions.
- "Propositions" (P1, P2…) are used in purely theoretical/conceptual papers where claims are supported by logical argument rather than data.
- Mixing hypotheses with conceptual reasoning (i.e., untested "hypotheses") is discouraged in the field's most prestigious outlets.

### Use of theory
- Named theoretical frameworks (e.g., institutional theory, resource dependence, upper echelons, social network theory) are identified early and used as scaffolding throughout.
- The theory section builds on and extends existing frameworks; it does not simply summarize them.
- Empirical papers test and refine theory; purely confirmatory tests of well-established predictions are considered insufficient for top journals.

## 5. Methods

### Quantitative methods
- Regression-based models (OLS, logit, probit, fixed-effects panel) are standard; structural equation modeling and survival analysis also appear.
- Sample sizes vary widely (dozens to tens of thousands depending on the unit of analysis); large archival samples are common in strategy/organizational behavior work.
- Fixed-effects models are expected when panel data is used; time and entity fixed effects are reported and justified.
- Identification concerns (endogeneity, selection) are addressed explicitly—instrumental variables, difference-in-differences, propensity score matching appear regularly.
- Robustness checks are reported as a matter of course: alternative specifications, subgroup analyses, different operationalizations of key variables.
- Tables report coefficient estimates with standard errors (in parentheses); asterisks denote significance levels; table notes define variables.

### Qualitative methods
- Ethnography, interview-based grounded theory, case study, and discourse analysis appear in AMJ, JOM, and Organization Science.
- Methods sections describe research setting, access, data collection procedures (number of interviews, hours of observation, archival sources), and analytic process (coding procedure, iteration between data and theory, theoretical saturation).
- Evidence is presented in findings sections as quotations or vignettes, selected to illustrate analytical categories. Quotes are attributed to anonymized or identified informants with brief contextual labels.
- Theoretical sampling and saturation are explained; trustworthiness is addressed (triangulation, member-checking, or reflexivity).

### Mixed and review methods
- Integrative reviews describe the search protocol (databases, keywords, inclusion/exclusion criteria) in a dedicated section. PRISMA-style flowcharts appear in some outlets.
- Bibliometric methods or content analysis appear in reviews; they are reported with the same precision as primary empirical methods.

### Tense
- Methods are reported in past tense ("We collected…", "We estimated…").
- Present tense is used for descriptions of the data that remain true ("The dataset contains…").

## 6. Results and Findings

### Quantitative results
- Tables are the primary vehicle; prose in the Results section guides interpretation.
- Authors report and interpret key coefficients; they do not merely refer the reader to the table.
- Hypotheses are explicitly revisited: "H1 predicted X; the coefficient is positive and significant (β = 0.32, p < .01), supporting H1."
- Effect sizes and confidence intervals are reported where relevant.

### Qualitative findings
- Organized around theoretical categories, not chronologically.
- Findings are presented as analytical constructs, not raw data summaries; each finding section develops a theoretical point with illustrative evidence.
- Data displays (tables of representative quotes, process models, typologies) are used to present evidence systematically.

## 7. Discussion and Conclusion

### Discussion structure
- Opens with a brief summary of findings (1–2 paragraphs).
- Develops theoretical implications: how findings extend, challenge, or qualify prior theory. Each implication connects back to specific cited work.
- Addresses practical implications where appropriate; management journals expect some managerial relevance, though theoretical contribution is primary.
- Discusses limitations openly: scope conditions, measurement, generalizability, or causal claims.
- Closes with future research directions framed as specific questions or calls for particular types of studies.

### Conclusion
- Either the final section of Discussion or a separate short section (2–4 paragraphs).
- Restates the core contribution concisely.
- Does not introduce new findings or substantially new arguments.
- May end with a brief statement about broader significance or future agenda.

## 8. Citation and Literature Engagement

### Style
- APA in-text citations: (Author, Year) or Author (Year) when the author is part of the sentence.
- Multiple citations in one parenthetical are ordered alphabetically or chronologically by convention: (Jones, 2010; Smith & Lee, 2015).
- Et al. for three or more authors from first citation.

### Density
- Introduction and Theory sections are citation-dense; virtually every empirical claim or theoretical assertion cites supporting work.
- Results and Findings sections cite little (methodology is already established); Discussion engages citations to interpret and situate findings.
- Foundational works (frequently cited classics) appear alongside recent publications; top management journals expect currency—citing work from the past 5–10 years alongside classics.

### Literature engagement
- Authors engage critically, not just approvingly; they note where prior work is limited, contested, or silent.
- Footnotes are used for definitional clarifications, literature tangents, or methodological details that would interrupt the main text.
- Reference lists are long (40–80 references is typical for empirical papers; reviews may exceed 100).

## 9. Hedging and Epistemic Stance

### Degree of hedging
- Moderate. Authors hedge interpretive claims but assert methodological procedures and established findings directly.
- Modal verbs ("may," "might," "could," "suggest," "appear to") mark inferences beyond what data directly support.
- "We find" and "Our results show" are used for reported results; "We argue," "We propose," "We suggest" mark theoretical positions.

### Phrases to note
- "Our findings suggest…" (hedged interpretation of results)
- "We argue that…" (direct theoretical assertion)
- "Consistent with [Theory X], we find…" (aligning results with theory)
- "Future research should examine…" (forward-looking recommendation)
- "We acknowledge that…" (limitation framing)
- "Although we cannot rule out…" (addressing alternative explanations)

## 10. Article-Type Variation Summary

| Feature | Quantitative deductive | Qualitative inductive | Theoretical/Conceptual | Integrative review |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-empirical section | Theory & Hypotheses | Theoretical Background | Conceptual sections | Literature synthesis |
| Central claim form | Numbered hypotheses (H1…) | Theoretical categories/process model | Numbered propositions (P1…) | Synthesized framework |
| Empirical section | Methods + Results | Research Site/Methods + Findings | None | Search/inclusion + synthesis |
| Evidence vehicle | Tables of regressions | Quotations, vignettes, data displays | Logical argument, conceptual diagrams | Systematic tables, narrative synthesis |
| Typical length | 10,000–15,000 words | 12,000–18,000 words | 8,000–14,000 words | 15,000–25,000 words |
| Robustness checks | Expected | Not applicable | Not applicable | Sensitivity analyses for inclusion criteria |
| First-person usage | "We" throughout | "We" throughout | "We" or "I" (single author) | "We" throughout |

## 11. Journal-Level Notes

- **AMR** (Academy of Management Review): Publishes only conceptual/theoretical work. No empirical data. Propositions required; hypotheses are not used. Discussion engages theory deeply. Single authors may use "I."
- **AMA Annals** (Academy of Management Annals): Publishes integrative reviews only. Review process must be described rigorously. Articles are substantially longer than in other outlets. A formal "Future Research Agenda" section is expected.
- **AMJ, ASQ, Organization Science, JOM**: Publish empirical work (quantitative and qualitative) and occasional theoretical pieces. Hypotheses for quantitative; qualitative papers use theoretical categories. AMJ and JOM have greater tolerance for inductive and ethnographic work; ASQ is associated with strong quantitative rigor and institutional/organizational theory.
