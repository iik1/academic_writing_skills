# Journal Writing Skill: The Sociological Review

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** SOC SCI
- **Publisher:** SAGE Publications (on behalf of The Sociological Review Foundation)
- **Scope:** UK's oldest sociology journal (founded 1908); international and interdisciplinary; publishes across all sociological traditions including theoretically advanced and methodologically diverse work; known for qualitative, theoretical, and critical sociology.
- **Guidelines URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/author-instructions/sor / https://thesociologicalreview.org/publish-with-us/journal/writing-for-the-sociological-review-journal/
- **Field baseline:** soc-sci-field-writing-style
- **Articles spot-checked:** 2 (DOI: 10.1177/00380261241293898; DOI: 10.1177/00380261241291676)

## Hard Constraints (Author Guidelines)

> These override FIELD_SKILL where they conflict.

### Structure
No section structure is mandated by the guidelines. The journal does not require a conventional IMRaD layout. FIELD_SKILL qualitative variation applies and is amplified here: pure theoretical/conceptual articles with no Methods or Data section are an accepted and common published format, not an exception (see Observed Deltas). For empirical qualitative work, standard qualitative structure applies.

### Length
**8,000 words maximum** for the main essay, including footnotes. References may add up to **1,000 additional words** and are excluded from the main count. Acknowledgements and funding statements are also excluded. The editor may approve a longer version at a later review stage but initial submission must meet the limit.

### Abstract
**Unstructured paragraph form**; approximately **250 words** maximum. No required sub-fields (no Background/Methods/Results/Conclusions labels). Both spot-checked articles use an unstructured paragraph that states the paper's argument, method, and contribution in continuous prose. Label: **ENFORCED**

### Citations
**APA (author–year) format** is required throughout. In-text citations take the form (Author, Year) or (Author, Year, p. X) for direct quotations. Reference list uses APA format with DOIs. Every in-text citation must have a corresponding reference list entry with identical author spelling and year. Label: **ENFORCED** (guidelines) and **CONFIRMED** in both articles.

### Headings
No prescriptive heading hierarchy is specified in guidelines beyond the instruction to "ensure your heading levels are clear, and the sections clearly defined." In practice, both spot-checked articles use substantive/evocative headings throughout (see Observed Deltas).

### Tables and Figures
Tables and figures must be **uploaded separately** from the manuscript. Accepted file formats: **JPEG and PNG only**. Items must be numbered in the order they are discussed in the text. Caption and formatting notes must be included. Lower resolution is acceptable at review stage; print quality required for final publication.

### Required Declarations Section
A **"Statements and Declarations"** section is required at the end of every submission, placed after Acknowledgements. It must include sub-headings covering: funding, conflicts of interest, ethics approval, data availability, and author contributions (where applicable). If a sub-heading does not apply, it must still appear with "Not applicable" beneath it. The journal explicitly encourages authors to address how ethical issues — including power inequalities, risk to participants, and confidentiality — were handled within the paper. Label: **ENFORCED**

### Keywords
Both spot-checked articles include a **keywords list** (5–7 terms) immediately after the abstract. The guidelines page for the journal confirms this as expected practice. Label: **ENFORCED**

### Explicit Prohibitions
- Simultaneous submission to another journal is prohibited.
- Manuscripts under consideration elsewhere will not be reviewed.
- Plagiarism and copyright violations subject manuscripts to duplication-detection checks.

## Observed Deltas from FIELD_SKILL

> Only dimensions where this journal deviates from the field baseline.

### Structural Deviations

**Delta 1 — Evocative/substantive section headings (PRACTICE ONLY)**

FIELD_SKILL describes the section set (Introduction, Theory/Literature Review, Data and Methods, Results, Discussion, Conclusion) but does not address heading *naming conventions*.

At The Sociological Review, both qualitative-empirical and purely theoretical articles consistently use interpretive, substantive headings rather than generic labels. Section titles carry the argument: e.g., "The non/monogamy complex: Infidelity, intimacy and secrecy"; "Doing secrecy: Secrets and institutionalised infidelity"; "From economic nationalism to cultural isolationism: A societal identity discourse"; "Securitization of halal markets: Leveraging and propagating Islamophobia." Generic headings ("Literature Review," "Results," "Discussion") do not appear in either article.

Classification: **PRACTICE ONLY** (not stated in guidelines but consistent across both articles).

**Delta 2 — Pure theoretical/conceptual articles without empirical methods (PRACTICE ONLY)**

FIELD_SKILL lists Data and Methods as "near-universal" in quantitative and mixed-methods work, and acknowledges variation in qualitative work, but does not specifically flag purely theoretical review articles — with no methods section, no data, and no empirical findings — as a standard published format.

At The Sociological Review, this format is actively published and normalised. One of the two spot-checked articles (Klesse & van Hooff, 2024) is a pure theoretical/literature review with no empirical data, no methods section, and no results section: the argument moves entirely through engagement with prior theory and published research. The section structure is Introduction → substantive conceptual sections → Conclusion, without any methodological or empirical content.

This is not a relaxation of quality — the theoretical density and citation engagement are at least as demanding as in empirical articles — but the structural template diverges from the near-universal IMRaD expectation in FIELD_SKILL.

Classification: **PRACTICE ONLY** (not stated in guidelines). For ABS 3: this is an extension of the qualitative variation noted in FIELD_SKILL, not a redefinition of the field baseline.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Exceeding the word limit on initial submission.** The 8,000-word cap (including footnotes, excluding references) is firm on submission. Reviewers or editors will not routinely overlook length; only post-review extensions may be negotiated with the editor.

2. **Using generic section headings.** Submitting with standard labels ("Literature Review," "Methodology," "Results," "Discussion") signals unfamiliarity with the journal's convention. Invest in interpretive, substantive headings that carry the paper's argument — this is a visible stylistic marker the journal consistently observes.

3. **Omitting or skeletonising the Statements and Declarations section.** All sub-headings must appear even when not applicable ("Not applicable"). Missing this section or treating it as optional is a straightforward compliance failure.

4. **Submitting a purely quantitative or causal-inference paper without considering fit.** The journal does not exclude quantitative work, but its profile is strongly qualitative, theoretical, and critical. Quantitative papers without a strong sociological theoretical framework are likely to be a poor fit for this outlet.
