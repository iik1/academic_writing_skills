# Writing Style Skill: Energy Economics

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** ECON
- **Publisher:** Elsevier
- **Discipline / Scope:** Premier field journal for energy economics and energy finance. Covers exploitation, conversion and use of energy; markets for energy commodities and derivatives; regulation and taxation; forecasting; environment and climate; international trade, development, and monetary policy. Accepts theoretical, empirical, and policy-oriented papers. Provides a forum for economic and econometric modelling of energy systems.
- **Typical article types:** Empirical research articles (panel data, time series, IV, DiD, spatial models), theoretical/structural models, applied policy analysis, simulation and equilibrium model papers, financial economics of energy (risk, volatility, hedging). ## Author Guidelines (Hard Rules)

> These are the journal's stated requirements. They take precedence over observed patterns when there is a conflict.

### Manuscript Structure
No rigid section sequence is mandated in the guide for authors. The journal accepts manuscripts across a wide variety of methodological approaches. For econometric papers, authors must provide programs, datasets, and a readme file showing how to replicate each table, graph, and result. For simulation/equilibrium/optimization model papers, authors must provide data and programs. For experimental or survey-based papers, original instructions, subject eligibility information, raw data, and analysis programs are required. Replication policy is applied in spirit rather than to the letter.

### Length Requirements
No explicit maximum word count is stated in the publicly accessible guidelines. In practice, published articles in Energy Economics typically run 8,000–12,000 words (excluding references), consistent with Elsevier economics journal norms. The journal does not impose page charges, but there is a submission fee of USD 100 for original manuscripts.

### Abstract
Unstructured (no mandatory fields such as Background/Methods/Results). A concise, factual abstract is required — no citations, no non-standard abbreviations. Any abbreviations used in the abstract must be defined at first mention within the abstract. Abstracts should accurately summarize the article's content and key findings.

### Citations and References
**Author-date (name-year) system.** In-text format: single author uses surname and year (e.g., Smith (2020) or (Smith, 2020)); two authors uses both surnames; three or more authors uses first author et al. and year. Citations can be direct (parenthetical) or narrative. Reference list arranged alphabetically, then chronologically for same-author entries. DOIs recommended. The journal's formal citation style for published articles is elsarticle-num (numbered), but the guide explicitly states that any consistent author-date or numbered system is accepted at submission — the journal applies its final style at proof stage. In practice, author-date is the convention used in manuscript text by authors in the field. JEL classification codes (up to 6) and keywords (1–7) are required. Keywords should be written in English; avoid multiple-word keywords with "and"/"of"; only well-established abbreviations are acceptable as keywords.

### Headings
No explicit heading hierarchy rules are stated. In practice, numbered sections (1, 2, 3...) with numbered subsections (2.1, 2.2...) are the operative norm. Heading depth rarely exceeds two levels in most articles.

### Tables and Figures
All tables must be submitted as editable text, not as images. All tables and all figures must be cited in the manuscript text in sequence. Table notes should appear below the table body. Figures should be of sufficient resolution for publication. Captions must be provided for all tables and figures.

### Language and Tone Requirements
Manuscripts should be written in American or British English — consistently, not mixing the two. The journal accepts contributions from a range of disciplines; language should be appropriate for an economics audience while remaining accessible to energy policy analysts. Authors should use the international system of units (SI) with equivalent values provided for any non-SI units mentioned.

### Prohibitions
- Do not mix American and British English conventions.
- Do not submit tables as images.
- Do not include non-standard abbreviations in the abstract without defining them.
- Do not omit replication materials for econometric, simulation, or experimental papers.
- Do not submit without the USD 100 submission fee.
- Highlights (a short collection of bullet points capturing novel results and new methods) are required at submission.

## Observed Writing Patterns

### Voice and Person
Active voice with first-person plural is the dominant convention. Phrases such as "We find that...", "We estimate...", "We use...", "We show...", "Our results suggest...", "We contribute to the literature by..." are standard across observed articles. The passive voice appears in methodology descriptions ("The model is estimated using..."), in data description ("Data are drawn from..."), and when describing what prior literature has done. First-person singular ("I") is used in single-authored papers. Impersonal constructions ("This paper investigates...") appear as an alternative to first-person plural in introductions, particularly for stating the paper's objective.

### Sentence and Paragraph Style
Sentences are medium length (20–40 words on average); mix of longer expository sentences and shorter declarative sentences for key claims. Paragraphs in the introduction and conclusion are typically 4–7 sentences; methods and results paragraphs tend to be shorter and more technical. Numbered or lettered lists are rare — continuous prose is preferred. Quantitative claims are integrated into prose rather than isolated as bullet points.

### Hedging and Epistemic Stance
Moderate hedging throughout. Causal claims in empirical work are hedged with "suggests," "indicates," "appears," "is consistent with," "we interpret this as." Definitive statements are used for results from well-identified designs (instrumental variables, difference-in-differences): "We find that..." Strong policy recommendations are often prefaced with "Our findings suggest that policymakers may wish to..." or "These results are consistent with the view that..." Overclaiming causality in observational settings is avoided; identification strategy limitations are acknowledged. Absolute certainty language ("proves," "demonstrates conclusively") is rare and typically reserved for theoretical proofs.

### Transitional and Cohesive Phrases
Standard signposting at article level: "The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents... Section 3 describes... Section 4 reports... Section 5 concludes." Within sections: "To motivate our approach...", "Before proceeding, we note that...", "Turning to [X]...", "A natural concern is...", "To address this issue...", "Consistent with [prior finding]...", "In contrast to [X], we find..." Literature transitions: "While [author, year] finds... we depart from this approach by...", "Building on [author, year]..."

### Formality and Register
High formal register throughout. No colloquialisms, contractions, or informal language. Technical vocabulary from economics and econometrics (endogeneity, identification, heteroskedasticity, stationarity, fixed effects, causal inference) used without definition when standard. Energy-sector terminology (merit-order effect, levelized cost, total factor productivity, green TFP, industrial agglomeration, feed-in tariff, tradable green certificates, emissions trading scheme) used with definition at first occurrence if not universally known. Acronyms defined at first use in text body; re-defined in abstract if used there.

### Guideline vs. Practice Discrepancies
- **Citation style:** The guide accepts any consistent style at submission; in practice virtually all published articles in this field use author-date, not numbered citations. Published articles show the name-year format in text. The numbered (elsarticle-num) format is the typeset production style.
- **Word count:** No explicit limit stated; observed articles run 8,000–12,000 words. Authors should not interpret the absence of a stated limit as license for very long papers — desk rejection risk increases beyond ~12,000 words.
- **Section structure:** Not mandated but numbered sections (Introduction / Literature Review or Background / Methodology or Model / Data / Results / Robustness / Conclusions) is strongly normative in empirical articles. Theory papers may omit the Data section.
- **Abstract length:** No word limit stated in guidelines, but observed abstracts are typically 150–200 words, consistent with Elsevier norms and space constraints on published pages.

## Section-by-Section Writing Guide

### Abstract
Unstructured; typically 150–200 words. Opens with a one-sentence statement of the paper's question or objective. Follows with a brief description of the data and method (one to two sentences). States the main findings in 2–4 sentences (specific enough to be informative — actual direction and magnitude of key results is preferred over vague summaries). May close with a one-sentence policy or contribution implication. No citations, no non-standard abbreviations, no discussion of prior literature. Ends before JEL codes and keywords, which are listed separately.

### Introduction
Typically 600–1,200 words. Structure follows CARS (Create a Research Space) model informally:
1. **Establish the territory:** Open by motivating the energy economics question with real-world significance or stylized facts (1–2 paragraphs). Reference the policy context or empirical puzzle.
2. **Establish a niche:** Survey the relevant literature briefly, identifying what is known and, crucially, what gap exists ("However, [X] has received little attention..." or "Existing studies have not addressed...").
3. **Occupy the niche:** State the paper's objective, data, method, and main findings in 2–3 paragraphs. Enumerate 2–4 specific contributions to the literature.
4. **Roadmap:** Close with a standard roadmap sentence ("The remainder of this paper proceeds as follows...").

Literature citations in the introduction are dense but typically parenthetical: (Smith, 2020; Jones, 2021). The introduction does not contain equations.

### Methods
Tense: present tense for describing the model; past tense for describing what was estimated. High procedural detail — sufficient to replicate. Equations are numbered sequentially throughout the paper. Econometric specifications use standard notation (subscripts i, t for unit and time; Greek letters for parameters; u or ε for error term). Endogeneity and identification threats are addressed explicitly ("A potential concern is..."). Estimation approach stated clearly: "We estimate Equation (3) using two-stage least squares with [instruments]..." References to prior methodological literature are used to justify choices (e.g., citing Heckman sample selection correction, Simar-Wilson bootstrap, spatial Durbin model). Robustness checks are described briefly here or in a dedicated subsection, with detailed results deferred to an appendix or online supplement.

### Results
Typically the longest section. Present tense for reporting estimated coefficients; past tense for describing the estimation procedure. Statistics presented in-text: "Column (1) of Table 2 reports an estimated coefficient of 0.43 (t-statistic = 3.2), indicating that..." or "The coefficient on [variable] is positive and statistically significant at the 1% level (β = 0.43, SE = 0.13)." Significance stars (***/**/*) used in regression tables with notes specifying thresholds. Standard errors reported in parentheses below coefficients in tables; robust or clustered standard errors noted in table notes. Figures used for time trends, distributions, and graphical depictions of heterogeneous effects. Interpretation is typically provided within the Results section for empirical papers (rather than deferred entirely to Discussion). Heterogeneity analysis and robustness checks follow the main results.

### Discussion
Not always a separate section in Energy Economics papers — discussion is often integrated into the Results section or absorbed into a combined Results and Discussion section. When present as a standalone section (more common in policy-oriented papers), it: connects findings back to the literature ("Consistent with [prior study]...", "In contrast to [prior finding]..."); discusses economic mechanisms; addresses alternative interpretations; presents policy implications; and frames limitations. Policy implications are stated concisely and directly: "Our findings suggest that carbon pricing policies of the type examined here are likely to [effect]."

### Conclusion
Typically 400–700 words. Opens with a brief restatement of the paper's question and main findings (2–3 sentences; does not recapitulate the analysis in detail). Discusses the broader significance of findings for the literature and for policy. States limitations explicitly (1–2 sentences): "Our analysis is limited to [scope]; generalizing to [other context] should be done with caution." States future research directions (1–3 suggestions): "Future work could extend this analysis by...". Does not introduce new results. Closes with a forward-looking or synthesis sentence. Avoid ending on an overly weak limitation — close on the paper's positive contribution.

### References
Author-date format in manuscript text. Reference list alphabetically ordered. Journal article format: Author, A.A., Author, B.B., Year. "Article title." Journal Name vol(issue), pages. DOI. Book format: Author, A.A., Year. Book Title. Publisher, City. Chapter in edited volume: Author, A.A., Year. "Chapter Title." In Editor, E.E. (Ed.), Book Title. Publisher, City, pp. X–Y. Working papers: Author, A.A., Year. "Title." Working Paper [number], Institution. DOIs are recommended for all references where available. The reference list is not numbered for in-text use at manuscript stage.

## Common Pitfalls
1. **Conflating the journal's production format with the submission format.** The elsarticle-num (numbered citations) format appears in typeset published articles. At submission, use consistent author-date; numbered citations at submission are unusual and may signal unfamiliarity with the journal.
2. **Omitting replication materials.** Energy Economics has an explicit replication policy. Submitting an econometric paper without being prepared to provide code, data, and a readme file risks rejection or mandatory revision. State data availability and code availability in the manuscript.
3. **Insufficient identification strategy discussion.** The journal's readership is sophisticated in econometrics. A paper that does not explicitly address endogeneity, omitted variable bias, or measurement error — or that treats OLS as causally identified without justification — will be scrutinized heavily. Articulate the identification strategy in the Methods section and defend it.
4. **Vague or overly broad abstracts.** Abstracts that describe only the topic and method without reporting actual findings (direction and magnitude of key results) are a missed opportunity. Energy Economics abstracts should be informative enough that a reader can assess fit without reading further.
5. **Missing highlights.** The journal requires highlights at submission — a short bulleted list of novel results and new methods. Authors unfamiliar with Elsevier submission systems may overlook this requirement.
6. **Heading structure that buries the methods.** Some authors from adjacent fields (e.g., environmental science) use an IMRaD structure without a literature review section. Energy Economics empirical papers almost always include a dedicated literature review or related literature section (Section 2 or integrated into the introduction). Omitting this can signal field-outsider writing.

## Representative Phrase Bank

### Introduction phrases
- Gap statement: "Despite the growing importance of [X], the empirical evidence on [specific relationship] remains limited." / "To the best of our knowledge, no study has examined [X] using [method/data]." / "Existing studies have primarily focused on [Y], leaving the question of [Z] largely unaddressed."
- Objective statement: "This paper investigates the causal effect of [X] on [Y] using [data] and [identification strategy]." / "We contribute to this literature in three ways." / "This paper provides the first [cross-country / firm-level / city-level] evidence on..."

### Methods phrases
- Procedure description: "We estimate the following panel fixed-effects regression:" / "To address potential endogeneity of [variable], we instrument using [instrument], which satisfies the exclusion restriction because..." / "Following [author, year], we construct [measure] as..."
- Data source statement: "Our primary data source is [name], which covers [units] over the period [dates]." / "Data on [variable] are drawn from [source] and merged with [other source] using [identifier]."

### Results phrases
- Statistic inline: "Column (2) of Table 3 reports a coefficient of 0.38 (standard error = 0.12), significant at the 5% level." / "A one-standard-deviation increase in [X] is associated with a [magnitude] increase in [Y], representing [economic significance interpretation]."
- Figure reference: "Figure 2 plots the estimated event-study coefficients, which show a sharp discontinuity at the policy cutoff." / "As shown in Panel B of Figure 3, the effect is concentrated among [subgroup]."

### Discussion phrases
- Connecting to literature: "Consistent with [Author, Year], we find that..." / "Our results extend those of [Author, Year] by showing that this relationship holds even after controlling for [factor]." / "In contrast to [Author, Year], who find [X], our estimates suggest [Y]; this divergence may reflect [explanation]."
- Limitation framing: "We acknowledge that our analysis is limited to [countries/firms/period] and may not generalize to [other contexts]." / "One caveat is that our data do not allow us to distinguish between [mechanism A] and [mechanism B]."

### Conclusion phrases
- Summary opener: "This paper examined the effect of [X] on [Y] using [data and method]. Our main finding is that [key result]." / "Using [identification strategy], we provide evidence that [main contribution]."
- Future direction: "An important avenue for future research is to examine whether these results hold in [other setting]." / "Future work could exploit [new data source / natural experiment] to shed further light on [open question]."
