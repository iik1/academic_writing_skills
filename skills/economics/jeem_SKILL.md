# Writing Style Skill: Journal of Environmental Economics and Management

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** ECON
- **Publisher:** Elsevier (ScienceDirect)
- **Discipline / Scope:** Environmental and resource economics. Publishes theoretical and empirical papers that address economic questions related to natural resources and the environment. Topics include environmental policy, non-market valuation, resource management, energy economics, agricultural economics with environmental dimensions, and environmental problems in developing countries.
- **Typical article types:** Empirical identification papers (majority of recent output); theoretical model papers; structural/quantitative analysis; methodological contributions; short notes. Does not publish book reviews, literature reviews, or policy briefs.

## Author Guidelines (Hard Rules)

> These are the journal's stated requirements. They take precedence over observed patterns when there is a conflict.

### Manuscript Structure
No mandated section sequence for regular articles. Required manuscript components: title page (separate, blinded for review), abstract, keywords (1–7), highlights (separate file), main body, acknowledgements, references, appendices. Graphical abstract optional. Replication package (data + code + README) required before final acceptance for empirical papers. Double-anonymized review: manuscripts must not identify the authors.

### Length Requirements
- **Regular articles:** No explicit word limit in guidelines. Observed range approximately 8,000–14,000 body words.
- **Notes and Short Papers:** Maximum **6,000 words** (body only, excluding tables, figures, appendices). Maximum **5 combined tables and figures**, each limited to one page. Appendices online-only. Word count must be stated at submission. **[ENFORCED]**
- **Submission fee:** USD 100 (full); USD 50 for full-time students.

### Abstract
- Unstructured prose; no labelled fields.
- Maximum **250 words**.
- Must state purpose, principal results, and major conclusions.
- Must stand alone; avoid references if possible.
- Define any non-standard abbreviations at first mention.

### Citations and References
- **In-text:** Author-year parenthetical: (Smith, 2020); (Smith and Jones, 2020); (Smith et al., 2020).
- **Reference list:** Alphabetical by first author. Format: LastName, F.N., Year. Title. Journal Volume, pages. DOI.
- Not numbered citations.
- References in Notes/Short Papers should be kept concise.

### Headings
Guidelines do not specify a heading format. Observed practice: Arabic numeral section numbering (1, 2, 3…); decimal subsection numbering (1.1, 1.2…); section headings bold; subsection headings bold or italic.

### Tables and Figures
- Regular articles: no stated limit.
- Notes/Short Papers: max 5 combined, each ≤1 page. **[ENFORCED]**
- Numbered sequentially; cited in text in order of appearance.
- Table captions above body; notes below body.
- Figure captions below figure.

### Language and Tone Requirements
- English required; non-native speakers encouraged to seek language editing.
- No explicit active/passive voice preference in guidelines.

### Prohibitions
- Does not publish: book reviews, literature reviews, policy briefs.
- Rarely publishes: pure replications; methods-applied-to-new-cases without added novelty; incremental theoretical extensions of known models.
- AI tools may not be listed as authors. AI use in preparation must be declared.
- Conflict of interest disclosures mandatory.

## Observed Writing Patterns

### Voice and Person
First-person plural ("we") dominates throughout all sections, including the abstract ("We study…", "We find…", "We estimate…", "We show…"). Single-authored papers use "I." Passive voice is used for data description and procedural steps ("Data are drawn from…", "Standard errors are clustered at…") but active voice dominates for findings and argument. The register is direct and assertive; understated or excessively hedged prose is atypical.

### Sentence and Paragraph Style
- Sentences average 20–35 words; declarative short sentences for findings, longer for methods detail.
- Paragraphs average 4–8 sentences.
- Results and arguments are front-loaded: the main finding appears in the abstract and again in the first two pages of the introduction.
- No "mystery novel" structure — conclusions are not withheld until late in the paper.

### Hedging and Epistemic Stance
Moderate hedging; less than in management or sociology journals.
- "suggest," "appear to," "consistent with," "may," "likely," "could" — uncertainty and interpretation
- Causal language ("causes," "leads to") used when identification is strong; correlational language ("is associated with") when identification is weaker.
- Robustness is explicitly addressed: "Our results are robust to…", "Results are qualitatively similar when…"
- Limitations acknowledged, typically briefly.

### Transitional and Cohesive Phrases
- Introduction road-map: "The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the data…"
- Section openers: "In this section, we…", "We now turn to…", "We next examine…"
- Results transitions: "Column (X) of Table (Y) shows that…", "As shown in Figure (Z)…", "Turning to [outcome]…"
- Contribution framing: "This paper contributes to the literature on [X] by…", "To the best of our knowledge, this is the first paper to…"

### Formality and Register
High formality; consistent academic register. Field-standard technical terms used without definition (externality, Pigouvian tax, willingness to pay, common pool resource, difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity). Acronyms defined at first mention in abstract (if used there) and again at first use in the body. Quantitative precision preferred: effect sizes are given with magnitudes and units.

### Guideline vs. Practice Discrepancies
- **JEL codes:** Not mentioned in guidelines but present in all published articles — include them with the abstract.
- **Abstract word limit:** Guidelines state 250 words; observed abstracts appear to use 150–230 words.
- **Reference style:** No named style specified in guidelines; observed practice consistent with author-year Chicago/Harvard variant.
- **Regular article word limit:** Not in guidelines; field norms and observed lengths suggest 8,000–12,000 body words as a practical target.

## Section-by-Section Writing Guide

### Abstract
- 150–250 words; unstructured; one paragraph.
- Implicit structure: (1) What is studied and why it matters; (2) Research design/identification strategy; (3) Main findings, quantified; (4) Optional: implications or contribution.
- Opening verbs: "We study…", "We examine…", "We analyze…", "We estimate…", "This paper investigates…"
- Findings: "We find that…", "Our results show that…", "We estimate that a [X]% increase in [A] leads to a [Y]% decline in [B]."
- Do not cite references unless essential.

### Introduction
- Length: approximately 800–1,500 words (2–4 pages).
- Follows CARS model:
  - **Move 1 (territory):** Open with the policy or economic phenomenon. Motivate why it matters. 1–3 paragraphs.
  - **Move 2 (niche):** Identify the research gap. Acknowledge prior work; explain what this paper adds that prior work could not. 1–2 paragraphs.
  - **Move 3 (occupying niche):** State the contribution explicitly. Describe data, identification strategy, and main result with magnitudes. 2–4 paragraphs.
- Embedded literature positioning paragraph relates the paper to 3–5 prior literature streams. Standalone literature review sections are uncommon.
- Final paragraph: road-map of the paper's structure.
- Main result is stated in the abstract AND repeated in the introduction.

### Methods
- Data subsection: appears before empirical strategy. Covers source, coverage, sample restrictions, key variables, summary statistics.
- Empirical strategy subsection: names and defends the identification strategy explicitly. States the identifying assumption. Common strategies: DiD, RD, IV, panel fixed effects, structural estimation.
- Equations numbered and referenced in text.
- Tense: present for describing the specification ("We estimate…"); past for sample construction ("We merged…", "We dropped…").
- Level of detail: sufficient for replication; full details in the replication package.

### Results
- Coefficient estimates with standard errors in parentheses and significance stars.
- Magnitude interpretation follows each estimate: "This implies that [contextual magnitude]."
- Table references: "Table 1, Column (2) reports our preferred specification."
- Interpretation is typically in the results section, not deferred to a standalone discussion section.
- Heterogeneity analysis and mechanism exploration often in subsections within results.
- Robustness subsection follows main results.

### Discussion
- Many JEEM empirical papers integrate discussion into results and conclusion; standalone Discussion sections are not universal.
- When present: addresses external validity, mechanisms, policy implications, limitations.
- Connects findings to prior literature: "Consistent with [Author, Year]…", "In contrast to [Author, Year]…"
- Limitations framed constructively: "While our analysis is limited to [scope], the identification strategy provides [credibility claim]."

### Conclusion
- Length: 200–600 words (0.5–1.5 pages).
- Structure: (1) Restatement of question and approach; (2) Summary of main findings, quantified; (3) Policy implications; (4) Limitations and future directions.
- No new results introduced.
- Opening: "This paper studies…", "In this paper, we…", "We have estimated…"
- Policy language is hedged: "Our results suggest that [policy] could [outcome]. However, [caveat]."

### References
- Alphabetical by first author surname.
- Format for journal article: LastName, F.N., Co-Author, F.N., Year. Title of article. Journal Name Volume (Issue), start–end. DOI.
- Working papers: Institution, Working Paper [number], Year.
- Data sources must be cited.
- DOI included for all works where available.

## Common Pitfalls
1. **Contribution framed as application, not innovation.** The guidelines explicitly exclude papers that apply established methods to new case studies without added novelty. A contribution paragraph that reads "we apply X to Y" will trigger desk rejection. Reframe: "We provide the first causal estimates of [effect] by exploiting [variation] and find [novel result]."
2. **Missing or incomplete Highlights.** Elsevier's submission portal requires a separate Highlights file with 3–5 bullet points, each a maximum of 85 characters including spaces. Omitting this file or exceeding the character limit blocks submission before editorial review begins.
3. **Abstract states methods but not results.** The 250-word abstract must report the main quantitative finding. "We find significant effects" is insufficient. Replace with "We find that a [X]% increase in [A] reduces [B] by [Y]%."
4. **Replication package not prepared.** For accepted empirical papers, a replication package (data + code + README following Social Science Data Editors' template) is required before final acceptance. Prepare this during the writing stage to avoid post-acceptance delays.
5. **Self-identification in double-anonymized submission.** Self-referential citations must be anonymized ("as in [AUTHOR, YEAR]" not "as in our earlier work"). Institutional affiliations and acknowledgements must be removed from the submitted manuscript file.

## Representative Phrase Bank

### Introduction phrases
- **Gap statement:**
  - "Despite a growing body of research on [topic], little is known about [specific gap]."
  - "Prior work has established [X] but has been unable to identify [Y] due to [data/identification constraint]."
  - "Existing estimates may [bias direction] if [concern], a limitation we address by [approach]."
- **Objective statement:**
  - "This paper studies the causal effect of [X] on [Y] using [data/strategy]."
  - "We exploit [natural experiment / policy change] to provide the first causal estimates of [effect]."
  - "We contribute to the literature on [stream] by [specific innovation]."

### Methods phrases
- **Procedure description:**
  - "We estimate the following regression model: [equation]. The outcome variable [Y] is [definition]. The key regressor [X] captures [economic concept]."
  - "Identification comes from [source of variation], which provides plausibly exogenous variation in [treatment] conditional on [fixed effects / controls]."
- **Data source statement:**
  - "Our primary data source is [source], which covers [unit/period/geography]."
  - "We merge [dataset A] with [dataset B] using [matching variable], yielding a panel of [N] [units] over [T] years."
  - "We restrict the sample to [subset] to ensure [comparability / clean identification]."

### Results phrases
- **Statistic inline:**
  - "Column (2) of Table 3 reports our preferred specification. A one-unit increase in [X] is associated with a [Y] percentage point [increase/decrease] in [outcome] (coefficient = [b], s.e. = [se])."
  - "This estimate implies that [contextual magnitude]."
- **Figure reference:**
  - "Figure 2 plots [what is shown]. The pattern is consistent with [interpretation]: [descriptive finding]."

### Discussion phrases
- **Connecting to literature:**
  - "This finding is consistent with [Author, Year], who document [related finding] in [context]."
  - "Our estimate lies within the range reported by [Author A, Year] and [Author B, Year]."
  - "In contrast to [Author, Year], we find [different result], which may reflect [difference in setting, time period, or identification]."
- **Limitation framing:**
  - "Our analysis focuses on [scope], which may limit external validity to [contexts]."
  - "While we cannot directly observe [mechanism], the pattern of heterogeneous effects is consistent with [mechanism] rather than [alternative explanation]."

### Conclusion phrases
- **Summary opener:**
  - "This paper studies [question] using [data and strategy]. Our main finding is that [quantitative result]."
  - "We have estimated the causal effect of [X] on [Y], exploiting [variation]. We find that [main result]."
- **Future direction:**
  - "Future research could extend this analysis to [context] or examine [related question] using [emerging data]."
  - "An important open question is whether [finding] generalizes to [other settings], which we leave for future work."
