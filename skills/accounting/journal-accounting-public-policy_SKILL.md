# Journal Writing Skill: Journal of Accounting and Public Policy

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** ACCOUNT
- **Publisher:** Elsevier (ScienceDirect)
- **Scope:** Publishes research at the intersection of accounting and public policy — how accounting practices affect policy outcomes and how regulatory/policy changes affect accounting behavior. Explicitly interdisciplinary; welcomes connections to economics, law, political science, and sociology.
- **Guidelines URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/journal-of-accounting-and-public-policy/publish/guide-for-authors
- **Field baseline:** account-field-writing-style
- **Articles spot-checked:** 3 ("Does Choice Matter? The Effect of Filing Method Autonomy on Taxpayer Aggressiveness in a Pre-Filled Tax Return System," Vol. 43 2024; "Do U.S. Multinationals Use Income Shifting to Facilitate and Hide Corruption?" Vol. 46 2024; "Public Country-by-Country Reporting and Corporate Social Responsibility," Vol. 55 2026)

## Hard Constraints (Author Guidelines)

> These override FIELD_SKILL where they conflict.

### Structure
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm for main articles. Additionally, the journal has a dedicated **"Accounting Letters"** format for short research articles (~3,000 words maximum), with expedited review (target: four weeks) and accelerated publication (target: four months post-acceptance). Accounting Letters submissions follow the same structural norms as regular articles but compressed. **ENFORCED**

### Length
No explicit word or page limit stated for standard research articles in the available guidelines text. Accounting Letters: ~3,000 words maximum. **ENFORCED** for Accounting Letters; unspecified for standard articles.

### Abstract
Unstructured (single paragraph). Word limit not explicitly stated in guidelines. Follows field norm: research question, method, key findings, contribution. **FIELD DEFAULT** for format; word limit unconfirmed.

### Citations
Elsevier house style. Specific format (numbered vs. author-date) not explicitly confirmed in available guidelines text. Standard accounting practice on Elsevier journals is author-date; verify against published articles in the target issue before submission.

### Headings
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm.

### Tables and Figures
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm. Standard Elsevier requirements apply (figure captions below figures, table captions above tables).

### Highlights
Required as a submission element (Elsevier standard): 3–5 bullet points summarizing core findings, presented separately from the abstract. Not part of the published article in all formats, but required at submission. **ENFORCED**

### Generative AI Declaration
Required. Any use of generative AI tools (beyond basic grammar/spell check) must be declared in a dedicated section placed before the References. AI tools may not be listed as authors. **ENFORCED**

### Inclusive Language
Explicitly required throughout the manuscript. Guidelines prohibit gender-specific pronouns and stereotyping. Where research involves human subjects or sex/gender-relevant topics, authors must integrate sex/gender analysis or note its absence as a limitation. **ENFORCED** — this is a stated requirement, not merely a recommendation.

### Submission Fee
USD $100 submission fee for new submissions (refundable if accepted). No fee for resubmissions or transfers via Elsevier's article transfer service. **ENFORCED**

### Preprints
Authors may post preprints on SSRN prior to peer review without affecting publication prospects. **GUIDELINE ONLY** (permission, not requirement).

### Double-Blind Review
Manuscripts must be fully anonymized — remove all identifying information from the manuscript and supplementary materials. **ENFORCED**

### Explicit Prohibitions
- Authors may not list AI tools as authors or co-authors.
- Gender-specific pronouns and stereotyping language are prohibited.

## Observed Deltas from FIELD_SKILL

> Only dimensions where this journal deviates from the field baseline.

### Structural Deviations

**Policy implications framing:** FIELD_SKILL treats policy/practice implications as a common-but-optional element of the Discussion/Conclusion. In this journal, explicit policy implications are a near-universal structural expectation — all three spot-checked articles concluded with substantive discussion of implications for regulators, standard-setters, or policymakers. This is driven by the journal's scope (accounting–public policy interface) and should be treated as a required section element rather than optional. **PRACTICE ONLY** (not explicitly mandated in guidelines, but consistently observed and consistent with the journal's stated scope).

### Citation Density and Scope

**Interdisciplinary citation scope:** FIELD_SKILL describes citation density calibrated to an accounting-specialist readership. This journal's articles consistently cite beyond accounting into economics (tax, regulatory), law (FCPA, Wayfair), and occasionally political science or sociology. Introductions position the paper in multiple literatures simultaneously. This is a mild scope expansion, not a density change. Authors writing for this journal should include key non-accounting citations that ground the policy context; reviewers will notice their absence. **PRACTICE ONLY**

### Other Journal-Specific Patterns

**Research design orientation toward policy shocks:** Articles overwhelmingly use exogenous regulatory or legal events (court decisions, enforcement actions, disclosure mandates) as identification sources. This is consistent with FIELD_SKILL's identification norms but the concentration on policy-shock designs is sufficiently pronounced that authors using alternative identification strategies should clearly justify why the design is appropriate for studying the accounting–policy interface. **PRACTICE ONLY**

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Omitting policy implications.** Unlike most accounting journals where practice implications are optional, this journal's scope requires substantive engagement with what findings mean for regulators, policymakers, or standard-setters. A discussion/conclusion that only addresses implications for accounting research will appear incomplete to reviewers.

2. **Forgetting Accounting Letters exists.** If your contribution is narrow and can be stated concisely, the ~3,000-word Accounting Letters track offers faster review and publication. Submitting a full-length paper for what is essentially a short focused finding misses this option.

3. **Missing the highlights requirement.** Elsevier requires 3–5 highlights at submission. These are not the same as the abstract. Omitting them at submission creates avoidable desk-rejection risk.

4. **Gender-specific pronouns in manuscript.** The inclusive language requirement is explicit and enforced. Generic "he/him" constructions or similar defaults will be flagged in review.
