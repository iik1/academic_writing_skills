# Writing Style Skill: International Journal of Production Economics

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** OPS&TECH
- **Publisher:** Elsevier B.V. (Netherlands)
- **Discipline / Scope:** Interdisciplinary — the interface between engineering and management. Covers all aspects of manufacturing and process industries and production in general, including the full product life cycle (research, design, development, test, launch, disposal) and the material flow cycle (supply, production, distribution). Key thematic areas: supply chain management, inventory theory, scheduling, pricing, production planning, digital transformation in operations, sustainability in operations, and operations economics. Economic and financial consequence tracing is a central expectation for all accepted work.
- **Typical article types:** (1) Analytical/game-theoretic modelling papers (highly prevalent — at least as common as empirical papers); (2) quantitative empirical papers (survey-based SEM, econometric panel data); (3) systematic literature reviews; (4) mixed-methods case studies; (5) simulation and heuristic algorithm papers. Review-only papers face a high bar and must demonstrate strong need, rigorous execution, and novel critique.

## Author Guidelines (Hard Rules)

> These are the journal's stated requirements. They take precedence over observed patterns when there is a conflict.

### Manuscript Structure
No single prescribed section sequence is mandated by the guidelines. Articles should be divided into clearly defined and numbered sections. Subsections are numbered 1.1, 1.2, 1.1.1, 1.1.2, etc. Subsections may have a brief heading, which should appear on its own line. The abstract is not included within section numbering. Common practice (not a hard rule): empirical papers follow Introduction → Literature Review → Methodology → Results → Discussion → Conclusions; analytical papers follow Introduction → Model Setup → Analysis → Discussion → Conclusions.

### Length Requirements
Manuscripts should not normally exceed approximately 15 printed journal pages (approximately 10,000 words). Review papers may be permitted to exceed this limit. No per-section word limits are stated in the guidelines. The 10,000-word norm is widely reported and consistent with published article lengths.

### Abstract
- Format: unstructured narrative (no labeled fields such as Background, Methods, Results)
- Word limit: maximum 250 words
- Content: briefly state purpose, principal results, and major conclusions
- Must be self-contained: avoid references; if essential, cite author(s) and year only
- Avoid non-standard or uncommon abbreviations; define any used at first mention
- No section numbering applied to the abstract

### Citations and References
- Style: author-date (Harvard/APA-like) in-text citations
- Single author: (Surname, Year) e.g., (Holt, 2012)
- Two authors: (Surname1 and Surname2, Year)
- Three or more authors: (Surname1 et al., Year)
- Multiple citations: separated by semicolons — (Holt, 2012; Mahowald and Schenck, 2005)
- Reference list format for journal articles: Surname, Initials., Year. Article title. Journal Name Volume, Page range.
- Example: Holt, R., 2012. US election: Politicians should think like scientists. Nature 489, 493–494.
- Web references include URL and access date
- Note: some reference manager templates label this journal as "elsarticle-num" (numbered), but in-text format is author-year. Use author-year.
- Paraphrase is the norm; direct quotation is rare

### Headings
- Sections: numbered (1, 2, 3, ...)
- Subsections: numbered hierarchically (1.1, 1.2, 1.1.1, 1.1.2, ...)
- Three levels of heading (1 / 1.1 / 1.1.1) is standard practice; more levels are uncommon
- Headings appear on their own line; kept brief and descriptive
- Abstract is not numbered

### Tables and Figures
- Follow standard Elsevier formatting conventions
- Figures must be submitted in high resolution; preferred file types: TIFF, EPS, PDF, or MS Office formats
- Graphical abstract (optional but encouraged): minimum 531 x 1328 pixels (h x w), readable at 5 x 13 cm at 96 dpi
- Generative AI must not be used to create or alter figures, images, or graphical abstracts; any AI-assisted artwork requires explicit disclosure in the methods section (including model name, version, and manufacturer)
- All figures and tables must be cited in order in the text

### Language and Tone Requirements
- English (American or British, applied consistently throughout)
- Inclusive language required: avoid bias based on age, gender, race, ethnicity, culture, sexual orientation, or disability; use gender-neutral constructions (plural nouns preferred as default)
- Language should be precise, formal, and academically appropriate
- Elsevier recommends non-native English speakers consider language editing services before submission
- Economic and financial consequences of the studied phenomenon should be traceable in the analysis — this is a core journal expectation, not merely a stylistic preference

### Prohibitions
- AI tools may not be listed as authors
- If AI tools were used in manuscript preparation (text writing, editing, language polishing), an explicit AI disclosure statement must appear before the references section, naming the tool and its version
- Generative AI must not produce or manipulate figures or images (except when the AI tool is itself the subject of research, in which case its use must be fully documented in the methods section)
- Do not submit simultaneously to another journal
- Do not include author names, affiliations, or other identifying information in the manuscript text or figures at submission (double-blind peer review)
- Self-citations that identify the authors must be removed or anonymized at submission
- Systematic reviews that merely describe publication patterns without generating novel critique are unlikely to be sent for review

## Observed Writing Patterns

### Voice and Person
First-person plural ("we") is standard and widely used across both empirical and analytical papers. Passive voice is used selectively, particularly in methods sections when describing procedures ("data were collected," "models were estimated"), but active voice dominates in introductions, contributions statements, and discussions. First-person singular ("I") is rare — most IJPE papers are co-authored and "we" is the assumed default. Impersonal constructions ("this paper," "this study," "the present study") appear as alternatives to "we" in framing sentences, particularly at the start of the introduction and abstract.

### Sentence and Paragraph Style
- Sentences are typically moderate in length (20–35 words), mixing shorter declarative sentences with longer complex sentences
- Paragraphs are medium length (4–8 sentences), with clear topic sentences
- Analytical and modelling papers tend toward more compact, formally structured prose; empirical papers have longer discursive passages
- Each paragraph advances a single main point; transitions are explicit
- Mathematical notation is integrated inline for modelling papers; equations are numbered and referenced in the text
- Numbered lists of contributions are commonly used in the introduction

### Hedging and Epistemic Stance
Hedging is present but moderate — consistent with a field that values both rigor and practical applicability. Common hedging markers:
- "suggest that," "indicate that," "appear to," "may," "tend to"
- "the results suggest," "our findings indicate," "evidence supports"
- Causal language is used where relationships are established empirically or analytically, but authors hedge when generalizing beyond the sample or model
- Analytical papers are somewhat less hedged when presenting proofs or propositions; empirical papers hedge more in the discussion and when extrapolating from specific samples

### Transitional and Cohesive Phrases
- Section openers: "Building on this foundation, ..."; "To address this gap, ..."; "The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: ..."
- Literature-to-gap transitions: "However, ..."; "Despite this progress, ..."; "Nevertheless, limited attention has been paid to ..."; "To the best of our knowledge, ..."
- Results transitions: "The findings reveal that ..."; "Consistent with our hypotheses, ..."; "Contrary to expectations, ..."
- Discussion transitions: "These results are consistent with ..."; "This finding extends ..."; "This suggests that ..."
- Conclusion openers: "This study examined ..."; "The primary contribution of this work is ..."; "Drawing on ..., this paper ..."

### Formality and Register
High formal register throughout. No colloquialisms. Technical and domain-specific terminology used precisely and consistently. Acronyms defined at first mention in both the abstract and the body text (separately). Field-specific vocabulary (supply chain, production economics, operations management, game theory, SEM, optimization) used with precision and without over-explanation. Interdisciplinary reach means authors often briefly define concepts that straddle engineering and management boundaries.

### Guideline vs. Practice Discrepancies
1. **Section structure:** The guidelines say no prescribed section sequence, but in practice empirical papers follow Introduction → Literature Review / Theoretical Background → Methodology → Results → Discussion → Conclusions almost universally; analytical papers follow Introduction → Model Setup → Analysis → Managerial Insights / Discussion → Conclusions.
2. **Word limit:** The guidelines list no formal limit in current text, but the approximately 10,000-word / 15-page norm is real and consistently observed.
3. **Citation style label:** Some reference manager templates call this "elsarticle-num" (numbered), but actual in-text citations are author-year (Harvard). Use author-year.
4. **Analytical modelling paper prevalence:** Empirical papers are not the modal article type. Analytical and game-theoretic modelling papers are equally if not more prevalent in any given issue.

## Section-by-Section Writing Guide

### Abstract
- Length: 150–250 words (aim well under 250)
- Format: single block paragraph, no headings or bullets
- Implicit structure: (1) problem context and motivation, 1–2 sentences; (2) objective or research question, 1 sentence; (3) method / data / approach, 1–2 sentences; (4) key findings, 2–3 sentences; (5) contribution / implication, 1 sentence
- Typical opening: "This paper examines ..."; "This study investigates ..."; "We develop a model to ..."; "We propose a framework for ..."
- Use "we" freely in the abstract
- Avoid references, unexplained acronyms, and jargon that non-specialist readers might not parse
- Do not repeat the title verbatim as the first sentence

### Introduction
- Length: approximately 800–1,500 words
- Follows CARS (Create a Research Space) logic:
  - Move 1 — Establish territory: situate the phenomenon in broader industrial or managerial context; cite recent high-impact work to show the field is active
  - Move 2 — Establish niche: identify a gap or limitation using contrast markers ("However, ..."; "Despite ..."; "To our knowledge, ..."); gap should be specific, not vague
  - Move 3 — Occupy niche: state research objective, approach, and contributions explicitly
- Research objectives stated explicitly, often in a dedicated paragraph or as a numbered list
- Contributions (theoretical and/or practical) are listed explicitly near the end of the introduction, sometimes as bullet points
- End of introduction: brief paper organization paragraph ("The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews ...")
- Citation density: high — 1–3 citations per paragraph is standard
- Tone: formal but accessible; avoid overstating the importance of the problem

### Methods
- Length: approximately 600–1,200 words for empirical papers; longer for analytical models
- Tense: past tense for data collection and procedures ("data were collected," "we distributed questionnaires"); present tense for descriptions of the model or analytical framework ("the model assumes," "the optimization problem is formulated as")
- Subsections typically include: research design / model setup, data collection or data source description, sample characteristics (empirical), variable operationalization or model parameters, estimation method or solution algorithm
- Data sources cited with specific identifiers (database names, survey periods, sample sizes)
- For SEM/survey papers: sample size and response rate reported; common method bias addressed; reliability and validity statistics (Cronbach's alpha, AVE, CR) reported
- For analytical papers: assumptions stated explicitly; model notation defined clearly; propositions or lemmas numbered
- Level of procedural detail is high — sufficient for replication or verification

### Results
- Length: approximately 600–1,200 words
- Tense: past tense for what was found ("results showed," "the coefficient was significant")
- Statistics reported inline: coefficient value, standard error or t-statistic, p-value or confidence interval, e.g., "beta = 0.43, p < 0.01"
- Tables and figures used extensively to present model fit, parameter estimates, or optimization outcomes; not repeated verbatim in prose
- Text highlights key findings and directs reader to tables and figures
- Interpretation: results sections typically report what was found with minimal interpretation; deeper interpretation is reserved for the Discussion section or a combined Results and Discussion section
- For analytical papers: propositions are proven or numerically verified; sensitivity analyses report how results change with parameter values

### Discussion
- When present as a separate section: approximately 600–1,000 words
- Connects results back to prior literature ("consistent with X, we find that ..."; "in contrast to Y, our findings suggest ...")
- Explains unexpected or nuanced results
- Theoretical implications: how findings extend or challenge existing theory
- Managerial and practical implications: explicitly expected in IJPE — managers, practitioners, and policy implications are a core requirement
- Limitations: presented honestly but compactly, typically 2–4 specific limitations
- Future research directions: brief (3–5 suggestions), phrased as open questions ("future research could examine ..."; "future work should explore ...")

### Conclusion
- Length: approximately 400–700 words
- Summarizes main findings concisely (no new results introduced)
- Restates contributions, often mirroring the contribution list in the introduction
- Ends with a forward-looking statement on implications or future directions
- Typical opener: "This study examined ..."; "Drawing on ..., this paper developed ..."; "The purpose of this paper was to ..."
- Does not reproduce abstract verbatim; provides synthesis, not summary
- Limitations and future research may be combined here if no separate Discussion section exists

### References
- Author-date format (Harvard)
- Listed alphabetically by first author surname
- Full article titles included
- Journal name spelled out in full (no abbreviations)
- DOI included where available
- Example journal article: Smith, J., Jones, A., 2023. Supply chain resilience under disruption: A game-theoretic analysis. International Journal of Production Economics 265, 108961. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijpe.2023.108961

## Common Pitfalls
1. **Missing or malformed Highlights.** IJPE requires 3–5 research highlights at submission (Elsevier standard: each bullet must be no longer than 85 characters including spaces). Omitting highlights or exceeding the character limit is likely to trigger a desk-review flag. Write highlights as concise noun phrases summarizing what is new and what was found.
2. **Abstract over 250 words or in a structured format.** The 250-word cap is enforced. Do not use labeled fields (Background:, Methods:, Results:). A single continuous paragraph is required. This catches authors migrating from journals that require structured abstracts.
3. **Insufficient manuscript anonymization for double-blind review.** All author names, affiliations, grant identifiers tied to named institutions, and self-citations phrased as "in our previous study" must be removed or replaced before submission. Failure at this step results in administrative rejection.
4. **Undisclosed or prohibited AI use.** If any AI tool (including large language models for writing assistance or language polishing) was used in preparing the manuscript, a brief AI disclosure statement is mandatory before the references section. AI-generated or AI-manipulated figures are prohibited unless the AI tool itself is the research subject.
5. **Calibrating to the wrong modal article type.** IJPE is unusual in OPS&TECH for the very high prevalence of analytical and game-theoretic modelling papers alongside empirical survey work. Authors should not assume the empirical SEM paper is the reference format. Modelling papers use formal propositions, proofs, and sensitivity analysis; empirical papers should not over-formalize to mimic that style.
6. **Vague or underdeveloped contribution statement.** The guidelines explicitly require an "essential novel scientific contribution" and that economic and financial consequences be traceable in the analysis. A paper without an explicit contribution list in the introduction and without managerial or practical implications will struggle in review.

## Representative Phrase Bank

### Introduction phrases
- **Gap statement:**
  - "However, little is known about how ..."
  - "To the best of our knowledge, no prior study has simultaneously examined ..."
  - "Despite growing interest in X, the relationship between X and Y remains poorly understood."
  - "Prior research has predominantly focused on ..., leaving the role of ... largely unexplored."
  - "While extant literature has addressed ..., scholars have paid limited attention to ..."
- **Objective statement:**
  - "This paper examines the effect of ... on ... using a sample of ..."
  - "We develop an analytical model to characterize optimal ... decisions under ..."
  - "The purpose of this study is to investigate how ... moderates the relationship between ... and ..."
  - "This paper aims to fill this gap by proposing a framework that ..."
  - "Drawing on [theory], this study investigates ..."

### Methods phrases
- **Procedure description:**
  - "We collected data via a structured questionnaire administered to ... in ..."
  - "The model is formulated as a mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) problem, solved using ..."
  - "We estimated the model using partial least squares structural equation modelling (PLS-SEM)."
  - "The sample consists of ... firms drawn from ..., covering the period ... to ..."
  - "To test the robustness of our findings, we conducted ... additional analyses."
- **Data source statement:**
  - "Data were obtained from the [Database Name] database for the period [year–year]."
  - "Primary data were collected through a survey, yielding ... usable responses (response rate: ...%)."
  - "Secondary data on ... were sourced from [official source] and matched with ..."

### Results phrases
- **Statistic inline:**
  - "The coefficient for ... is positive and significant (beta = 0.43, SE = 0.09, p < 0.01), supporting H1."
  - "The optimization model yields a cost reduction of ...% compared to the benchmark."
  - "Model fit statistics indicate acceptable fit: CFI = 0.96, RMSEA = 0.05, SRMR = 0.06."
  - "Proposition 1 establishes that the optimal wholesale price increases with ..."
- **Figure reference:**
  - "Fig. 2 illustrates the relationship between ... and ... across different parameter values."
  - "As shown in Table 3, the standardized path coefficients confirm ..."
  - "The sensitivity analysis results, presented in Fig. 3, reveal that ..."

### Discussion phrases
- **Connecting to literature:**
  - "These findings are consistent with [Author, Year], who argue that ..."
  - "This result extends the work of [Author, Year] by demonstrating that ... even when ..."
  - "In contrast to [Author, Year], we find that ..., which may be explained by ..."
  - "Our results lend support to [theory name], suggesting that ..."
- **Limitation framing:**
  - "This study is not without limitations. First, the cross-sectional design precludes causal inference; future research employing longitudinal data would strengthen these conclusions."
  - "The sample is limited to ... industry in ..., which may affect the generalizability of our findings to other contexts."
  - "Our model assumes ..., which may not hold in all practical settings."

### Conclusion phrases
- **Summary opener:**
  - "This study examined the relationship between ... and ..., using a sample of ... and employing ..."
  - "Drawing on [theory], this paper developed an analytical model to characterize ..."
  - "The primary objective of this research was to investigate ... We find that ..."
- **Future direction:**
  - "Future research could extend this work by examining ... in the context of ..."
  - "We encourage scholars to explore the moderating role of ... using longitudinal designs."
  - "An important avenue for future research is to incorporate ... dynamics into the model."
