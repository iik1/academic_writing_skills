# Journal Writing Skill: Journal of Economic Literature

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 4
- **Field:** ECON
- **Publisher:** American Economic Association
- **Scope:** Commissioned survey articles and review essays synthesising recent research across all economics fields for a broad professional audience; also publishes book reviews and an annual dissertation index. Does NOT publish original empirical research papers.
- **Guidelines URL:** https://www.aeaweb.org/journals/jel/submissions/guidelines
- **Style Guide URL:** https://www.aeaweb.org/journals/jel/style-guide
- **Field baseline:** econ-field-writing-style
- **Articles spot-checked:** 2 (Currie, MacLeod, and Musen 2026, DOI 10.1257/jel.20251762; Eggertsson and Egiev 2025, DOI 10.1257/jel.20241306 — abstract and metadata only; full text inaccessible; structural inferences drawn from abstracts and page-range data)

## Hard Constraints (Author Guidelines)

> These override FIELD_SKILL where they conflict.

### Structure

JEL does not publish original empirical or theory papers. All full-length submissions are survey articles or analytic review essays synthesising existing literature. The editorial criteria require four elements in every article:

1. Addresses "why we should care" about the surveyed topic
2. Accessible exposition communicable in plain English without sacrificing rigour
3. Selective rather than exhaustive literature coverage
4. Synthesis that produces clear statements of important unanswered questions

No canonical section order is prescribed. Sections are organised around subtopics of the survey's territory, not around the empirical research architecture (Data → Empirical Strategy → Results) described in FIELD_SKILL. **Label: ENFORCED**

### Length

No upper page limit is stated in the accessible guidelines or style guide for completed articles. Observed published articles range from approximately 40 to 130+ typeset pages (e.g., a 127-page article appeared in December 2025). This substantially exceeds the AER norm of ~35–36 typeset pages. Proposals are limited to approximately 10 pages excluding references. **Label: PRACTICE ONLY (for article length range); ENFORCED (for proposal)**

### Abstract

Two-tier limit: maximum 150 words for survey articles; maximum 100 words for review articles. FIELD_SKILL does not specify a word limit; this is a binding journal constraint. **Label: ENFORCED**

### Citations

Chicago Manual of Style author-date format. Text citations: list all names for 1–4 authors; first author + "et al." for 5+. Reference list: list all names for 1–10 authors; first seven + "et al." for 11+. Data sources and registered studies must appear in the reference list. This is consistent with FIELD_SKILL's author-year norm but adds the reference-list et al. threshold explicitly. **Label: ENFORCED**

### Headings

Roman numerals (I., II., III.) for major section headings; capital letters (A., B., C.) for first-level subsections. Arabic numerals are not acceptable. FIELD_SKILL states "either is acceptable"; JEL requires Roman numerals. **Label: ENFORCED**

### Tables and Figures

- No asterisks to denote statistical significance. Standard errors must be reported in parentheses instead. This prohibition is not mentioned in FIELD_SKILL and overrides any implied practice of asterisk notation. **Label: ENFORCED**
- Portrait orientation only; maximum 9 columns; horizontal lines only; no shading.
- Zero must precede decimal (0.357, not .357).
- Footnote keys: lowercase letters (a, b, c); no asterisks.
- Figures must be in vector format (PDF, EPS, AI, WMF, or PPT); photographs at minimum 300 dpi.

### Explicit Prohibitions

- No asterisks for significance in tables or text (use standard errors in parentheses).
- No endnotes: all notes must appear as footnotes at the bottom of the relevant page.
- AI software may not be listed as an author; use must be disclosed at submission.
- No title page; title and byline appear at the top of page one.

## Observed Deltas from FIELD_SKILL

### Article Type and Purpose

**FIELD_SKILL describes:** Empirical papers reporting original research findings, or theory papers deriving new results.

**JEL requires:** Survey and review essays synthesising existing literature. There are no "results" sections in the FIELD_SKILL sense. The article's core contribution is analytical synthesis, not discovery. This is a fundamental scope mismatch: writing a paper "for JEL" means writing a literature survey, not a research paper. **Label: ENFORCED**

### Structure

**FIELD_SKILL describes:** Introduction → Background → Data → Empirical Strategy → Results → Robustness → Conclusion (or theory analogue).

**JEL instead:** Introduction framing the survey territory → multiple thematic or subtopic sections synthesising bodies of work → a closing section that draws implications and identifies open questions. There is no Data section, no Empirical Strategy section, and no Results section in the traditional sense. The closing section is expected to include explicit statements of important unanswered questions; this is a formal deliverable, not a brief gesture. **Label: ENFORCED**

### Introduction Moves

**FIELD_SKILL describes:** Motivating hook → research question → approach/data → preview of main results → contributions → roadmap.

**JEL instead:** The introduction frames the survey territory (not a research question), justifies why a survey is needed now, describes the organisational logic of the article, previews key themes or findings from the synthesised literature, and ends with a roadmap paragraph. The "contributions" framing of FIELD_SKILL is largely absent; the survey's value is its synthesis, not enumerated bullet contributions. Results are not "announced" because there are no original results; instead, key patterns or conclusions from the surveyed literature are previewed. **Label: PRACTICE ONLY** (inferred from guidelines and article abstracts; full text not confirmed)

### Voice and Register

**FIELD_SKILL describes:** Technical vocabulary density is high throughout; readers assumed to be trained economists; discipline-specific terminology used without definition.

**JEL requires:** Accessible exposition "without sacrificing rigour," communicated in "plain English." The intended audience is "professional economists but not just those working in the subject area." This explicitly requires cross-subfield accessibility that FIELD_SKILL does not mandate. Jargon specific to a sub-field should be defined or contextualised; the survey must be readable by a macroeconomist reading a survey of, say, industrial organisation. **Label: ENFORCED**

### Citation Density

**FIELD_SKILL describes:** High in introduction, medium in methods, low to medium in results.

**JEL instead:** Citation density is uniformly very high throughout all substantive sections. Surveying and citing the relevant literature is the primary activity of every section, not a contextualising activity concentrated in the introduction. **Label: PRACTICE ONLY** (consistent with survey article function; inferred from article metadata and abstracts)

### Statistical Reporting

**FIELD_SKILL:** Does not address asterisk conventions.

**JEL:** Asterisks for statistical significance are prohibited in all tables and figures. Standard errors must appear in parentheses. This applies whether the article is reporting its own (limited) empirical work or reproducing results from surveyed literature. **Label: ENFORCED**

## Dimensions With No Delta

- Voice and person: FIELD DEFAULT (first-person plural "we" is standard)
- Citation format: FIELD DEFAULT (Chicago author-date, consistent with FIELD_SKILL)
- Active voice preference: FIELD DEFAULT
- Hedging norms for external validity and mechanism claims: FIELD DEFAULT
- Roadmap paragraph: FIELD DEFAULT (near-universal in JEL introductions, consistent with FIELD_SKILL)
- Paraphrase over direct quotation: FIELD DEFAULT
- Conclusion relative length: FIELD DEFAULT (shorter than introduction)

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Submitting an empirical paper.** JEL does not publish original empirical research. A paper reporting new estimates, experiments, or theoretical derivations will be rejected regardless of quality. The submission must be a literature survey or review essay.

2. **Specialist register.** Writing as if the reader is a specialist in the sub-field violates JEL's explicit accessibility requirement. Cross-subfield readers must be able to follow the argument; sub-field jargon must be introduced.

3. **Asterisks in tables.** The AEA prohibition on significance asterisks applies to all JEL tables. Submitting with asterisk notation will require revision before acceptance.

4. **Exhaustive rather than selective citation.** JEL explicitly requires selective literature coverage; a survey that attempts to cite every paper in the area violates the editorial standard. The value is synthesis and organisation, not comprehensiveness. Curate and analyse; do not catalogue.

5. **Omitting open questions.** Identifying important unanswered questions is a formal editorial criterion, not a conventional closing gesture. A conclusion that lacks substantive statements of research gaps does not satisfy JEL's requirements.
