# Field Writing Style: ACCOUNT

## Scope and Intended Use

This baseline encodes shared writing conventions observed across ABS 4* journals in the ACCOUNT field (The Accounting Review, Accounting Organizations and Society, Journal of Accounting and Economics, Journal of Accounting Research). Where ABS 4 journals were consulted (Contemporary Accounting Research, Review of Accounting Studies), they confirmed the same norms without systematic deviation.

This baseline covers: canonical article structure, voice and register, argumentation patterns, methods presentation, results reporting, citation practices, and hedging conventions. It applies to the dominant quantitative/archival/experimental strand of accounting research. Where Accounting, Organizations and Society (AOS) departs from field-wide norms through its qualitative and interpretive tradition, those departures are noted explicitly.

This baseline does NOT cover: journal-specific formatting rules, word or page limits, submission requirements, publisher-specific conventions, or statistical reporting thresholds that differ by journal. Journal-specific skills take precedence whenever they conflict with this baseline.

---

## Typical Article Structure

At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect a canonical section set that is stable across archival, experimental, survey, and analytical papers. The dominant quantitative tradition follows this structure closely; AOS qualitative/interpretive papers deviate as described below.

**Near-universal sections (present in virtually all articles):**

1. **Abstract** — Short, structured summary. States the research question, primary method or data, key findings, and main contribution in a single paragraph. Short enough to be read in under a minute. Keywords and JEL codes appear as separate fields.

2. **Introduction** — The most critical section. Always present; always first; always given an explicit heading. Contains the research question, motivation, method preview, results preview, contribution statement, and literature positioning. See Argumentation section for full structure. Introduction ends with an optional roadmap sentence.

3. **Hypothesis Development / Theory / Background** — Near-universal in empirical and experimental papers. Reviews prior literature, develops the theoretical rationale, and states formal hypotheses. Often the longest section after Results. May include an "Institutional Background" subsection when the setting is specialized. In analytical and review articles, this section frames the conceptual contribution instead of stating hypotheses.

4. **Data and Research Design / Methods** — Describes sample construction, variable definitions, empirical model specification, and estimation approach. Includes a descriptive statistics table. In experimental papers, describes the experimental task, subject pool, and procedure.

5. **Results / Empirical Analysis** — Presents the main findings. Organized around hypotheses or research questions. Subsections divide results into main tests, cross-sectional variation, and mechanism tests. Nearly always followed by or includes additional analyses and robustness checks.

6. **Additional Analysis / Supplemental Analysis / Robustness** — Common but not always a standalone section; may be embedded as subsections within the Results section. Documents sensitivity of findings to alternative specifications, samples, or variable constructions.

7. **Discussion and Conclusion** — Often merged into a single section. Restates key findings, states contributions, frames limitations, and sometimes suggests future research. Occasionally Discussion and Conclusion are separate sections but the merged form is more common.

8. **References** — Always present; follows text.

9. **Tables and Figures** — Placed after references or at end of manuscript. Tables carry panel labels, variable definitions in notes, and explicit reporting of standard-error clustering or other statistical choices.

10. **Appendix** — Present in many papers; holds extended variable definitions, robustness tests, proofs, or experimental instruments.

**Common but not universal:**

- **Standalone Robustness section** — Common in longer archival papers; absent in shorter or more focused work.
- **Formal model / Analytical section** — Present in theoretical and structural papers.
- **Roadmap paragraph** — Frequently but not universally included at the end of the introduction.

**Section numbering:** Practice varies across journals. The Accounting Review and other AAA journals use Roman numerals with ALL-CAPS headings for Level 1 (e.g., I. INTRODUCTION, II. HYPOTHESIS DEVELOPMENT). JAE and JAR papers commonly use Arabic numerals (1. Introduction, 2. Data, etc.) with title-case headings. Subsection numbering (I.A or 2.1 style) follows the same journal convention. Authors should follow the target journal's style guide; this baseline records the field norm, not a single format.

**AOS qualitative/interpretive papers** — A significant minority of AOS articles follow an interpretive, critical, or ethnographic tradition. These papers replace the hypothesis-testing structure with a more discursive organization: extensive theoretical framing (often drawing on social theory — Foucault, Bourdieu, Bhabha, institutional theory), a Methodology/Research Context section describing fieldwork or case selection, and Findings or Themes sections that present evidence thematically rather than by hypothesis. The Discussion and Conclusion section interprets findings through the theoretical lens. This strand is heterodox relative to the field's quantitative mainstream, and norms in this tradition are not fully captured by the structure above.

## Voice, Person, and Register

At the ABS 4*/4 level, reviewers expect:

- **Person:** First-person plural ("we") is the dominant convention regardless of author count. Standard verbs: "we find," "we document," "we examine," "we test," "we argue," "we hypothesize," "we predict," "we contribute," "we investigate," "we suggest," "we add to." First-person singular ("I") is used by single-authored papers in some sub-traditions (particularly AOS interpretive work) but "we" is expected in most contexts. Impersonal constructions ("this paper shows…," "the evidence indicates…") appear but are not the primary register.

- **Active voice:** Active constructions dominate throughout. Passive voice is reserved for procedural descriptions where the subject is truly irrelevant ("standard errors are clustered at the firm level," "responses were collected via online survey").

- **Formality:** High throughout. No colloquialisms, no rhetorical questions as structural devices, no casual transitions. Accounting journals tend toward a clinical, precise register in empirical work.

- **Confidence calibration:** Findings that are well-identified warrant direct declarative claims. Mechanistic interpretations and implications warrant measured language ("our results suggest," "consistent with," "indicate that"). Over-hedging weakens the contribution; overconfidence on speculative claims is not acceptable.

- **AOS interpretive tradition:** Voice is somewhat more reflective and the author's position is sometimes acknowledged explicitly, particularly in fieldwork-based papers. The register remains formal but may be more discursive.

## Sentence and Paragraph Style

- **Sentence length:** Medium to long. Accounting writing uses complex sentences with embedded clauses and multi-part statements, particularly in the introduction and hypothesis development. Short sentences appear for emphasis following a complex chain of reasoning, but are not the default mode.

- **Paragraph density:** Dense, particularly in the introduction, hypothesis development, and results sections. Results paragraphs typically span a full analysis unit (one hypothesis, one table panel, or one mechanism test). Conclusion paragraphs are more compact.

- **Technical density:** High. Regression model equations, statistical notation, and variable definitions are written out fully. The expected reader is a specialist in accounting research, not a general business audience.

- **AOS qualitative papers** tend toward longer paragraphs with more analytical prose and less tabular interruption; the writing integrates theory and evidence within extended paragraphs.

## Argumentation and Rhetorical Moves

**Introduction structure (consistent across all four ABS 4* journals):**

The introduction follows a recognizable pattern, broadly consistent with the CARS model:

1. **Establish significance of the phenomenon.** The opening move motivates the research question by asserting that the phenomenon matters — economically, institutionally, or for financial reporting practice. Prior literature is cited immediately to anchor the claim.

2. **Survey the gap.** Prior work is acknowledged and then bounded. The gap is framed as a specific unanswered question, an identification challenge in prior studies, or an unexplored setting. Common framing: "However, we know little about…," "Prior work has not examined…," "Existing studies face challenges in establishing causality because…"

3. **Describe the study.** A concise statement of what the paper does: data source, research design, and identification strategy (if archival). In experimental papers: the experimental task and key manipulation. The description is precise but not exhaustive.

4. **Enumerate key results.** Results are previewed explicitly, often in a numbered list ("First, we find… Second, we document… Third, our results indicate…"). This enumeration signals the paper's scope and the density of evidence.

5. **State contributions.** A paragraph (sometimes two) explicitly positions the paper in the literature. "This study contributes to [strand] in several ways. First,… Second,… Finally,…" Contributions are framed relative to specific prior papers cited by name.

6. **Optional roadmap.** A closing sentence maps the paper's section structure.

**Gap framing:** Reviewers expect the gap to be stated clearly and specifically. Vague claims that "little is known" without citation of the nearest prior work are not sufficient. The standard move is: "While [prior work, cited] has shown X, we do not know Y, because [reason]."

**Contribution positioning:** Contributions are always stated explicitly. Leaving the contribution implicit is not acceptable at the ABS 4*/4 level. Contributions are typically framed as: (a) new empirical evidence on an open question, (b) a novel research design that overcomes prior identification problems, (c) new data or measurement that expands what can be studied, (d) new theoretical insight, or (e) implications for practice or standard-setting.

## Methods Conventions

- **Tense:** Past tense throughout the methods section. Sample construction, variable construction, and estimation procedures are all written in past tense ("we required firms to have…," "we estimated the model using…").

- **Procedural detail:** High. Sample construction describes each inclusion/exclusion criterion and its rationale. Variable definitions are precise and typically include the data source and year range. Regression specifications are written out as numbered equations. Estimation approach specifies the estimator, standard-error clustering, and any fixed effects.

- **Identification strategy:** In archival papers, the identification strategy is a first-order concern. Authors explain what identifies causal variation, describe any natural experiment or instrumental variable, and discuss threats to identification. Language distinguishing association from causality is calibrated carefully: strong causal claims require explicit identification via exogenous variation; descriptive results use correlational language.

- **Robustness:** Expected. The baseline norm is to report at least three to five robustness checks: alternative variable definitions, alternative samples, placebo tests, and (where applicable) alternative estimation approaches. Robustness may appear in the main results section, a dedicated robustness section, or the appendix.

- **Ethics and data statements:** Required in many journals; typically placed at the end of the manuscript or in the methods section. IRB approval is standard for experimental papers. Data availability statements are increasingly required.

- **Descriptive statistics:** A dedicated table of summary statistics and correlation matrix is near-universal. The table typically appears immediately before or within the methods section.

## Results and Reporting Norms

- **Tables vs. prose balance:** Tables are the primary vehicle for communicating quantitative results. Prose interprets and narrates; it does not replicate all table content. The standard pattern: briefly describe the specification ("Table X presents OLS regressions of Y on X"), state the key finding ("We find that [variable] is positively associated with [outcome], with a coefficient of [value] and a t-statistic of [value]"), and then interpret ("This finding suggests that…").

- **Statistical narration:** Precise. Coefficient estimates and statistical significance are reported in prose when discussing main results. Standard practice references the coefficient and p-value or t-statistic in the sentence or parenthetically. Statistical significance thresholds (1%, 5%, 10%) are reported consistently throughout. Economic significance (magnitude relative to mean, standard deviation, or comparable benchmark) is increasingly expected alongside statistical significance.

- **Reporting structure:** Results are organized by hypothesis or question, in the same order as the hypothesis development section. This parallel structure is a strong field norm.

- **Null results:** Not suppressed. Null findings on secondary hypotheses or mechanism tests are reported straightforwardly. Where the null finding is theoretically meaningful, it is discussed. Selective reporting is a serious concern in this literature and journals expect transparency.

- **AOS interpretive papers:** Reporting is narrative. Evidence is presented as extended quotations from fieldwork transcripts, archival documents, or observation notes. These quotations are the primary evidence; tables are absent or minor. The author interprets evidence through the theoretical frame developed earlier.

## Discussion and Conclusion Norms

- **Merged vs. separate:** Discussion and Conclusion are typically merged into a single final section. A standalone Discussion section (distinct from Conclusion) is less common in accounting than in some other social science fields, though it does appear in experimental papers.

- **Content of the Discussion and Conclusion:**
  - Brief restatement of the research question and approach
  - Summary of key findings, typically in the same order as the results
  - Explicit contributions (often restated from the introduction in condensed form)
  - Explicit limitations paragraph — this is a strong field norm across all journals. Limitations are acknowledged directly and typically include: cross-sectional design precluding causal inference (for survey/archival studies without identification), potential construct validity concerns, sample selection, and generalizability bounds. Understatement of limitations is noticed negatively by reviewers.
  - Future research directions — common but not universal; typically one or two sentences

- **Tone:** The conclusion is confident but not overreaching. Findings from the study are stated as findings; broader claims about practice or policy are presented as implications that "suggest" or "may inform" rather than as proven conclusions.

## Citation and Literature Engagement

- **Format:** Author-date throughout. In-text citations appear as "(Author Year)" or "Author (Year)" depending on grammatical role. Multiple citations in a single parenthetical are separated by semicolons, in alphabetical or chronological order depending on house style.

- **Citation density by section:**
  - *Introduction*: High — virtually every substantive claim about prior findings is supported by citation. The introduction may cite 15–40 works.
  - *Hypothesis Development/Theory*: High — each hypothesis is grounded in prior literature and theory, all of which are cited.
  - *Methods*: Medium — established methods are cited; novel data or constructions are described rather than cited. Statistical methods are cited when non-standard.
  - *Results*: Low to medium — citations appear primarily when interpreting findings relative to prior work.
  - *Conclusion*: Medium — prior work is re-cited when positioning contributions.

- **Paraphrase vs. quotation:** Paraphrase is the universal norm. Direct quotation from prior scholarly literature is essentially absent in the accounting mainstream (including quantitative AOS papers). Authors paraphrase and cite. The only exception is fieldwork-based AOS papers, where direct quotation of interview and observation material constitutes the primary evidence.

- **Disagreement with prior work:** Framed diplomatically. Standard constructions: "Our findings differ from [prior study], potentially because…," "Unlike [prior study], we find…, which may reflect differences in [setting/sample/period]." Open methodological criticism of prior work is uncommon and requires careful hedging; it is more common to note that prior designs "faced challenges" or that the present study "offers cleaner identification."

---

## Hedging and Epistemic Caution

- **Overall level:** Medium. Accounting research at the top tier calibrates hedging to the identification strength of the claim. Well-identified results are stated with direct verbs ("we find," "we document"). Mechanistic interpretations and implications use softened language ("suggest," "indicate," "consistent with," "appear to").

- **Causal claims:** Reserved for studies with exogenous identification. The standard locution for a well-identified quasi-experiment is "we conclude that [relationship] is likely causal" or "[intervention] causes [outcome]." Without credible identification, the language reverts to "is associated with" or "predicts."

- **Limitations acknowledgment:** Expected and standard — not a sign of weakness. Reviewers expect authors to name specific threats honestly; formulaic disclaimers not specific to the paper's design are not valued.

- **Common hedging locutions:** "our results suggest," "consistent with," "our evidence is consistent with the interpretation that," "we interpret this as evidence that," "may reflect," "likely driven by," "appears to be," "one possible explanation is."

- **Contexts where hedging is NOT used:** Direct reporting of regression coefficients, t-statistics, and significance levels is stated without hedging. Methodological claims about what the research design achieves are stated directly. The contribution statement in the introduction is declarative, not tentative.

## What This Baseline Does NOT Specify

- Word limits, page limits, or abstract word counts for any specific journal
- Journal-specific section naming conventions or heading formats (Roman vs. Arabic numerals, ALL CAPS vs. title case)
- Publisher formatting requirements (font, spacing, margin)
- Submission system requirements or cover letter conventions
- Specific statistical reporting conventions that vary by journal (e.g., whether to report t-statistics vs. p-values vs. standard errors in tables)
- Accepted reference formatting details beyond author-date in-text style
- Open science requirements (pre-registration, data sharing, replication code) that are evolving and journal-specific
- Editorial policies specific to individual journals (e.g., double-blind review, word length caps)
- AOS-specific requirements for interpretive and qualitative work beyond the notes embedded above
