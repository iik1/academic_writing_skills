## Deviations from Field Baseline

Every item below records something IJFE does differently from the `finance-field-writing-style` baseline. Items matching the baseline exactly are listed under **Field Defaults** only.

---

### 1. Standalone Theory and Literature Sections Before Methodology [RELAXATION]

**Field baseline**: "A standalone Literature Review section is essentially never used." Prior literature is woven into the introduction or combined with motivation; a separately numbered section titled "Literature Review" signals lower-tier work.

**IJFE practice**: Both articles dedicate one or two full major numbered sections to theory and literature review, positioned between the introduction and the methodology section:

- Article 1: Section 2 "Review of literature" with three subsections (2.1, 2.2, 2.3), each concluding with a hypothesis statement
- Article 2: Section 2 "Theoretical framework" (agency theory, stakeholder theory, resource-based theory as subsections) + Section 3 "Empirical literature review and hypotheses development" (four subsections, each ending with a hypothesis statement)

**Classification**: RELAXATION — IJFE explicitly relaxes the ABS 4*/4 norm. Omitting a dedicated theory/literature section may signal unfamiliarity with the journal's conventions.

**Implication for writing**: Budget approximately 20–30% of total word count for Sections 2 (and optionally 3). Section 2 should name the theoretical frameworks explicitly (agency theory, signaling theory, etc.) as subsections. The literature review subsections should culminate in formal hypothesis statements (see Delta 2 below). Do not compress this material into the introduction.

---

### 2. Formal, Numbered Hypothesis Statements as Standalone Typographic Elements [PRACTICE ONLY]

**Field baseline**: Hypotheses are typically embedded in prose as directional predictions. Numbered hypothesis labels as distinct formatted elements are not standard at ABS 4*/4.

**IJFE practice**: Both articles present every testable prediction as a labeled, formatted hypothesis statement:

- Labels: **H1**, **H2**, **H3**, **H4** (sequential across the entire paper)
- Typography: bold label, italic or bold-italic hypothesis text
- Position: final sentence(s) of the literature review subsection that motivates each prediction
- Form: directional positive/negative association statement (not null-hypothesis form)

Illustrative pattern:
> **H1**: *Age diversity of audit committee financial experts is negatively associated with earnings management.*

**Classification**: PRACTICE ONLY — consistent across both articles; no guideline text available to confirm enforcement.

**Implication for writing**: Number every testable prediction with an Hx label. Do not fold hypotheses into flowing prose. State hypotheses in the direction predicted (e.g., "positively associated with") rather than as null hypotheses. Hypotheses should be mutually exclusive, each emerging from its own subsection of the literature review.

---

### 3. JEL Codes Absent from the Manuscript [PRACTICE ONLY]

**Field baseline**: "JEL classification codes and keywords appear as separate fields on the published page." At ABS 4*/4, JEL codes typically appear in the manuscript immediately after the abstract.

**IJFE practice**: Neither article includes JEL codes anywhere in the manuscript. Only a "Keywords" line follows the abstract (four to seven terms as a comma-separated list).

**Classification**: PRACTICE ONLY — consistent across both articles. JEL codes are likely assigned editorially post-acceptance rather than required in the submitted manuscript.

**Implication for writing**: Include only a Keywords line after the abstract. Do not add a JEL codes line in the submission manuscript.

---

### 4. In-Text Placeholder for Tables; Tables Collected at End [PRACTICE ONLY]

**Field baseline**: Table placement conventions vary across journals; FIELD_SKILL does not specify.

**IJFE practice**: Both articles insert a bracketed placeholder instruction in the text at the point of first mention, with all tables collected after the References (or in Appendices):

- Article 1: `[Insert Table X]` in bold brackets
- Article 2: `[Please insert Table X here]` in bold brackets

Tables are not embedded inline in the running text of the manuscript body.

**Classification**: PRACTICE ONLY — consistent across both articles.

**Implication for writing**: At first mention of each table in the text (e.g., "...as shown in Table 3"), insert a line break and the placeholder `[Insert Table 3]`. Collect all tables after the References section (or as appendices). Do not embed tables in the body.

---

### 5. Acknowledgements Section Naming Editor by Name [PRACTICE ONLY]

**Field baseline**: Acknowledgements, when present, are brief and positioned before References. The norm at ABS 4*/4 is a single short paragraph without naming the handling editor.

**IJFE practice**: Article 2 includes a full Acknowledgements section that names the Editor (Keith Pilbeam) and Guest Editors by title and full name and explicitly thanks them for handling the paper:

> "We would like to thank the Editor Professor Keith Pilbeam and the Guest Editors…"

**Classification**: PRACTICE ONLY — observed in one of two articles; likely common practice.

**Implication for writing**: Include an Acknowledgements section. If the paper went through the normal editorial process, naming the handling editor by name is standard and positively received at this journal. Position the section after the Conclusion and before the References.

---

### 6. Appendix for Variable Definitions [PRACTICE ONLY]

**Field baseline**: Variable definitions may appear in table notes, the text, or a data section appendix — FIELD_SKILL does not specify placement.

**IJFE practice**: Article 1 uses a dedicated "Appendix 1: Variable definitions" table positioned after the References, with columns for Variable Name, Abbreviation/Label, Measurement definition, and Data Source. This keeps the methodology section clean of long definitional lists.

**Classification**: PRACTICE ONLY.

**Implication for writing**: For empirical papers with more than approximately eight variables, create a standalone Appendix table for variable definitions. Reference it in the data/variables subsection of the methodology section rather than reproducing definitions inline.

## Composite Structure Template

The canonical IJFE empirical paper structure observed across both articles:

```
Abstract  (~150–250 words, unstructured prose)
Keywords  (4–7 terms, comma-separated; NO JEL codes)

1. Introduction

2. [Theoretical Framework]
   2.1  [Theory A — e.g., Agency Theory]
   2.2  [Theory B — e.g., Stakeholder Theory]
   2.3  [Theory C — e.g., Resource-Based Theory]

3. [Empirical Literature Review and Hypotheses Development]
   3.1  [Topic / Predictor 1]  →  H1: [directional statement in bold/italic]
   3.2  [Topic / Predictor 2]  →  H2: [directional statement in bold/italic]
   3.3  [Topic / Predictor 3]  →  H3: [directional statement in bold/italic]
   3.4  [Topic / Predictor 4]  →  H4: [directional statement in bold/italic]

   NOTE: Sections 2 and 3 may be merged into a single "Review of literature"
   section when theory and hypothesis development are tightly integrated
   (as in Article 1).

4. Research Methodology
   4.1  Data and Sample Selection
   4.2  Variable Measurement
   4.3  Model Specification / Econometric Approach

5. Empirical Results [and Discussion]
   5.1  Descriptive Statistics
   5.2  Main Regression Results
   5.3  Robustness Checks / Additional Analysis

6. Conclusion[s]

Acknowledgements
  [Name handling editor and any guest editors explicitly]

Data Availability Statement

References
  [APA style; journal names in full]

Appendix 1: Variable Definitions  [table with Name / Label / Measurement / Source]

Table 1
Table 2
…
```

## Hard Constraints

> **Caveat**: Author guidelines were not accessible (Wiley HTTP 403). All items below derive from article observation and are classified PRACTICE ONLY pending manual verification of the guidelines.

- Abstract must be unstructured prose; do not use structured abstract format (Objective / Methods / Results / Conclusions headings)
- Keywords line required after abstract; JEL codes are absent from the manuscript body
- A dedicated theory/literature section (or sections) is expected before the methodology; do not fold all prior literature into the introduction
- Every testable prediction must appear as a labeled Hx hypothesis statement in bold or italics at the end of its motivating subsection
- Table placeholders ("**[Insert Table X]**") must appear in-text at first mention; tables collected at end of manuscript after References
- Include an Acknowledgements section; naming the editor by name is conventional
- Include a Data Availability Statement after the Conclusion
- Use first-person plural voice throughout
