# Academic Writing Conventions: Marketing (MKT)

## 1. Macro Structure

### Standard article structure
Marketing articles follow one of three structural templates depending on methodology:

**Behavioral / experimental (consumer research)**: Abstract → Introduction → Theoretical Background / Conceptual Framework → Study 1 (Methods → Results) → Study 2 (Methods → Results) → … → General Discussion → Conclusion

**Quantitative empirical / econometric**: Abstract → Introduction → Background / Literature Review → Data → Model / Empirical Strategy → Results → Discussion → Conclusion

**Conceptual / review**: Abstract → Introduction → [Thematically organized conceptual sections] → Discussion / Research Agenda → Conclusion

### Section presence and labeling
- Behavioral papers organize empirical content into individually labeled studies (Study 1, Study 2, etc.) rather than single Methods and Results sections. Each study has its own Methods and Results subsections.
- Quantitative papers use a continuous section structure analogous to economics: separate Data, Model, and Results sections are standard.
- The pre-empirical section in behavioral work is labeled "Theoretical Background," "Conceptual Framework," or "Theory and Hypotheses." It develops the theory and closes with explicitly stated, numbered hypotheses.
- "General Discussion" follows all studies in multi-study papers; it synthesizes across studies rather than repeating study-level results.
- Acknowledgments, conflict-of-interest disclosures, data availability statements, and open-science links (OSF, AsPredicted) appear at the end.

### Abstract
- Unstructured; typically 150–250 words.
- States the research question or phenomenon, briefly describes the method or overall study design, summarizes the main finding, and identifies the theoretical and/or managerial contribution.
- No citations in abstract.
- Behavioral papers often open with a motivating observation or phenomenon before stating the research question.

## 2. Introduction

### CARS structure (Create-A-Research-Space)
Marketing introductions follow the CARS model consistently across both behavioral and quantitative work:

1. **Establish territory**: Open by identifying the phenomenon, market context, or theoretical domain. Cite foundational and recent work to establish that the territory is active and consequential.
2. **Establish niche**: Identify a gap in knowledge, a theoretical tension, an empirical puzzle, or a real-world problem that existing work has not resolved. State explicitly what remains unknown or contested.
3. **Occupy niche**: State the research question or objective, preview the overall approach (experimental design, empirical strategy, qualitative method), enumerate the contributions, and outline the paper structure.

### Research question
- Stated explicitly, usually in the second half of the introduction.
- Often framed as a direct question ("We ask: …") or a statement of purpose ("We examine how…", "We investigate whether…", "We propose that…").
- Behavioral papers frequently frame the question around a psychological mechanism or boundary condition.
- Quantitative papers frame the question as an identification or estimation problem.

### Contribution statement
- Contributions are announced explicitly in the introduction, typically as a numbered list or as distinct sentences beginning "First, we contribute…", "Second,…", "Third,…"
- Two to four contributions is the norm.
- Contributions are framed with reference to specific literatures ("We extend the literature on mental accounting…"; "We contribute to research on customer lifetime value…"; "Our findings speak to debates about…").
- Methodological contributions (a new measure, a novel identification strategy, a new dataset) are named explicitly alongside theoretical contributions.

### Length
- Introductions typically run 600–1,200 words (roughly 3–5 paragraphs).
- Literature review is not conducted in the introduction; citations appear to establish territory and gap, with full theoretical engagement reserved for the Theoretical Background or Literature Review section.

## 3. Voice and Person

### Pronoun usage
- First-person plural ("we") is the default for all co-authored work throughout the paper—theory, methods, results, discussion, and contribution claims. This applies whether the paper has two or six authors.
- Passive voice is used selectively: for describing stimulus materials, survey administration, or data collection procedures where the agent is unimportant. Active constructions dominate.
- Single-authored work also tends to use "we" in behavioral journals; first-person singular appears more often in methodological or quantitative papers.

### Tone
- Academic but direct. Theoretical claims are advanced with confidence ("We propose that…", "We argue that…", "We predict that…").
- Results are reported factually and assertively.
- Hedging is used for interpretive inferences and limitation acknowledgments, not for main claims.

## 4. Argumentation

### Logical architecture
- Behavioral papers build a causal chain: psychological mechanism → predicted effect → boundary conditions (moderators). Each hypothesis follows logically from the preceding theoretical reasoning.
- Hypotheses are stated as directional predictions and labeled sequentially (H1, H1a, H1b, H2, etc.); interaction or moderation hypotheses are numbered distinctly from main-effect hypotheses.
- Quantitative papers frame the argument as an empirical identification problem: What is the causal quantity of interest? What variation identifies it? What are the key threats to identification and how are they addressed?
- Conceptual papers develop propositions (P1, P2…) rather than testable hypotheses; logical argument and illustrative examples substitute for statistical tests.

### Hypothesis form
- Behavioral: "H1: [Directional prediction about Y as a function of X]." Full sentences, active voice.
- The mechanism linking X to Y is stated before the hypothesis; the hypothesis is a conclusion of the theoretical argument, not its starting point.
- Moderation hypotheses explicitly state the direction of the interactive effect.

### Use of theory
- Named psychological or economic theories (e.g., construal level theory, prospect theory, social comparison theory, search theory) are identified and used as scaffolding throughout the Theoretical Background.
- Authors extend, qualify, or challenge existing theory rather than merely applying it. Pure replication of an established prediction is insufficient for top outlets.
- Quantitative papers situate findings in economic or marketing theory but emphasize causal identification and empirical contribution alongside theoretical framing.

## 5. Methods

### Behavioral / experimental methods
- Each study is described in its own Methods subsection covering: participants (N, recruitment source, compensation, exclusions), design (factorial notation, e.g., "2 × 2 between-subjects"), stimuli and procedure, and measures.
- Online panel samples (Prolific, Amazon Mechanical Turk, Qualtrics panel) are standard; lab studies and field experiments also appear.
- Pre-registration is increasingly reported; OSF or AsPredicted links appear in footnotes or at the start of the study's Methods section.
- Validated multi-item scales are used for psychological constructs; Cronbach's α or composite reliability is reported.
- Manipulation checks are conducted and reported before outcome analyses.
- Past tense is used throughout ("Participants read a scenario…", "We randomly assigned…").

### Quantitative / econometric methods
- Large secondary datasets (scanner data, administrative records, platform transaction data) are common; dataset provenance and time span are described in a dedicated Data section.
- Regression models are specified with equation numbers and formal notation; difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity, and structural models appear.
- Fixed effects (entity, time) are standard for panel data; the identifying variation is described in the text, not just the table.
- Standard errors are clustered at an appropriate level (market, store, individual); bootstrapped confidence intervals appear for mediation analysis.
- A separate Empirical Strategy or Identification section may appear before Results to explain the causal logic.
- Present tense for model specifications ("The model takes the form…"); past tense for estimation procedures ("We estimated the model using…").

### Mixed and multi-method designs
- Sequential mixed-method designs combine qualitative pilot work (focus groups, netnography, open-ended responses) with confirmatory experimental studies; the qualitative phase is reported briefly to motivate the experimental stimuli or constructs.
- Multimethod integrative papers may combine survey studies, archival analysis, and laboratory experiments within a single paper.

## 6. Results and Findings

### Behavioral results
- Results are reported study-by-study within each study's own Results subsection.
- Manipulation check results are reported first ("The manipulation check confirmed that…").
- Main effects are reported with F-statistics, degrees of freedom, p-values, and effect size (η²p for ANOVA; Cohen's d for t-tests).
- Regression-based analyses report β (or b), SE, t-statistic, and p-value.
- Mediation is tested using Hayes PROCESS (Model 4 or Model 7); bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals for the indirect effect are reported, with the CI range determining significance ("95% CI [LL, UL], excluding zero").
- Moderation results include interaction F-statistics and are probed with simple slopes or cell-mean comparisons; figures illustrating the interaction are common.
- Non-significant results for predicted null effects or boundary conditions are reported as evidence of specificity.
- Authors revisit hypotheses explicitly: "Consistent with H1, we found…"; "Replicating Study 1, we again found…"

### Quantitative results
- Results tables present coefficient estimates with standard errors in parentheses; statistical significance is indicated with asterisks (*** p < 0.01, ** p < 0.05, * p < 0.1).
- Coefficient magnitudes are interpreted substantively, not just statistically; authors translate estimates into economic or behavioral units ("a one-unit increase in X is associated with a Y% increase in sales").
- Event study plots, treatment-effect figures, and marginal-effect plots accompany main regression tables.
- Robustness checks are reported as a matter of course: alternative specifications, placebo tests, falsification checks, subgroup analyses.
- Structural model parameters are reported with standard errors and model fit statistics.

## 7. Discussion and Conclusion

### General Discussion structure (behavioral)
- Opens by briefly summarizing what the full set of studies collectively demonstrated (2–3 sentences).
- Develops theoretical implications: how findings extend, qualify, or challenge the specific literature invoked in the Theoretical Background.
- Managerial or policy implications are discussed; top journals expect practical relevance alongside theoretical contribution, though theory is primary.
- Addresses limitations openly: sample characteristics, ecological validity of experimental stimuli, boundary conditions not yet tested.
- Closes with a future research section offering specific, tractable questions rather than generic calls for more research.

### Discussion / Conclusion structure (quantitative)
- Summarizes the main empirical finding and its magnitude.
- Interprets findings in light of the theoretical or managerial debate introduced in the Introduction.
- Discusses external validity and generalizability explicitly.
- Notes policy implications where the empirical context warrants (pricing policy, platform design, regulatory intervention).
- Identifies data or identification limitations and suggests next steps.

### Conclusion
- Either the final section of Discussion or a separate short section.
- Restates the core finding and contribution concisely.
- Does not introduce new findings or substantially new arguments.
- May close with a sentence about broader significance for theory, practice, or policy.

## 8. Citation and Literature Engagement

### Style
- APA in-text citations: (Author, Year) or Author (Year) when the author is syntactically part of the sentence.
- Multiple citations in one parenthetical are ordered alphabetically: (Ariely, 2008; Thaler & Sunstein, 2008).
- "et al." for three or more authors from first citation in behavioral work; quantitative work sometimes cites all authors on first mention.

### Density
- Introduction and Theoretical Background / Literature Review sections are citation-dense; nearly every empirical claim or theoretical assertion is supported by at least one citation.
- Methods sections cite scale sources and established procedures but are otherwise sparse.
- Results sections contain minimal citations.
- Discussion sections cite selectively to situate findings within existing debates; citation density is moderate.
- Reference lists typically contain 40–80 citations for empirical papers; review papers may exceed 100.

### Literature engagement
- Authors engage critically rather than just approvingly; they distinguish their contribution from closely related prior work.
- Footnotes carry methodological details, alternative analyses, or literature qualifications that would interrupt the main argument.
- Currency is expected: top journals look for engagement with work published in the past 3–7 years alongside foundational references.

## 9. Hedging and Epistemic Stance

### Degree of hedging
- Moderate. Main theoretical claims and hypotheses are stated with confidence. Interpretive inferences beyond what data directly support are hedged.
- Modal verbs ("may," "might," "could") and epistemic markers ("appear to," "seem to," "seem consistent with") mark inferences, not central findings.
- Limitation acknowledgments use explicit hedging ("We acknowledge that…", "Although we cannot rule out…", "Our findings may not generalize to…").

### Key phrases
- "We find that…" / "Our results show that…" (direct report of confirmed finding)
- "We argue that…" / "We propose that…" / "We predict that…" (theoretical assertion)
- "Consistent with [H1 / prior theory], we find…" (aligning result with prediction)
- "Our findings suggest that…" (hedged interpretive inference)
- "Together, these studies demonstrate that…" (synthesis across studies)
- "Future research could examine…" / "An important next step is…" (forward-looking recommendation)
- "We acknowledge that…" / "One limitation of our work is…" (limitation framing)

## 10. Article-Type Variation Summary

| Feature | Behavioral / experimental | Quantitative empirical | Conceptual / review |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-empirical section | Theory & Hypotheses | Literature Review / Background | Thematic conceptual sections |
| Central claim form | Numbered hypotheses (H1…) | Identification strategy + estimands | Numbered propositions (P1…) or taxonomy |
| Empirical organization | Study 1, Study 2, … (each with Methods + Results) | Data → Model → Results (continuous) | None / illustrative examples |
| Evidence vehicle | Experimental F-stats, t-stats, β, η²p, Cohen's d, Hayes PROCESS CIs | Regression tables, event-study plots, significance stars | Logical argument, conceptual diagrams, illustrative cases |
| Effect size reporting | η²p, Cohen's d, bootstrapped CI | Economic magnitude, elasticity, % change | Not applicable |
| Typical number of studies | 3–7 studies | 1 primary analysis + robustness checks | Not applicable |
| Pre-registration | Increasingly common (OSF / AsPredicted) | Rare | Not applicable |
| Typical length | 8,000–14,000 words | 8,000–15,000 words | 8,000–16,000 words |
| First-person usage | "We" throughout | "We" throughout | "We" throughout |

## 11. Journal-Level Notes

- **Journal of Consumer Research (JCR)**: The flagship outlet for behavioral consumer research. Strongly favors multi-study designs (4–7 studies) using experiments, surveys, and sometimes qualitative or archival components. Mechanism and boundary condition testing are expected. Pre-registration increasingly noted. High tolerance for multimethod integrative papers. Statistical reporting follows APA conventions with η²p and Hayes PROCESS mediation.
- **Journal of Marketing (JM)**: Publishes both empirical (quantitative and experimental) and conceptual work with an explicit managerial orientation. Practical implications receive more prominent treatment than in JCR or JMR. Methodological range is broad (field experiments, structural models, machine learning, qualitative). Contributions to marketing practice are weighted alongside theoretical contributions.
- **Journal of Marketing Research (JMR)**: Emphasizes methodological rigor and novel analytical techniques. Experimental designs with many studies (7–9 is possible), field experiments, and econometric studies all appear. More tolerance than JCR for papers with fewer studies if identification is strong. Method papers introducing new measures or estimation approaches are a distinct article type.
- **Marketing Science**: The quantitative/economics-facing outlet. Papers are structured like economics working papers: numbered sections, formal mathematical or econometric model specifications with equation numbers, LaTeX-style notation, heavy use of structural models and natural experiments. Behavioral content appears but in a minority. Significance stars in tables (***/**/*) are conventional. Managerial implications are expected but analytical contribution is primary.
- **Journal of Consumer Psychology (JCP)**: Closely related to JCR in style; multi-study experimental designs, hypothesis-driven, APA statistical reporting. Somewhat shorter than JCR; 3–5 studies is typical. Psychological theory is primary; marketing application is secondary.
- **Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science (JAMS)**: Broad empirical scope covering B2B, services, branding, and strategy alongside consumer behavior. Structural equation modeling (SEM) with fit indices (CFI, RMSEA, SRMR) is especially common. Both quantitative and qualitative methods appear. Managerial implications are prominent. Scale development papers are a recognized article type.
