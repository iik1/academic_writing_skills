# Journal Writing Skill: Journal of Accounting Research

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 4*
- **Field:** ACCOUNT
- **Publisher:** Wiley, on behalf of the Chookaszian Accounting Research Center, University of Chicago Booth
- **Scope:** Original research using analytical, empirical archival, experimental, or field study methods on questions of information and measurement in organizations, markets, governments, regulation, and standards. Publishes five issues per year; the June issue is dedicated to the annual conference.
- **Guidelines URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/page/journal/1475679x/homepage/forauthors.html
- **Field baseline:** account-field-writing-style
- **Articles spot-checked:** 1 (Boulland, Bourveau & Breuer, "Company Websites: A New Measure of Disclosure," JAR 2025, DOI: 10.1111/1475-679X.70007)

## Hard Constraints (Author Guidelines)

> These override FIELD_SKILL where they conflict.

### Structure
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm for main body. End matter order is journal-specific: appendices appear **before** the reference list (not after). Tables and figures are embedded in the body text, not collected at the end.

### Length
No explicit word or page limit stated in available guidelines.

### Abstract
Unstructured single paragraph, 150–250 words, followed by **JEL codes** and **Keywords** as separate labeled fields on the title page. No structured subheadings within the abstract. Label: ENFORCED (word range from guidelines; format confirmed in article).

### Citations
JAR uses its own citation style that departs materially from the field's parenthetical convention.

**In-text:** Author **[Year]** with square brackets — not `(Author Year)`. Examples from article:
- `Beyer et al. [2010]`
- `Dyer, Lang, and Stice-Lawrence [2017]`
- `(e.g., Beyer et al. [2010], Dranove and Jin [2010])`

**Reference list:** `Last, F.I.; Last, F.I.; and Last, F.I. "Title." Journal Volume (Year): pages.` Authors separated by semicolons; year placed after volume number in parentheses, not immediately after author names.

Label: ENFORCED (stated in guidelines + consistently observed in article).

### Headings
Main section headings use Arabic numerals with Title Case (e.g., `1. Introduction`, `2. Construction`, `6. Conclusion`). Subsections use decimal Arabic numbering with small-caps typography (e.g., `2.1 Wayback Machine`, `5.2 Nonfinancial Disclosure`). Appendices titled `Appendix A: Title` in small caps. This differs from the AAA/TAR convention of Roman numerals with ALL-CAPS Level 1 headings. Label: PRACTICE ONLY (confirmed in article; heading format not fully specified in available guidelines text).

### Tables and Figures
Tables are embedded in the body text, not collected at the end. Table titles appear in small caps (e.g., `TABLE 6`), with a subtitle on the next line. Table notes appear immediately below each table.

### Explicit Prohibitions
- AI/generative content tools (e.g., ChatGPT) may not be listed as authors.
- Authors are capped at **4 new submissions per rolling two-year period** (revise-and-resubmit decisions do not count against the cap).
- A **submission fee** is required before a paper enters review: $750 (high-income countries), $500, or $50 depending on authors' country of residence. Non-payment within one week of submission results in withdrawal.

## Observed Deltas from FIELD_SKILL

### Citations
**Field norm:** Author-date in-text as `(Author Year)` or `Author (Year)`.
**JAR:** Square brackets around the year: `Author [Year]` or `(Author [Year])`. The reference list places year after volume number, not after author names. This is JAR's proprietary style — authors must follow it throughout, including in tables and appendices.
**Classification:** ENFORCED

### Structural Deviations
**Field norm:** Reference list follows the main text; appendices may appear before or after references depending on journal.
**JAR:** Appendices precede the reference list. Tables are embedded in text, not collected at manuscript end.
**Classification:** ENFORCED

### Other Journal-Specific Patterns

**Online appendix hosted centrally:** JAR hosts online supplements at a dedicated URL on the Chicago Booth website (`chicagobooth.edu/research/chookaszian/journal-of-accounting-research/online-supplements-and-datasheets`). Articles reference this URL on the title page. Authors should expect to deposit supplementary materials there, not as journal-hosted PDFs. Label: PRACTICE ONLY.

**Data and code sharing:** A formal data and code sharing policy applies. A data availability statement is expected on the title page or in the methods section. Label: ENFORCED.

**Registered Reports article type:** JAR offers a two-stage editorial process for Registered Reports — intended for "research with potentially higher outcome risk that involves gathering new data." Stage 1 is a theoretical/design review before data collection; Stage 2 is a post-collection review. This is not a feature of most accounting journals. Label: ENFORCED.

**Surveys and methodological contributions:** JAR accepts a distinct submission type for literature surveys and methodological papers. Proposals (a few pages only) may be submitted at any time via the Research Exchange platform; a separate proposal review fee ($250) applies. Label: ENFORCED.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Wrong citation brackets.** The field default `(Author Year)` is incorrect here. JAR requires `Author [Year]` and `(e.g., Author [Year])` with square brackets throughout. This is the single most common mechanical error by authors submitting from other accounting journals.

2. **Reference list format.** Authors who follow APA, Chicago author-date, or JAE style will produce a reference list that does not match JAR's format. JAR places the year after the volume number `Journal Vol (Year): pages`, not after the author names.

3. **End matter order.** Placing the reference list before the appendices (as is common at JAR's peer journals) inverts JAR's required order. Appendices come first, then references.

4. **Submission fee and cap.** Overlooking the submission fee requirement causes automatic withdrawal. The four-paper rolling cap may constrain prolific submitters who are revising multiple papers simultaneously.
