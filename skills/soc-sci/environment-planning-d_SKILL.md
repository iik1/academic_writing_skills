# Journal Writing Skill: Environment and Planning D: Society and Space

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 4
- **Field:** SOC SCI
- **Publisher:** SAGE Publications
- **Scope:** Critical, empirically engaged research on the spatial dimensions of power, difference, and socio-political life; welcomes work that furthers critical epistemological approaches including Indigenous, decolonial, feminist, and ethnographic perspectives.
- **Guidelines URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/author-instructions/epd
- **Field baseline:** soc-sci-field-writing-style
- **Articles spot-checked:** 1 (Otchere-Darko & Weszkalnys, 2025, DOI: 10.1177/02637758241312090)

---

## Hard Constraints (Author Guidelines)

> These override FIELD_SKILL where they conflict.

### Structure
No required section sequence is prescribed. Follows FIELD_SKILL norm for qualitative/ethnographic work, with deviations documented under Observed Deltas.

### Length
**7,000–10,000 words** inclusive of abstract, all tables, figures, and references. Figures count as 250–500 words depending on size. [ENFORCED]

### Abstract
Guidelines specify a **structured abstract of 200 words maximum** covering purpose, major findings, and conclusions. However, published articles consistently use **unstructured prose** with no labeled fields — the abstract reads as a single flowing paragraph. [GUIDELINE ONLY — practice diverges]

### Citations
**SAGE Harvard style.** In-text: (Surname, year) or (Surname, year: page) for quotations. Multiple authors connected with "and" (not "&"). Multiple citations separated by semicolons within one set of parentheses. Reference list: surname, initials. Year. Title. *Journal* volume(issue): pages. DOI where available. Book titles and journal names italicised. [ENFORCED]

### Headings
No explicit rules stated in guidelines. In practice, section headings are **bold**, use **thematic or evocative language** rather than standard academic labels, and are not numbered. See Structural Deviations below. [PRACTICE ONLY]

### Tables and Figures
Submit artwork in electronic format following SAGE guidelines. Equations via Office Math ML or MathType. High-resolution figures required.

### Explicit Prohibitions
- Simultaneous submission to another journal is prohibited.
- Plagiarism actively checked; violations result in retraction.
- Manuscripts must be fully anonymised for double-blind review.

### Additional Requirements
- **Author biographies** required: 80–120 words per author. [ENFORCED]
- **Data availability statement** required. [ENFORCED]
- No submission or publication fees (unless opting into SAGE Choice open access).

## Observed Deltas from FIELD_SKILL

> Only dimensions where this journal deviates from the field baseline.

### Voice and Register
**Named authorial reflexivity** is a strong EPD practice. In multi-authored ethnographic work, authors explicitly identify individual researchers by name within the body text to attribute specific fieldwork activities (e.g., "One of us [Author A] attended..."). FIELD_SKILL acknowledges positionality statements in qualitative methods sections but does not anticipate this level of named intra-author differentiation within the narrative. [PRACTICE ONLY]

The journal's stated scope calls for work that is "empirically engaged" and "furthers critical epistemological approaches" to "decenter and explode existing ways of knowing." This signals a stronger tolerance for explicitly political and decolonial register than FIELD_SKILL's baseline "moderately more accessible" description of critical human geography. [ENFORCED in scope statement; PRACTICE ONLY in degree]

### Statistical Reporting
Not applicable. The journal is dominated by qualitative, ethnographic, and theoretical work. No statistical reporting patterns observed.

### Structural Deviations
Three journal-specific structural patterns diverge from FIELD_SKILL:

**1. Opening narrative vignette.** Articles characteristically begin the Introduction with an extended narrative or ethnographic scene (several paragraphs) before any theoretical framing or literature engagement. This scene-setting move is not described in FIELD_SKILL and is a distinctive EPD rhetorical convention. [PRACTICE ONLY]

**2. Methods integrated into Introduction.** There is no separate Methods or Data and Methods section. Methodological detail — fieldwork duration and mode, participant description, researcher role, and ethical positioning — is woven into the Introduction or early body of the paper. FIELD_SKILL acknowledges that qualitative work may have different structure, but EPD takes this further: the methods account is dispersed rather than gathered. [PRACTICE ONLY]

**3. Creative, evocative section headings.** Section headings are thematic and interpretive rather than procedural (e.g., "Uncommon timelines," "Park life," "Assembling a just transition," "From transition experiments to propositional politics"). Standard labels such as "Background," "Findings," "Discussion," or "Data and Methods" are not used. FIELD_SKILL notes thematic subsections in qualitative work but does not flag the consistently evocative register of EPD headings as a field-wide norm. The journal-level expectation is stronger. [PRACTICE ONLY]

**4. No Discussion section.** Articles move directly from the final empirical/thematic section to "Conclusions" without a separate Discussion section. Interpretation, theoretical dialogue, and implication are woven throughout the thematic sections rather than gathered in a dedicated Discussion. [PRACTICE ONLY]

### Other Journal-Specific Patterns
- A **Keywords** section (labelled in bold) follows the abstract, listing 5–7 comma-separated terms.
- **Endnotes** (labelled "Notes") are used for supplementary theoretical or empirical commentary rather than footnotes.
- End matter follows a fixed sequence: Acknowledgements → Declaration of conflicting interests → Funding → Notes → References → Author biographies.
- Primary data quotations (interview excerpts, activist statements, documentary sources) are embedded in the prose without off-set block formatting unless extended; they carry the main evidential weight.

- Voice and person (first-person plural for multi-authored work): FIELD DEFAULT
- Hedging (epistemic rather than statistical; interpretive plurality acknowledged): FIELD DEFAULT
- Citation density by section (high in introduction and theory; low in empirical sections): FIELD DEFAULT
- Paraphrase vs. quotation norms for secondary literature: FIELD DEFAULT
- Contribution framing (embedded in flowing argument, not enumerated): FIELD DEFAULT
- Closing moves (Conclusions restate finding, connect to broader implications, acknowledge limitations): FIELD DEFAULT

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Submitting a structured abstract with labeled fields.** Although the guidelines request a "structured" abstract, published EPD articles use flowing, unstructured prose. A mechanically labeled abstract (Purpose / Methods / Findings / Conclusions) may signal unfamiliarity with the journal's actual conventions.

2. **Using a separate Methods section.** EPD ethnographic and qualitative articles integrate methodological discussion into the introduction or body narrative. A standalone "Data and Methods" section reads as out of register for this outlet.

3. **Standard academic section headings.** Headings like "Literature Review," "Findings," or "Discussion" are out of place. EPD expects conceptually resonant, field-specific titles that carry interpretive weight.

4. **Exceeding the word ceiling.** The 10,000-word maximum is inclusive of all components. EPD articles are substantive but tightly written; papers that rely on extensive framing, repetitive literature review, or lengthy methods descriptions often exceed the limit before the empirical contribution is fully developed.
