## Author Guidelines (Hard Rules)

> These are the journal's stated requirements. They take precedence over observed patterns when there is a conflict.

### Manuscript Structure
No mandatory section template. Structure is flexible by paper type. Empirical papers: Introduction → Data/Model → Estimation/Methods → Results → Conclusion. Theoretical papers: Introduction → Model Setup → Propositions/Theorems → Applications → Conclusion. Headings use the decimal system (1, 1.1, 1.1.1).

### Length Requirements
No explicit overall word count limit stated. Practice: 8,000–16,000 words including appendices for empirical papers; 4,000–6,000 words for short notes/reviews.

### Abstract
- Length: 150–250 words (stated)
- Format: Unstructured single paragraph (no subheadings)
- No undefined abbreviations or unspecified references
- Keywords: 4–6 required

### Citations and References
- Style: Author-year (Harvard) in parentheses
- (Smith 2012) — no comma between name and year; (Li et al. 2024) for 3+ authors; (Author1 2020; Author2 2021) for multiples
- Narrative: "Smith (2012) finds that..."
- Reference list: alphabetical by surname, Springer standard format
- Journal article: Surname Initials (Year) Title. Journal Vol(Issue):pp–pp. DOI
- Book: Surname Initials (Year) Title. Publisher, City

### Headings
- Decimal system: 1 / 1.1 / 1.1.1 — maximum three levels
- No Roman numerals or letter headings

### Tables and Figures
- Tables: use table function (not spreadsheet)
- Equations: use equation editor or MathType
- All tables/figures must be titled and referenced in text
- Three-line table format is standard

### Language and Tone Requirements
- English (British or American, consistent)
- Non-native speakers encouraged to have manuscripts professionally edited
- No stated active/passive voice preference

### Prohibitions
- Undefined abbreviations in abstract
- References in abstract
- More than three heading levels
- Spreadsheet-produced tables
- Missing Data Availability Statement

## Observed Writing Patterns

### Voice and Person
- First-person plural ("we") dominates in multi-author empirical papers: "we propose," "we develop," "we show," "we find," "we apply."
- Single-author papers use "I" or impersonal: "this paper shows," "the paper proposes."
- Passive for background/literature/model structure: "has been demonstrated," "is estimated," "was introduced by."
- Results section mixes active ("we find that...") with passive ("evidence is found").
- Impersonal for contribution statements: "The main contribution of this paper is...," "The goal of this paper is to..."

### Sentence and Paragraph Style
- Sentences average 20–30 words; complex math in displayed numbered equations.
- Paragraphs: 4–8 sentences; introductions shorter/punchier, methods sections denser.
- High jargon density; acronyms (DEA, SFA, TFP, MPI, DMU, CRS, VRS) defined at first use.
- Numbered environments (Assumption, Proposition, Theorem, Corollary) in methodological papers; proofs in appendix if lengthy.

### Hedging and Epistemic Stance
- Methodological contributions stated assertively: "We develop," "This paper proves."
- Empirical findings hedged: "may suggest," "appears to," "the evidence is consistent with," "our results indicate," "it is plausible that."
- Causal language carefully qualified: "a positive contribution of X to Y" not "X causes Y."
- Limitations stated as factual constraints: "the analysis is limited to...," "we cannot rule out..."
- Avoid: "clearly," "obviously," "of course."

### Transitional and Cohesive Phrases
- Roadmap (end of introduction): "The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents... Section 3 describes... Section 4 reports... Section 5 concludes."
- Into results: "We now turn to the empirical results." / "Table X reports the estimation results."
- Summary: "Overall, the results suggest that..." / "Taken together, these findings indicate..."
- Literature link: "This is consistent with [Author Year]." / "In contrast to [Author Year], we find..."
- Internal reference: "As discussed in Section 2," / "Following [Author Year],"

### Formality and Register
- Highly formal; no contractions, no colloquialisms.
- Technical terms used without re-definition after initial setup.
- JEL codes on title page (after abstract and keywords).
- Footnotes used moderately for technical asides and brief qualifications.

### Guideline vs. Practice Discrepancies
- Decimal headings required; in practice, most empirical papers use only two levels (1, 1.1).
- Abstract limit 150–250 words; practice is typically 180–220 words.
- No word count limit stated; empirical papers average 8,000–14,000 words.
- No IMRAD mandate; empirical papers informally follow modified IMRAD; theoretical papers do not.

## Section-by-Section Writing Guide

### Abstract
Single unstructured paragraph, 150–250 words. Cover: (1) research problem in 1–2 sentences; (2) methodological approach in 2–3 sentences; (3) key findings in 2–3 sentences; (4) brief implication in 1 sentence. No subheadings, no citations, no undefined acronyms. JEL codes and keywords follow on separate lines.

### Introduction
- Open: broad area importance (1–2 sentences).
- Gap: specific limitation in prior work (2–3 sentences, 2–4 citations).
- Contribution: assertive statement of what this paper does (1–3 sentences).
- Brief methodological overview (1–2 paragraphs).
- Key findings summary (1–2 paragraphs; most important finding first).
- Roadmap paragraph naming each subsequent section.
- Length: 600–1,200 words.
- Citation density: 1–3 per paragraph in gap/literature portion.
- Tense: present ("productivity has declined...," "this paper shows...").

### Methods
- Section name reflects content: "Model," "Methodology," "Econometric Framework," "Data," or "Data and Methodology."
- Numbered equations (right-aligned, in parentheses).
- Numbered Assumption/Proposition/Theorem environments where relevant.
- Tense: present for model description; past for prior authors ("Kumbhakar (2000) showed...").
- Data subsections: source, coverage, sample selection, descriptive statistics table, variable definitions.
- Cite established methods briefly; do not re-derive standard results from scratch.

### Results
- Section name: "Empirical Results," "Results," "Simulation Results," or "Applications."
- Orient reader before each table: "Table 1 reports estimation results for the baseline model."
- Report statistics factually then interpret with hedged language.
- Discuss statistical and economic significance together.
- All tables/figures referenced inline.
- Robustness checks: brief, with results in appendix or online supplement.

### Discussion
- Often not a standalone section; integrated into Results or Conclusion.
- When standalone: connects findings to prior literature, considers alternatives, brief policy implications (1–2 sentences).

### Conclusion
- Titled "Conclusion" or "Concluding Remarks."
- Opens with summary of what was done: "This paper developed..."
- 3–5 key findings in 1–2 paragraphs.
- Limitations in 2–4 sentences.
- Future directions in 1–2 sentences.
- Length: 300–600 words.
- No new results or extensive new citations.

### References
- Alphabetical; no numbering.
- Springer author-date format.
- Data Availability Statement placed immediately before reference list.

## Common Pitfalls
1. **Forcing a theoretical paper into IMRAD.** Theoretical papers have no Data section or empirical Results. Use section names reflecting actual content: "Model," "Propositions," "Extensions."
2. **Writing a structured abstract with subheadings.** JPA requires a single unstructured paragraph (150–250 words). No Background/Methods/Results labels, no citations, no acronyms.
3. **Non-decimal heading numbering.** Roman numerals, letters, or more than three levels violate the guidelines. Use 1 / 1.1 / 1.1.1 only.
4. **Omitting the Data Availability Statement.** Springer requires this for all original research. Place it before the reference list. Omission can block publication.
5. **Overstating causal claims.** JPA editors and readers are quantitative economists. State "associated with" or "consistent with" unless a clean causal identification strategy (IV, RDD, DiD, randomization) is explicitly established and documented.
6. **Insufficient formalism for methodological contributions.** New estimators or theoretical results require formally stated Propositions/Theorems with proofs (appendix acceptable for length). Informal verbal descriptions alone are insufficient.

## Representative Phrase Bank

### Introduction phrases
- **Gap statement:**
  - "Despite the growing literature on [X], relatively little attention has been paid to [Y]."
  - "A key limitation of existing approaches is that they assume [restriction], which may not hold in practice."
  - "To date, no study has jointly addressed [A] and [B]."
- **Objective statement:**
  - "This paper proposes a new approach for estimating [X] that accounts for [Y]."
  - "The main contribution of this paper is to extend the framework of [Author Year] by incorporating [feature]."
  - "We develop an estimator that simultaneously handles [problem 1] and [problem 2]."
  - "The goal of this paper is to clarify [issue] and provide practical guidelines for practitioners."

### Methods phrases
- **Procedure description:**
  - "We follow [Author Year] and define the production frontier as [...]."
  - "The efficiency score is obtained by solving the following linear program."
  - "Following standard practice in the literature (see, e.g., [Author Year]), we assume that [assumption]."
  - "The model is estimated via maximum likelihood."
- **Data source statement:**
  - "The data are drawn from [source] and cover [N] [units] over the period [T1]–[T2]."
  - "We construct the sample by merging [source A] with [source B], yielding an unbalanced panel of [N] firms."
  - "Descriptive statistics are reported in Table 1."

### Results phrases
- **Statistic inline:**
  - "The estimated coefficient on [variable] is [value], statistically significant at the [level] level (t = [value])."
  - "The average technical efficiency score is [value], indicating that [units] operate at approximately [X]% of their productive potential."
  - "A 1% increase in [X] is associated with a [value]% increase in [Y]."
- **Figure reference:**
  - "Figure [N] plots the estimated efficiency distribution across [units/periods]."
  - "As shown in Table [N], the baseline results remain robust across alternative specifications."

### Discussion phrases
- **Connecting to literature:**
  - "This finding is consistent with [Author Year], who found that [X] in [context]."
  - "In contrast to [Author Year], we find evidence that [Y], which may reflect [explanation]."
  - "Our estimates are broadly in line with the magnitude reported by [Author Year]."
- **Limitation framing:**
  - "A limitation of the present analysis is that [X], which may affect the generalizability of the findings."
  - "We cannot rule out the possibility that [alternative explanation] contributes to the result."
  - "The results should be interpreted with caution given [data or identification limitation]."

### Conclusion phrases
- **Summary opener:**
  - "This paper developed a new framework for [X] and applied it to [empirical setting]."
  - "We have proposed an estimator for [X] and demonstrated its performance through [Monte Carlo simulations / an empirical application to Y]."
- **Future direction:**
  - "Future research could extend the current framework to accommodate [additional feature]."
  - "An important avenue for future work is to investigate [related question], which we leave for future research."
  - "The approach developed here could be fruitfully applied to [other sector/country/context]."
