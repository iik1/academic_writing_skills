# Journal Writing Skill: World Development

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** SOC SCI
- **Publisher:** Elsevier
- **Scope:** Multi-disciplinary monthly journal of development studies addressing poverty, governance, inequality, environmental degradation, and related global challenges across developing and developed countries.
- **Guidelines URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/world-development/publish/guide-for-authors
- **Field baseline:** soc-sci-field-writing-style
- **Articles spot-checked:** 2 (Faguet 2026, DOI: 10.1016/j.worlddev.2026.107338; Daoud et al. 2026, DOI: 10.1016/j.worlddev.2026.107400 / arXiv:2509.25648)

## Hard Constraints (Author Guidelines)

> These override FIELD_SKILL where they conflict.

### Structure
No mandatory section order beyond standard academic structure. Follows FIELD_SKILL norm for sequence. Numbered section headings with decimal subsections (1, 1.1, 2, 3.1…) are the observed Elsevier house style. Observed in practice: first section heading may be substantive and thematic rather than the generic label "Introduction."

### Length
No overall word limit stated in guidelines. FIELD_SKILL norm applies.

### Abstract
Unstructured single paragraph. **Maximum 250 words.** No internal subheadings. [ENFORCED]

### Keywords
3–6 keywords required for indexing. [ENFORCED]

### Citations
At submission, any consistent reference style is acceptable. Published articles use author-date (Harvard) format throughout: (Author, Year) in-text; full reference list at end. No numbered citation system. [PRACTICE ONLY for style; flexibility at submission is GUIDELINE ONLY]

### Headings
No explicit rules stated. Numbered headings with decimal subsections used in published articles (Elsevier convention). [PRACTICE ONLY]

### Tables and Figures
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm.

### Mandatory Declarations (Elsevier)
All submissions must include the following, each as a labelled section after the main text: [ENFORCED]
- **Declaration of competing interest** (or explicit statement of none)
- **Funding sources** (standardised disclosure)
- **Generative AI and AI-assisted technologies statement** (required even if no AI was used; state "No AI was used")
- **Data availability statement**
- **CRediT authorship contribution statement** (each author's role listed by CRediT taxonomy)

### Explicit Prohibitions
- AI tools may not be listed as authors or co-authors
- Concurrent submission to another journal is prohibited
- Authorship changes after acceptance are not permitted
- Inclusive, gender-neutral language is required throughout; bias and stereotyping are prohibited

## Observed Deltas from FIELD_SKILL

> Only dimensions where this journal deviates from the field baseline.

### Development-Studies Framing (Scope Requirement)
**Field norm:** Policy relevance is expected but framing varies by sub-discipline.
**This journal:** Introduction and conclusion must anchor the paper's contribution explicitly to development outcomes — poverty, inequality, governance, public services, or environmental conditions in developing and/or transitional contexts. Papers that do not connect findings to these themes fall outside scope even if methodologically sound. The Faguet (2026) article illustrates this with an explicit subsection ("A development studies contribution to social science") closing the conclusions. [PRACTICE ONLY — confirmed in both articles; derivable from stated scope]

### Mandatory Post-Text Declarations
**Field norm:** Ethics statements and data availability notes are increasingly common but not universally structured.
**This journal:** Five labelled declarations are required in every submission (see Hard Constraints above). These are non-negotiable Elsevier requirements, not field conventions, and their absence will block editorial processing. [ENFORCED]

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Missing development nexus.** Framing contributions purely in disciplinary terms (e.g., advancing political science theory or econometric method) without connecting findings to development outcomes risks desk rejection. The journal expects the "so what for development?" question to be answered explicitly.
2. **Omitting Elsevier declarations.** Missing any of the five required declaration sections (competing interests, funding, generative AI, data availability, CRediT) will delay or block submission processing regardless of scientific merit.
3. **Abstract over 250 words.** The 250-word cap is stricter than many field journals; submissions over this limit must be trimmed before the system accepts them.
4. **Over-specifying citation style at submission.** The journal accepts any consistent reference style at submission stage; there is no need to reformat to Harvard prior to first submission, though the final published version will use author-date format.
