# Journal Writing Skill: Economy and Society

- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** SOC SCI
- **Publisher:** Routledge (Taylor & Francis)
- **Scope:** Critical and interpretive work on economic and social relations, states, governance, and cultural economy. Publishes theoretical, conceptual, and qualitative empirical work in the traditions of critical social theory, STS, and economic sociology.

## Observed Deltas from FIELD_SKILL

### Methodological scope — RELAXATION (ABS 3)

**Field norm:** SOC SCI is methodologically diverse; FIELD_SKILL covers quantitative, qualitative, mixed-methods, and critical theory approaches.

**This journal:** Both spot-checked articles are qualitative/theoretical. The journal's established intellectual identity — critical social theory, STS, post-Marxist and Foucauldian economic sociology — means quantitative causal inference work is out of scope in practice. Writers should not expect the quantitative conventions in FIELD_SKILL (identification strategies, robustness checks, statistical narration) to apply here. Engage with the journal's theoretical traditions.

**Classification:** RELAXATION — ABS 3 outlet with a narrow qualitative/theoretical scope; FIELD_SKILL's quantitative norms are inapplicable rather than relaxed.

### Numbered endnotes — PRACTICE ONLY

**Field norm:** FIELD_SKILL does not specify endnote practice for qualitative work.

**This journal:** One article used numbered endnotes (4 notes) to elaborate theoretical asides and qualifications that were judged too digressive for the body text. Endnotes supplement rather than replace author-date citations. The other article had no notes. This is an available device, not a requirement.

**Classification:** PRACTICE ONLY

### Non-academic source citations — PRACTICE ONLY

**Field norm:** FIELD_SKILL notes that paraphrase of secondary literature is standard; direct citation of grey literature is not addressed.

**This journal:** Reference lists prominently include journalistic articles, organisational reports, blogs, and web-archived URLs alongside academic sources. This reflects the empirical objects studied (philanthropic movements, policy documents, online communities) as much as any editorial preference, but authors should not feel constrained to academic-only citation.

**Classification:** PRACTICE ONLY

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Submitting quantitative work.** The journal publishes no quantitative empirical work based on observed practice. Even if not explicitly prohibited, a regression-based manuscript would be out of scope for this outlet's intellectual community.

2. **Imposing a separate Theory or Literature Review section heading.** The journal's qualitative articles integrate the literature throughout an extended introduction and thematic sections. A labelled "Literature Review" section is not the convention here.

3. **Neglecting the theoretical genealogy.** Economy and Society expects engagement with the intellectual traditions that define its scope — Foucauldian governmentality, political economy, STS, cultural economic sociology. An empirical article that does not situate itself within these conversations is likely to read as insufficiently theorised for this journal.

4. **Under-citing from non-academic sources when relevant.** If the empirical object under study includes policy documents, organisational reports, or online communities, citing these sources directly in the reference list is normal practice here — do not paraphrase them away.
