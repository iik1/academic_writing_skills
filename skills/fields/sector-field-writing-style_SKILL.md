# Field Writing Style: Tourism, Transport and Hospitality

## Scope and Intended Use

This baseline covers shared writing conventions across ABS 4 journals in the Tourism, Transport and Hospitality field. No ABS 4* journals exist in this field at the time of compilation; all evidence derives from ABS 4 sources.

The field spans journals with substantially different methodological cultures — from interpretive tourism ethnography and behavioural experiments to discrete-choice modelling and algorithmic transport optimisation. Where conventions genuinely converge, this document states them as field norms. Where they diverge across methodological cultures within this field, the document marks the variation explicitly rather than forcing a false consensus.

This baseline does NOT cover: journal-specific formatting, word limits, submission requirements, publisher rules, or manuscript templates. Journal-specific skills take precedence when they conflict with this baseline.

## Typical Article Structure

Near-universal across ABS 4 journals in this field, full research articles follow this canonical sequence:

1. Abstract
2. Introduction — establishes the research territory, identifies a gap, previews contributions
3. Literature Review / Theoretical Background / Conceptual Framework — may be labelled differently or, in shorter quantitative papers, folded into the Introduction
4. Method / Methodology / Data and Analysis
5. Results / Findings
6. Discussion
7. Conclusion
8. References

Optional but common: a Highlights section (bulleted summary, standard in Elsevier journals), a standalone Managerial or Practical Implications section, and a standalone Limitations and Future Research section. These are more often integrated into Discussion and Conclusion than presented separately.

Practice varies substantially across methodological cultures within this field:

- Mathematical modelling and operations research papers follow a different canonical structure: Introduction → Problem Formulation / Model → Solution Algorithm → Numerical Experiments or Case Study → Conclusion. A standalone literature review section is often absent or brief.
- Multi-study experimental papers structure content around sequential studies: Introduction → Theoretical Framework → Study 1 (Method + Results) → Study 2 (Method + Results) → General Discussion → Conclusion. The unified results section is replaced by per-study blocks.
- Short communications and research notes — accepted by several leading journals in this field — compress the standard structure into fewer sections at reduced length.

Leading journals in this field also publish commentary, viewpoint, and forum pieces, which follow no fixed structural template but are clearly labelled as non-standard formats.

## Voice, Person, and Register

A consistently high level of formal academic register is expected across all article types at the ABS 4 level in this field. Colloquial phrasing, rhetorical questions (except in essay-style commentaries), and advocacy-style prose are absent from high-quality work in this field.

Practice varies on grammatical person:

- First-person plural ("we") is the dominant convention in quantitative empirical, experimental, and mathematical modelling papers. Researchers signal authorial agency: models are proposed, data are collected, hypotheses are tested, by "we".
- Third-person impersonal constructions ("the study examines", "the analysis reveals", "results indicate") are common in qualitative and mixed-methods papers, though many qualitative papers also use "we".
- Single-author papers may use "I", but this is uncommon; "we" appears even in sole-authored work in some journals in this field.

Practice varies on active versus passive voice:

- Active voice dominates in introduction and conclusion sections across virtually all article types.
- Passive voice is frequently used in methods sections to foreground the procedure rather than the researcher ("participants were recruited", "data were collected", "the model was estimated").
- Mathematical and technical sections alternate between active voice for model propositions ("we define the cost function as") and passive or definitional constructions for derivations.

## Sentence and Paragraph Style

At the ABS 4 level in this field, sentences fall in the medium-to-long range. Theoretical and conceptual sections favour complex subordinate clause structures to convey nuanced argument. Methods and results sections tend toward shorter, procedurally denser prose.

Paragraph density is medium to dense. Each paragraph develops a single idea and typically follows a claim-evidence-interpretation pattern. Paragraphs in technical mathematical sections may be shorter and more formulaic, anchoring notation before advancing an argument.

Practice varies: Leading tourism and hospitality journals in this field have a culture of accessibility and instruct authors to prioritise clarity and avoid unnecessary technical jargon when simpler language suffices. At the opposite end of the field, mathematical transport methodology papers are notation-heavy, with dense technical prose interspersed with formal definitions, lemmas, proofs, and propositions — a convention entirely absent from behavioural or interpretive papers in the same field.

## Argumentation and Rhetorical Moves

Introduction structure follows a recognisable variant of the CARS (Create a Research Space) model across the ABS 4 journals in this field:

1. **Establish territory**: The introduction opens by asserting the importance and activity of the research area, supported by a cluster of foundational and recent citations.
2. **Establish niche**: A gap, limitation, or underexplored dimension is identified — typically framed as "little attention has been paid to", "the literature has not yet addressed", "existing models do not account for", or "findings remain inconsistent".
3. **Occupy niche**: Research questions or objectives are stated explicitly, and the paper's contributions are previewed. At the ABS 4 level, this preview is expected to be explicit and, in most empirical papers, itemised.

At the ABS 4 level, reviewers expect contributions to be stated explicitly in the introduction — not implied or emergent. Contributions are commonly listed as enumerated items or structured "this study contributes by…" statements, positioned against the specific stream or streams of literature being advanced.

Closing moves in the Conclusion section follow a near-universal sequence: restatement of the core finding, theoretical implications, practical or managerial implications, limitations, and future research directions. All five moves appear in the large majority of ABS 4 articles in this field, though their relative length and emphasis differ by article type.

## Methods Conventions

Across ABS 4 journals in this field:

- Methods sections are written predominantly in past tense ("data were collected", "we recruited participants", "the model was estimated using").
- Mathematical definitions and model specifications may use present tense for definitional claims ("let X denote the demand flow", "the objective function is defined as").
- Procedural detail is expected to be sufficient for replication: sample characteristics, data source and collection period, variable operationalisation, estimation strategy, and software used are routinely reported.
- Robustness and sensitivity analysis are increasingly expected in quantitative papers across this field. Alternative model specifications, out-of-sample validation, or alternative identification strategies are reported as a standard component of the methods section or as an appendix.

Practice varies substantially by methodological culture:

- Qualitative papers describe data sources, sampling rationale, saturation criteria, analysis procedure (grounded theory, thematic analysis, content analysis, ethnography), and, where relevant, researcher positionality. Quality criteria (credibility, transferability, confirmability) substitute for statistical reliability and validity.
- Quantitative survey-based papers report sampling frame, survey design, measurement scales, confirmatory factor analysis or item reliability (Cronbach's alpha, AVE, CR), and common-method bias procedures (Harman's test, marker variables, CFA approach).
- Mathematical modelling papers centre on problem formulation with formal proofs, algorithmic design with complexity analysis, and convergence or optimality demonstrations. Social-science-style measurement validation does not apply and is not expected.
- Multi-study experimental papers describe each experiment in sequence (design, participants, stimuli, manipulation, measures, manipulation check), with pilot studies or pretests commonly reported inline.

Ethical approval statements, data availability declarations, and conflict-of-interest disclosures are now expected across leading journals in this field for studies involving human participants.

## Results and Reporting Norms

At the ABS 4 level, reporting (what was found) and interpretation (what it means) are expected to be distinguishable, even when they appear within the same section label. High-quality papers report outcomes in Results and interpret them in Discussion; blending the two is more accepted in qualitative papers, where findings and interpretation are co-constructed, than in quantitative ones.

Practice varies by methodology:

- Quantitative behavioural and survey papers present results through standardised model tables supplemented by prose narration. The convention is to state the direction and significance of effects in the text and report precise coefficients, standard errors, and fit statistics in tables. Selective reporting of only significant results is strongly disfavoured at the ABS 4 level.
- Mathematical modelling papers present results through solution quality benchmarks, computational performance metrics, and sensitivity analyses. Graphical and tabular presentation of numerical outcomes is essential; verbal narration interprets patterns in the data.
- Qualitative papers organise results into themes or conceptual categories. Participant language may be paraphrased or selectively quoted (direct quotation is more permissible in qualitative findings sections in this field than in other contexts), with each theme illustrated by supporting evidence.

Statistical precision norms: At the ABS 4 level, effect sizes, confidence intervals, and model-fit statistics (R², RMSEA, CFI, and equivalents) are expected alongside significance values. Reporting p-values alone without effect sizes is below the current bar.

Null results are reported and interpreted rather than suppressed, particularly in hypothesis-driven experimental and quasi-experimental designs.

## Discussion and Conclusion Norms

At the ABS 4 level in this field:

- Discussion and Conclusion are typically separate sections in full research articles. Merged "Discussion and Conclusion" sections are more common in research notes, short communications, and mathematical papers where interpretation is embedded in the results.
- Discussion sections synthesise findings, interpret them in relation to the theoretical framework developed earlier, and engage explicitly with prior work — both confirming and disconfirming results require engagement.
- Limitations are stated explicitly and specifically. Reviewers expect at least two or three concrete limitations, not generic caveats. Overly brief or dismissive treatment of limitations is a basis for revision requests at this level.
- Future research directions are expected to be specific and derive logically from the paper's limitations or open questions. Vague calls for "further research" without specification are insufficient.
- Both theoretical implications (what the findings contribute to theory or conceptual development) and practical or managerial implications (what decision-makers or practitioners should do differently) are expected in tourism and hospitality papers. The weight on each varies: service and tourism management papers give substantial space to managerial implications, while mathematical transport methodology papers foreground technical extensions and application contexts over practitioner guidance.

## Citation and Literature Engagement

Citation format: Author–year (APA) is used across all journals in this field.

Citation density norms by section:

- Introduction and Literature Review: High. At the ABS 4 level, all major claims must be anchored to prior work. A lightly cited introduction or theoretical section reads as insufficiently engaged with the literature.
- Methods: Moderate. Methodological choices are cited (for instruments, established procedures, or adopted frameworks), but procedural descriptions do not require citation at every step.
- Results: Low. Results sections cite prior work primarily when benchmarking findings against reported statistics from prior studies.
- Discussion: High. Each substantive finding is connected back to supporting or contrasting prior literature. Failure to engage prior work in the discussion is a common basis for reviewer criticism.

Direct quotation of prior scholarly work is rare in these journals. Paraphrase is the strong norm for citing theory and findings. Direct quotation appears selectively in qualitative findings sections to preserve participant voice — it is not used to cite theory or prior research.

When findings differ from prior work, leading journals in this field expect the disagreement to be framed constructively: prior findings are refined, contextualised, or given boundary conditions rather than flatly contradicted. Polemical framing is absent from high-quality work in this field.

## Hedging and Epistemic Caution

Moderate epistemic caution is the overall norm at the ABS 4 level in this field, though the appropriate level depends heavily on the methodological type of the paper.

Practice varies by methodology:

- Qualitative and interpretive papers: Relatively higher hedging. Causal language is avoided or heavily qualified; findings are "tentatively proposed", "suggested by the data", or "indicated by participants". Generalisation claims are narrowly scoped.
- Quantitative survey-based and cross-sectional papers: Moderate hedging, especially around causality. Results from cross-sectional designs acknowledge the limits of causal inference; experimental and longitudinal designs permit greater confidence in stated effects.
- Mathematical modelling papers: Minimal hedging for proven model properties (proven results stated as established facts); moderate hedging when applying the model to empirical scenarios ("the proposed algorithm outperforms benchmarks in the tested instances").

Commonly used hedging registers in this field:

- Gap framing: "receives limited attention", "remains underexplored", "has not been systematically examined", "findings have been inconsistent"
- Findings: "suggests", "indicates", "is associated with", "tends to", "appears to", "generally"
- Limitations and generalisability: "may not generalise beyond", "future research should verify in other contexts"

Overstatement — claiming universal generalisability from a single-country or single-sector sample, or claiming causal effects from a correlational design — is a common basis for rejection at the ABS 4 level in this field.

## What This Baseline Does NOT Specify

This baseline does not cover and should not be used to infer:

- Word limits or page limits for any specific journal in this field
- Required or preferred section heading labels mandated by individual journals
- Publisher formatting requirements: templates, font, spacing, or margin specifications
- Submission system requirements or manuscript preparation checklists
- Journal-specific statistical reporting requirements (e.g., whether to report standardised vs. unstandardised coefficients, or which fit indices to prioritise)
- Qualitative reporting standards specific to particular traditions (grounded theory, phenomenology, discourse analysis, netnography, ethnography)
- Requirements for supplementary materials, preregistration, or open data
- Anything else that represents a journal-level or publisher-level requirement rather than a field-level norm

When a journal-specific skill conflicts with this baseline, the journal-specific skill takes precedence.
