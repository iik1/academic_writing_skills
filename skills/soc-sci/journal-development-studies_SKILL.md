# Journal Writing Skill: Journal of Development Studies

## Journal Profile
- **ABS 2024 Rating:** 3
- **Field:** SOC SCI
- **Publisher:** Taylor & Francis (Routledge)
- **Scope:** Interdisciplinary research on development theory, policy, and practice with clear implications for low and middle income countries; welcomes economics, political science, sociology, geography, and anthropology approaches.
- **Guidelines URL:** https://www.tandfonline.com/action/authorSubmission?journalCode=fjds20&page=instructions (requires institutional access; inaccessible via direct fetch during skill construction)
- **Field baseline:** soc-sci-field-writing-style
- **Articles spot-checked:** 2 (Natal & Nordman, IZA DP 18210 / JDS forthcoming, full text; Bitzer & Gören 2024, JDS 60(10), 10.1080/00220388.2024.2377294, structure via conference paper version)

## Hard Constraints (Author Guidelines)

> These override FIELD_SKILL where they conflict.

### Structure
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm. No prescribed section order found in guidelines. Observed practice: numbered sections (1., 2., 2.1.) throughout; supplementary materials appended for robustness tables and additional diagnostics.

### Length
Regular articles: no confirmed word limit found in accessible sources. Special issues: maximum 9,000 words inclusive. [GUIDELINE ONLY — applies to special issues only; regular limit unconfirmed]

### Abstract
Unstructured single paragraph, approximately 130–150 words based on observed articles. No labeled subfields (no Background/Objectives/Methods tags). Followed immediately by:
- **JEL Classification:** economics codes (e.g., D91, G51, O12)
- **Keywords:** 5–8 terms

[PRACTICE ONLY — confirmed in two articles; JEL codes and keywords placement not verified against live guidelines]

### Citations
APA (author-date) format. Confirmed from the Taylor & Francis citation style index and consistent across observed articles. In-text: (Author, Year) or (Author et al., Year) for 3+ authors. Reference list: APA 7 format with DOIs. [ENFORCED]

### Headings
Numbered sections observed in all articles (1. Introduction, 2. Context and data, 2.1. Context). [PRACTICE ONLY]

### Tables and Figures
Follows FIELD_SKILL norm. Tables carry bold numbered titles ("**Table 1:** ...") above the table; Note and Source lines appear below. Significance stars (*p<0.1, **p<0.05, ***p<0.01) are standard in quantitative work.

### Explicit Prohibitions
Submissions must have clear relevance to low and middle income developing countries. Papers without an LMIC empirical focus or without explicit development implications are rejected at desk. [ENFORCED — stated in scope, confirmed in editorial practice]

## Observed Deltas from FIELD_SKILL

> Only dimensions where this journal deviates from the field baseline.

### Citation Style
FIELD_SKILL: author-date norm described generically.
JDS: specifically APA 7 format with DOIs required. Not Chicago author-date or Harvard. [ENFORCED]

### Abstract Format
FIELD_SKILL: does not specify abstract length or sub-field requirements.
JDS: unstructured ~150-word abstract followed by JEL classification codes and keywords block. JEL codes are an economics-convention not typical in sociology or geography outlets within SOC SCI. [PRACTICE ONLY]

### Structural Deviations
**Context section:** Empirical articles consistently include a dedicated "Context" or "Background" subsection (within the data/methods section or as a standalone section) describing the country setting, policy history, and institutional features relevant to the study. This is more elaborated than the optional "Background" section described in FIELD_SKILL and reflects the journal's LMIC focus — reviewers expect authors to demonstrate country-level knowledge. [PRACTICE ONLY]

**Merged conclusion:** In observed articles, Discussion and Conclusion are merged into a single closing section. No standalone Discussion section observed. [PRACTICE ONLY]

### Other Journal-Specific Patterns
**Scope constraint as editorial filter:** JDS applies a strict LMIC relevance filter at desk review. Even methodologically strong papers are rejected quickly if development implications for low/middle income countries are not explicit. This is stronger than a typical ABS 3 journal's editorial posture. [ENFORCED]

**Disciplinary pluralism with economics dominance:** The journal is formally interdisciplinary but the majority of quantitative empirical work follows development economics conventions: numbered sections, regression tables with clustered standard errors, identification discussion, robustness checks in supplementary materials, JEL codes. Qualitative and mixed-methods work is published but is a minority.

## Common Pitfalls
1. **Missing LMIC framing.** A paper on a general social science topic without explicit grounding in a low or middle income country context will be desk-rejected regardless of methodological quality.
2. **Wrong citation format.** Submitting with Chicago or Harvard references instead of APA. JDS is indexed in the Taylor & Francis APA category; use APA 7 with DOIs.
3. **No JEL codes.** Omitting JEL classification codes from the abstract header is a formatting error for economics-style submissions; include them even if the paper crosses into sociology or political science.
4. **Underselling the country context.** JDS reviewers expect a substantive "Context" section. Treating the empirical setting as a mere data source without situating it in its institutional and historical specifics reads as generic and weakens the development contribution.
